# Norway's oil and gas expansion vs. EU energy transition

[On Thin Ice: Norway's Fossil Ambitions and the EU's Green Energy Future](../sources/on-thin-ice-norway-s-fossil-ambitions-and-the-eu-s-green-energy-future.md)
(2024), co-published with Oil Change International, Zero Carbon Analytics, WWF Norway and
Greenpeace Norway, argues that EU gas and oil demand is in structural long-term decline under
every plausible climate scenario, making new Norwegian oil/gas supply — especially planned Arctic
and Barents Sea exploration (141 licences issued since 2010) — both unnecessary and a stranded-
asset risk.

Under a net-zero-aligned scenario, the report projects EU gas demand falling 60% by 2030 and 97%
by 2050 versus 2022 (32%/93% under a less ambitious "announced pledges" scenario), with the EU
able to end Norwegian gas imports entirely by 2035 — even as LNG import capacity is set to expand
78% by 2030. Oil demand is projected to fall 16-30% by 2030 and 59-92% by 2050 depending on
scenario, with Norway's own oil production seen falling 66% by 2050 versus 2021 even accounting
for new field development.

The report also takes a position on hydrogen: blue hydrogen (produced from gas with carbon
capture) is argued not to be cost-competitive with green hydrogen and incompatible with full
decarbonisation — BloombergNEF is cited projecting green hydrogen cheaper than blue by 2028, and
24-45% cheaper in Germany specifically by 2030. T&E's recommendation is that the EU and Norway
halt new licensing and exploration, and that the EU resist Norwegian pressure to expand Arctic
extraction beyond 2030.
