# IMO shipping fuel standard (Net-Zero Framework)

*Updated: 2025-10-31*

*See also: [Biofuels § Shipping and the IMO Global Fuel Standard](biofuels.md#shipping-and-the-imo-global-fuel-standard)*
for the biofuel-feedstock-safeguard argument this policy fight connects to, and
[E-fuels financing and infrastructure](e-fuels-financing.md) for how T&E wants shipping ETS
revenue used to fund e-fuels specifically.

**Contents:** [The opening warning and the London agreement (February-April 2025)](#the-opening-warning-and-the-london-agreement-february-april-2025) · [A historic but weak first step (April-July 2025)](#a-historic-but-weak-first-step-april-july-2025) · [The October 2025 political fight](#the-october-2025-political-fight)

## The opening warning and the London agreement (February-April 2025)

T&E's campaign on the IMO framework began with the Cerulogy study behind
[IMO: Fuelling deforestation](biofuels.md#shipping-and-the-imo-global-fuel-standard) (February
2025): without excluding high-ILUC feedstocks, the draft rules could make nearly a third of global
shipping fuel biofuels by 2030 (versus under 1% today, 60% of it palm and soy), adding an
estimated 270 MtCO2e/year — worse than doing nothing — a warning that both NGOs and shipping
operators echoed (see [Biofuels](biofuels.md) for the full figures and the industry pushback).
[Why international shipping needs a global carbon levy AND a global fuel standard](../sources/why-international-shipping-needs-a-global-carbon-levy-and-a-global-fuel-standard.md)
(op-ed, April 2025, ahead of the London talks) restated the same 270 MtCO2e figure while arguing
the IMO needs *both* a carbon levy and a fuel standard — neither fossil fuels nor crop-based
biofuels should count as "green," and green hydrogen fuels need dedicated financial support to
compete.

Negotiators then reached the draft agreement itself:
[UN shipping agreement a victory for multilateralism but a failure for the climate](../sources/un-shipping-agreement-a-victory-for-multilateralism-but-a-failure-for-the-climat.md)
(11 April 2025) reports the London deal as the first framework requiring ships to move away from
fossil fuels, but criticises it for not excluding or capping any fuel type — meaning the cheapest
compliance route is first-generation crop-based biofuels (palm, soy, rapeseed) — and for revenue
from its economic incentives running out by 2032 without additional top-ups, undermining support
for scalable zero/near-zero fuels.

## A historic but weak first step (April-July 2025)

In April 2025, the International Maritime Organization adopted its Net-Zero Framework (NZF) — the
first binding global climate rule for an entire industry sector.
[Shipping is flying the flag for multilateral climate action: why aren't we celebrating yet?](../sources/shipping-is-flying-the-flag-for-multilateral-climate-action-why-aren-t-we-celebr.md)
(op-ed, July 2025) sets out T&E's ambivalence: historic in principle, but weak in substance — the
NZF as adopted exempts roughly 90% of shipping emissions from any carbon penalty, and its
penalty-mechanism revenue (~$10bn/year globally) matches what the EU's own ETS already raises from
EU shipping alone. Worse, because the framework ignores indirect land-use change (ILUC), its
cheapest compliance routes — crop-based biofuels and LNG — risk doubling the global biofuel market
(including road transport) by 2035, which T&E estimates could add up to 270 MtCO2e/year of
additional emissions from deforestation by 2035 (roughly France's annual emissions) — see
[Biofuels](biofuels.md). No incentive structure for e-fuels or electricity kicks in until around
2040. T&E's position: support the multilateral step forward, but resist industry attempts to use
the IMO deal as a pretext to dismantle the EU's stricter FuelEU Maritime and ETS rules, which tax
roughly 6x more of EU shipping's carbon than the IMO framework will.

[Towards 2040: a strengthened ETS to support the transition of the shipping sector](../sources/towards-2040-a-strengthened-ets-to-support-the-transition-of-the-shipping-sector.md)
(EU ETS review consultation response, July 2025) makes the case for *why* the EU needs both
systems rather than deferring entirely to the IMO: only around 15% of ETS-priced EU shipping
emissions would also be priced under the NZF, leaving over 85% of EU shipping emissions unpriced
under the global framework alone. T&E's recommendations for the EU ETS itself: extend it to small
ships (400-5,000 gross tonnage — at least 7,500 vessels, 17.8 MtCO2/year, worth an estimated
€2.4bn/year in extra revenue 2028-2035), remove the current exemption for electrifiable
short-hop ferry routes by 2028 (citing routes like Ibiza-Denia and Capri-Naples, both within
existing battery-electric ferry range), add carbon-leakage/evasion safeguards for transhipment,
and align the ETS with FuelEU Maritime's ban on food/feed-crop biofuels — which are currently
"zero-rated" (treated as zero-emission) under the ETS despite the same ILUC risk raised above.

Alongside the IMO track, T&E also pushed a second, national-level channel for regulating shipping
emissions:
[Ocean protection means including shipping in countries' Paris climate targets](../sources/ocean-protection-means-including-shipping-in-countries-paris-climate-targets.md)
(joint press release with Opportunity Green and Seas at Risk, June 2025, at the UN Ocean
Conference) argues international shipping emissions belong in countries' Nationally Determined
Contributions (NDCs) under the Paris Agreement — a "glaring gap" in most national climate plans
today — regardless of what the IMO itself eventually agrees. T&E frames this as complementary
rather than a substitute: NDC inclusion would give countries a legislative mandate to enforce
their own shipping-emissions measures, and provide the investment certainty the NZF alone (see
below) does not yet deliver.

## The October 2025 political fight

By October 2025, the IMO's formal vote on adopting the NZF became a live political fight, and T&E
published three pieces in quick succession defending the framework and the EU's own parallel
shipping law.

[US-led pressure on shipping deal an attack on EU sovereignty, says T&E](../sources/us-led-pressure-on-shipping-deal-an-attack-on-eu-sovereignty-says-t-e.md)
(16 October 2025) accused the US, UAE, Saudi Arabia and other oil-producing states of pressuring
the EU to weaken or abandon its own shipping ETS and FuelEU Maritime green-fuel mandates in
exchange for a weaker global IMO deal. T&E argued the NZF as drafted was already far weaker than
existing EU law and risked enabling mass adoption of deforestation-linked crop biofuels (the same
concern raised in [Biofuels](biofuels.md)); it estimated the IMO rules as proposed would exempt
85% of Europe's shipping emissions from carbon pricing, and that abandoning the EU's own ETS for
shipping would cost Europe at least €10bn/year in lost revenue.

[Green shipping fuel producers call on IMO to seize 'once-in-a-generation' opportunity](../sources/green-shipping-fuel-producers-call-on-imo-to-seize-once-in-a-generation-opportun.md)
(13 October 2025) — a letter from 29 green e-fuel producers (including European Energy, Liquid
Wind, ET Fuels, HIF Global) — argued e-fuels are currently disadvantaged relative to cheaper, less
sustainable options like LNG and first-generation biofuels under the NZF as drafted, and urged the
IMO to adopt the framework with specific e-fuel incentives, framing shipping decarbonisation as a
major offtake opportunity for green-fuel projects worldwide.

[US intimidation leaves future of shipping uncertain but appetite for green future remains](../sources/us-intimidation-leaves-future-of-shipping-uncertain-but-appetite-for-green-futur.md)
(17 October 2025) reported the vote was ultimately postponed by a year following US/Saudi-led
pressure — T&E's framing was that this delay could push the NZF's entry into force out to 2030
even if adopted at the rescheduled October 2026 session, but noted strong underlying support for
decarbonisation remained. T&E's position was that the extra time should be used to strengthen
safeguards (e-fuel incentives, anti-deforestation biofuel safeguards) rather than retreat, and
that the EU's own maritime laws (ETS, FuelEU Maritime) remain the only substantive
shipping-emissions regulation in force regardless of the IMO's eventual outcome.

[Trump's attack on UN shipping deal is a harbinger of what's to come. Here's how countries can still get climate action done](../sources/trump-s-attack-on-un-shipping-deal-is-a-harbinger-of-what-s-to-come-here-s-how-c.md)
(op-ed, 31 October 2025) frames the US-led postponement as a preview of further attempts to derail
multilateral climate action, and argues countries that want to keep progress alive should press
ahead with their own regional measures (e.g. the EU's ETS and FuelEU Maritime, above) rather than
wait for consensus at the IMO.
