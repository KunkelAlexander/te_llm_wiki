# Biofuels

Across 2024-2025, T&E's biofuels work builds a consistent, hardening case: sustainable biofuel
feedstocks are far scarcer than policy targets assume, "advanced" or "waste" labels are
increasingly unreliable, and land-based biofuels can be worse for the climate than the fossil
fuels they replace once indirect land-use change (ILUC) is counted.

## Sustainability limits of advanced and waste biofuels

[How sustainable are advanced and waste biofuels?](../sources/how-sustainable-are-advanced-and-waste-biofuels.md)
(July 2024) argues advanced/waste biofuels — promoted as a cleaner alternative to crop-based
biofuels — face real environmental limits and a scarce sustainable supply, and that much of the
feedstock is already used elsewhere, so diverting it to fuel causes indirect emissions increases.
Advanced/waste biofuels had already reached 40% of all EU biofuels consumed in 2022 (60% of the
transport target's contribution once double-counting incentives are included), while biofuels
overall covered only ~7% of EU transport energy demand. T&E recommended capping the advanced
biofuels target at a maximum of 3.5%, warning that including aviation and maritime in scope was
expected to triple Part A (advanced) feedstock demand by 2030 versus 2022.

## Fraud and mislabeling

*Main page: [POME](pome.md).* T&E's clearest fraud finding concerns Palm Oil Mill Effluent
(POME), a "waste" feedstock whose reported EU/UK biofuel volumes have repeatedly exceeded
plausible global production — see the POME page for the detailed figures and T&E's recommended
fixes. This is presented as an illustration of a broader risk: as scarce genuine waste/residue
feedstocks get squeezed by rising demand, mislabeling and fraud become more attractive and harder
to police, even under tightened certification rules.

## Shipping and the IMO Global Fuel Standard

[IMO: Fuelling deforestation](../sources/imo-fuelling-deforestation.md) (February 2025) applies
the same high-ILUC-risk logic to the shipping sector: without excluding high-ILUC feedstocks
(palm, soy) from the IMO's proposed Global Fuel Standard, the policy could increase net global
emissions rather than cut them, since existing waste-oil feedstocks (UCO, animal fats, vegetable
oils) cannot scale to meet IMO decarbonisation demand — T&E argues novel fuels (e-fuels, advanced
cellulosic biofuels) are needed instead of a feedstock free-for-all.

## Global outlook to 2030

[CrOP30: Why burning food for land-hungry biofuels is fuelling the climate crisis](../sources/crop30-why-burning-food-for-land-hungry-biofuels-is-fuelling-the-climate-crisis.md)
(October 2025), commissioned from Cerulogy, generalises the argument globally for the first time:
global biofuels use has grown seven-fold in two decades to 4% of transport energy demand in 2023
(over 75% from the US, Brazil and Europe), and demand is set to rise a further 40% by 2030 as IMO
shipping rules and SAF mandates add further pull. Despite the promotion of advanced/waste
biofuels, over 90% of biofuels will still come from land-hungry food and feed crops by 2030,
requiring cropland the size of France. Counting full supply-chain and ILUC impacts, biofuels
already emit **16% more CO2 on average than the fossil fuels they replace**, and are projected to
emit 70 MtCO2e more per year than displaced fossil fuels by 2030 (equivalent to ~30 million diesel
cars) — while T&E estimates returning that cropland to natural ecosystems could absorb over 400
MtCO2e/year, nearly double the direct emissions the biofuels are credited with avoiding.

Read together, these reports show T&E's biofuels argument moving from "the sustainable supply is
smaller than policy assumes" (2024) to "the current volumes already look fraudulent" (early 2025,
see [POME](pome.md)) to "the entire global growth trajectory is a net climate negative" (late
2025).
