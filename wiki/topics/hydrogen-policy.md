# Hydrogen policy: blue vs. green

*See also: [Norway's oil and gas expansion vs. EU energy transition](norway-oil-and-gas.md)*, which
covers the same blue-vs-green cost comparison from the gas-supply side.

[Blue threat: will the EU's hydrogen policy stay green?](../sources/blue-threat-will-the-eu-s-hydrogen-policy-stay-green.md)
(September 2025) is T&E's assessment of the EU's Low-Carbon Fuels Delegated Act (adopted July
2025), which sets the rules for "blue" hydrogen — produced from fossil gas with carbon capture
and storage (CCS) — as a lower-carbon alternative to today's near-entirely fossil hydrogen supply
(96% of EU hydrogen is currently fossil-based, and the REPowerEU target of 20 Mt of renewable
hydrogen by 2030 is going unmet).

T&E's central concern is that the Act's accounting still understates blue hydrogen's real
climate impact in two ways: it uses outdated methane-leakage defaults (a default natural gas
leakage rate of ~1.2%, versus a 2.8-3.2% global average and 5%+ in supply chains like US, Algeria
or Nigeria LNG), and it uses a 100-year global warming potential for methane rather than the
20-year GWP that better reflects methane's short-term climate impact. Using the Act's own
compliance threshold (70% GHG savings versus a fossil comparator of 94 gCO2e/MJ, i.e. a ceiling of
28.2 gCO2e/MJ), T&E's analysis implies the required CCS capture rate to qualify ranges from about
67% for hydrogen made from Norwegian pipeline gas up to "130%" (i.e. technically impossible) for
hydrogen made from US LNG — since standard steam methane reforming (SMR) alone emits about 71
gCO2e/MJ of hydrogen, and commercially available CCS caps out at around 60-70% capture. T&E also
flagged a "biofuels loophole": fossil gas SMR/ATR plants can substitute biofuels for process heat
to qualify as low-carbon, which both greenwashes fossil hydrogen production and diverts scarce
advanced-biofuel feedstock (see [Biofuels](biofuels.md)) away from other uses.

A second concern is regulatory asymmetry: low-carbon (non-RFNBO) electrolytic hydrogen faces
looser rules than green (RFNBO) hydrogen — notably no additionality or hourly-matching
requirement — which T&E argues risks an uneven playing field tilted toward the fossil-adjacent
pathway. T&E's recommendation is to keep strong lifecycle GHG-accounting safeguards, avoid any
backsliding on RFNBO rules, and concentrate public support on genuinely first-of-a-kind green
hydrogen projects (e.g. via the European Hydrogen Clearing House) rather than blue hydrogen at
scale.
