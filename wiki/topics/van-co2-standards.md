# Van CO2 standards and electrification

[How regulation is failing to electrify Europe's van market](../sources/how-regulation-is-failing-to-electrify-europe-s-van-market.md)
(2021) is T&E's main analysis of the van segment, which it treats as a smaller but instructive
parallel to the truck CO2-standards story (see
[Zero-emission trucking and EU HDV CO2 standards](zero-emission-trucking.md)): weak regulation
plus generous compliance flexibilities let manufacturers under-deliver on electrification even
where the economics already favour it.

Vans account for 13% of road transport CO2, and van CO2 emissions had risen 58% since 1990 — far
outpacing the roughly 20% rise for cars and trucks — while van sales grew 57% between 2012 and
2019. T&E found the official 147 gCO2/km target was effectively weakened to 159 gCO2/km once
compliance flexibilities were applied, and that only 2% of van sales were electric in 2020,
compared with roughly 11% for cars. T&E argued the electric van business case (total cost of
ownership) was already favourable at the time, yet manufacturers supplied only the regulatory
minimum — the same "flexibilities blunt real ambition" pattern later documented in the truck
segment.

T&E's recommended trajectory: -20% by 2025, moving the -31% target forward from 2030 to 2027,
-60% by 2030, -86% by 2033, and an ICE phase-out by 2035 — paired with a dedicated zero-emission
vehicle (ZEV) sales target rather than CO2 targets alone. BloombergNEF market projections cited
in the report suggested 15-17% electric-van market share was achievable by 2025 and 46-73% by
2030, with price parity arriving as early as 2026.
