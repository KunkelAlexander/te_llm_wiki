# Fossil fuel windfall taxation

*Updated: 2025-10-30*

[Excessive fossil fuel profits should be taxed and given back to citizens](../sources/excessive-fossil-fuel-profits-should-be-taxed-and-given-back-to-citizens.md)
(PwC study commissioned by T&E, March 2025) argues that fossil fuel majors reaped windfall profits
from the 2022 energy crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and that these should be taxed
and the proceeds redistributed to citizens. The underlying PwC analysis quantifies net
profits/margins across oil, gas and coal value chains in the EU-27 for 2021-2023, split by
upstream/midstream/downstream segment and by country.

Headline figures: €180bn in taxable profits EU-wide over the two years following the invasion.
Within that, the oil distribution/marketing/end-use segment accounted for roughly €64bn (47% of
the three-year oil total), while gas distribution/marketing/end-use accounted for roughly €86bn
(73% of the three-year gas total) — i.e. downstream/retail margins, not just upstream extraction,
drove much of the windfall. Germany and France together accounted for €21.3bn (~70%) of EU gas net
profits in 2023; the Netherlands recorded a record €3.9bn net profit from gas exploration and
production in 2022 alone. Downstream oil segment profits rose from €19.4bn to €22.4bn between 2021
and 2022 (+15%, ~5% margin), with wide company-level variation — e.g. TotalEnergies' Belgian
operations reached a ~7% margin in 2022 versus under 1% for Edison in Italy.

This is currently a single-report topic in this wiki; expect it to connect to future T&E work on
energy pricing and just-transition funding as more publications come in.
