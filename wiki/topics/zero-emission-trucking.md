# Zero-emission trucks

*Updated: 2025-11-17*

T&E's consistent position since 2021 is that **direct electrification (battery-electric, with
overhead-catenary electric road systems as a complementary option) is the dominant realistic
pathway to decarbonise heavy trucking**, that hydrogen and e-fuels are far less efficient
detours, and that EU CO2 standards for trucks have been too weak to drive the transition on
their own. Sections below follow T&E's own three-way "Trucks" subtopic split.

**Contents:** [Truck CO2 standards](#truck-co2-standards) · [False solutions](#false-solutions) · [Enabling conditions](#enabling-conditions)

## Truck CO2 standards

[EU truck targets too weak to incentivise transition to zero-emission vehicles](../sources/eu-truck-targets-too-weak-to-incentivise-transition-to-zero-emission-vehicles.md)
(2021) showed truckmakers were already on track to overachieve the 2025 CO2 target (-15% vs a
52.7 gCO2/tkm baseline) without increasing ZEV production, partly via flexibilities like the ZLEV
bonus and banking/borrowing — Scania alone could overachieve by 6.9 percentage points. T&E called
for the 2022 standards review to raise ambition substantially and add intermediate 2027/2028
targets.

By 2023, [Ready or not: Who are the frontrunners in the global race to clean up trucks?](../sources/ready-or-not-who-are-the-frontrunners-in-the-global-race-to-clean-up-trucks.md)
shows T&E's ask had hardened considerably: recommending **-65% for 2030 and -100% for 2035**,
against the European Commission's proposed -45%/-65%/-90% for 2030/2035/2040. That report also
warned that Europe's ZE-truck "readiness" varied sharply by manufacturer (Scania, Mercedes-Benz
Trucks and MAN ranked ahead of DAF, which had no 2030 ZET target), that China held 90% of global
zero-emission truck registrations in 2022 versus 7% for EU+EFTA+UK, and that the Commission's
-45% 2030 target implied only 28% ZET sales by 2030 — weaker than OEMs' own voluntary plans of
47%. The recurring throughline: T&E has repeatedly found that EU truck CO2 targets, as proposed,
undershoot both the available technology and industry's own trajectory.

By late 2025, the standards review had become a live industry-pushback story:
[Truckmakers lobby European Commission to weaken their emissions targets and cut zero emission truck sales](../sources/truckmakers-lobby-european-commission-to-weaken-their-emissions-targets-and-cut-.md)
(30 October 2025) reports that Scania, MAN, Volvo Trucks, Daimler, IVECO and Ford wrote to EU
Commissioners asking to delay Europe's transition to zero-emission trucks — the same manufacturers
whose own overachievement of past CO2 targets T&E had documented above.

## False solutions

[LNG trucks: a dead-end bridge](../sources/lng-trucks-a-dead-end-bridge.md) (2021) is T&E's
rebuttal to gas-industry claims that LNG trucks meaningfully cut emissions. Independent on-road
testing (commissioned from Graz University of Technology) found only a 7.5% GHG reduction versus
diesel on a 100-year GWP basis — far below the ~15% claimed by IVECO — and 13.4% *higher*
emissions on a 20-year GWP basis, plus 37x more ultrafine particles than regulated limits. T&E
also modelled that even with subsidies of up to 6x the fossil gas retail price, biomethane could
only cover 4-28% of expected 2050 road freight energy demand across the six largest EU countries
— used to argue that LNG/biomethane investment is a stranded-asset risk that displaces
electrification rather than complementing it.

## Enabling conditions

The German long-haul decarbonisation study
([Decarbonising long-haul trucking in Germany](../sources/die-dekarbonisierung-des-lkw-fernverkehrs-in-deutschland.md),
2021) compared battery-electric (BEV), overhead-catenary BEV, hydrogen fuel-cell (FCEV), and
e-fuel ICE trucks. Direct electrification needs the equivalent of only 46% of Germany's 2020 net
renewable electricity generation by 2050 to fully electrify long-haul trucking, versus 75% for a
hydrogen pathway and 100-106% for e-fuel ICE routes — a large efficiency gap that recurs
throughout T&E's freight work. On cost, the same report found OC-BEV and BEV trucks reaching
total-cost-of-ownership (TCO) parity with diesel by the mid-2020s, versus roughly 2030 for FCEV.

[Unlocking electric trucking in the EU](../sources/unlocking-electric-trucking-in-the-eu-recharging-along-highways.md)
(2021) sized the near-term charging infrastructure need: trips over 400km are only 5% of trips
but 40% of tonne-km activity and ~20% of truck emissions, and are heavily concentrated on the
TEN-T core network (80% of EU road freight activity, 88% of long-haul activity). T&E estimated
the EU needed 4,400 public high-power chargers plus 6,600 destination chargers by 2030 for an
EU27 rollout (~€1.9bn cumulative investment, only ~0.2% of EU transport infrastructure spend), a
figure it later scaled up to 236,000-617,000 battery-electric trucks in a combined-segment
scenario, avoiding up to 59 MtCO2 (32%) of 2030 truck emissions. Hydrogen FCEVs were framed as a
secondary, niche technology for ports and industrial clusters later in the decade.

[Buying electric: How shippers can drive the transition to zero-emission trucking](../sources/buying-electric.md)
(2025) shifts the argument from regulating truckmakers to targeting the companies whose freight
fills the trucks. It proposes zero-emission procurement targets for large shippers (cargo owners)
under the EU's Decarbonise Corporate Fleets initiative, reasoning that hauliers themselves operate
on 1-4% margins and can't absorb the roughly 2x purchase-price premium of electric trucks, whereas
large companies — just 0.2% of EU companies by count, but over 50% of turnover and roughly 40% of
EU truck activity — could. Modelling three ambition scenarios (targeting large manufacturers,
wholesalers and retailers, who alone account for 26% of truck activity), T&E recommends a
"moderate" scenario reaching 20% zero-emission procurement by 2030, 60% by 2035 and 100% by 2039.
Even with green premiums of up to 30% passed through the supply chain, the report estimates the
maximum end-consumer price impact at just 1.1% by 2039 (transport being roughly 10% of a typical
product's price) — used to argue the shipper-procurement lever is nearly free at the checkout while
unlocking demand that TCO alone hasn't yet delivered (TNO's 2022 finding that electric trucks
already undercut diesel on TCO by 2035 notwithstanding).

[Electric heavy-duty trucks: how to turn the trial into a success](../sources/poids-lourds-lectriques-comment-transformer-l-essai.md)
(French briefing, October 2025) extends the same demand-side lever to the national level, setting
out proposals to support demand for zero-emission trucks in France specifically. And
[Long-distance electric trucks: the revolution is underway in road transport](../sources/camions-lectriques-longue-distance-la-r-volution-est-en-marche-dans-le-transport.md)
(French briefing, November 2025) argues the enabling conditions have already arrived in practice:
it presents concrete cases of hauliers already running electric trucks on long-distance routes as
day-to-day operational reality, not a pilot.
