# Mobility

*Updated: 2025-03-31*

[People do not simply want to get from A to B](../sources/people-do-not-simply-want-to-get-from-a-to-b.md)
(op-ed by T&E President Arie Bleijenberg, March 2025) argues the biggest misconception about
mobility is that people want to travel a fixed distance: in practice, people choose their origin
and destination based on whatever transport options are available to them (cycling to the local
supermarket, taking the tram downtown, driving to an out-of-town shopping centre), and have done so
throughout history at a roughly constant "travel time budget" of around 1.1 hours a day. Faster
transport modes — carriage to train to car to plane — have simply let that constant time budget
cover ever-greater distances: the Paris-Lyon high-speed line boosted commuting between the two
cities, and aviation alone makes the Canary Islands a viable tourist destination. The corollary:
since average car speeds have been flat at around 45 km/h since the turn of the century, per-capita
car mobility has stopped growing in wealthy countries too — Dutch car-kilometres per inhabitant
actually fell 3% between 2005 and 2019, with the 6% rise in car-kilometres driven entirely by lower
average occupancy, not more distance travelled per person. Only air travel, still getting
comparatively faster, keeps expanding personal mobility. Bleijenberg's conclusion, developed further
in his book *New mobility — beyond the car era*: most government transport forecasts don't account
for this travel-time/speed relationship and so overstate future per-capita car-travel growth.
