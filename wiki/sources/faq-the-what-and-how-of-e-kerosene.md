# FAQ: the what and how of e-kerosene

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-02-05*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/faq-what-and-how-e-kerosene) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/FAQ-e-kerosene.pdf)

> E-kerosene, sometimes known as efuels and synthetic kerosene, have the potential to substantially reduce the climate impact of aviation, one of the most carbon intensive sectors of the economy.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- European aviation CO2 emissions grew 26% between 2013 and 2018, reaching 3.7% of European emissions; non-CO2 effects roughly double that climate impact.
- A Ricardo Energy & Environment study (commissioned by T&E) estimates e-kerosene's levelised cost of energy at €137–233/MWh (€/L) in 2030 depending on DAC cost — 3–4x the average 2019 fossil kerosene price (€557/tonne).
- DENA projects domestic EU DAC-kerosene costs falling from €125/MWh in 2030 to €70/MWh in 2050.
- A 2% e-kerosene blending target in 2030 is estimated to add only about €1 per passenger per 1,000km of flying (roughly €2/kg fuel cost differential, applied to ~1kg of a 2000km flight's 48kg fuel burn).
- Without demand management, aviation would need around a quarter of all EU renewable electricity generated in 2050 (1,304 TWh) to produce the e-kerosene needed for full decarbonisation; halving business travel and holding leisure travel at 2019 levels could cut that to 658 TWh.
- T&E's recommended EU-wide e-kerosene blending mandate: 0.04% in 2025, rising to 2% in 2030, covering all EU intra, in and outbound flights.
- Point-source (non-DAC) e-kerosene production costs are cited elsewhere in this FAQ as materially cheaper but environmentally inferior to DAC-sourced carbon.

- This November 2022 T&E FAQ briefing explains why e-kerosene (a subcategory of e-fuels made from green hydrogen and direct-air-captured CO2) is central to aviation decarbonisation, published as the EU negotiated its ReFuelEU Aviation legislation.
- Core argument: liquid fuel demand will persist through 2050 because zero-emission aircraft (hydrogen/electric) won't be viable at scale until the late 2030s/2040s and can't easily serve long-haul routes, so decarbonising the fuel itself — via e-kerosene, which is "drop-in" compatible with existing aircraft/infrastructure — is essential alongside demand management and other measures.
- T&E argues e-kerosene is preferable to other Sustainable Aviation Fuels (biofuels) because it draws on a more scalable renewable-electricity feedstock rather than land-constrained biomass, and because some RED II Annex IX "advanced" biofuel feedstocks (e.g. tall oil, animal fats, PFAD) have real sustainability/displacement problems.
- T&E insists on two conditions for e-kerosene to be genuinely zero-carbon: additional (not diverted) renewable electricity for hydrogen production, and CO2 sourced via Direct Air Capture rather than industrial point sources.
- Non-CO2 co-benefits highlighted: e-kerosene (paraffinic, aromatics-free) reduces contrail formation and local air pollution (particulates, SOx) around airports, and offers minor operational/maintenance benefits from higher energy density and lower soot buildup.
- T&E recommends funding e-kerosene supply via Contracts for Difference financed by ending free EU ETS allowances for aviation, alongside an industrial strategy and continued openness to hydrogen/electric aircraft for short/medium-haul routes.
