# Make, buy, protect Europe

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2024-12-20*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/make-buy-protect-europe)

> CATL's huge investments present the EU with an early test for its industrial ambitions

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Chinese battery maker CATL received full-control European investments backed by taxpayer money: €7.3bn CATL project in Hungary (with €800m state aid) and a €4.1bn CATL battery plant in Zaragoza, Spain (with ~€300m state aid, pre-approved by the Commission under the Temporary Crisis and Transition Framework).
- Gotion (China) received a €2.5bn Slovakia investment backed by €214m state aid; Hungary was separately considering ~€240m in subsidies for a BYD car plant.
- Proposed "Make Europe" support level: up to €25/kWh for EU-produced-and-controlled battery cells, or €2/kg for green hydrogen, as an output-based alternative to the current guideline "acronym soup" (TCTF, R&D&I, CEEAG, IPCEI).
- On trade: EU tariffs are ~30% on Chinese EVs but only ~1% on batteries — an imbalance the authors argue lets Europe "merely assemble cars with Chinese battery packs."

- Op-ed by T&E's Executive Director William Todts and Jos Dings (ex-T&E, ex-Tesla) proposing a three-pillar EU industrial strategy for cleantech: "Make Europe" (simplified, output-based, EU-content-conditional state aid replacing today's fragmented guidelines), "Buy Europe" (public procurement and EU-content rules for EV/heat-pump subsidies, generalising France's EV eco-bonus model), and "Protect Europe" (trade tariffs as a last-resort lever, since they can't fix upstream/downstream supply chains alone and don't improve EU firms' export competitiveness).
- Argues the EU has undersold its own market power (450 million consumers, a single market) by focusing on minimising internal distortions while China and the US maximise external ones, and that recent foreign-controlled cleantech investments (CATL, Gotion, LG, Samsung) have delivered little supply-chain, IP or skills spillover to Europe despite heavy public subsidy.
- Frames Teresa Ribera (the new Commission's Climate and State Aid lead) as the person who should overhaul state-aid rules along these lines.
