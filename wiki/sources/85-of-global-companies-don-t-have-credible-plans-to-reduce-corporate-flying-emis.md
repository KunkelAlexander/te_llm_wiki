# 85% of global companies don't have credible plans to reduce corporate flying emissions

*Press release · Brussels · 2023-03-15*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/85-of-global-companies-dont-have-credible-plans-to-reduce-corporate-flying-emissions) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2023-Travel-Smart-Briefing_Final.pdf)

> World's largest companies fail to set targets on most climate intensive form of business travel - air travel

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- 85% (272 of 322) of companies ranked have no credible business-flying reduction target; only 4 companies (Novo Nordisk, Swiss Re, Fidelity International, ABN Amro) meet the "gold standard" of reporting air travel emissions AND committing to cut them 50%+ by 2025 or sooner.
- If the 10 biggest-emitting companies without a target set 50% reduction goals, this alone would achieve roughly half of the -50%-by-2025 global corporate flying target; the 10 biggest flyers without targets (Volkswagen, KPMG, Johnson & Johnson, Accenture, Siemens, IBM, Microsoft, Alphabet, Merck & Co., SAP) together emitted 3.5 MtCO2 (20% of ranked companies' emissions) from air travel in 2019.
- Only 40 companies (12%) report non-CO2 aviation effects, despite these representing two-thirds of aviation's climate impact; corporate air travel emissions fell 64% (2020) and 70% (2021) versus 2019 and have not rebounded as fast as commercial aviation overall — but current company targets only imply a 9% cut by 2025 and 11% by 2030 versus the -50% needed.
- Business travel is estimated to account for 15-20% of global air travel emissions (~154 MtCO2 in 2019) and around 27% of EU27+UK aviation emissions.

- This March 2023 T&E press release presents the 2023 "Travel Smart Ranking," benchmarking 322 major global companies (up from 229 in 2022) on corporate flying-emissions commitment, reduction and reporting, finding widespread reporting but very little actual target-setting.
- Grading methodology: companies score from -1 to 14 points across ten indicators (commitment type, target ambition/timeline, reporting specificity/history, non-CO2 reporting, emitter-size penalty), sorted into categories A-D; only 11 of 322 companies (3%) achieve the top "A" grade.
- Notes sector and country disparities: financial, consulting and pharmaceutical companies rank best; manufacturing, retail and construction rank worst; Germany has zero A- or B-graded companies, while the UK/France/US perform comparably (UK slightly ahead, partly due to a heavier finance-sector presence and existing mandatory GHG reporting law).
- Recommends governments make air-travel CO2 and non-CO2 emissions reporting mandatory, public and independently verified (citing the EU's incoming Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, covering 50,000 EU companies, as a partial step), and require climate transition plans to include specific business-travel reduction targets.
