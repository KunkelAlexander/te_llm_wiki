# Europe-Asia Green Corridors

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-05-24*

> Case for Morocco as a green shipping H2 hub

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Of 2,348 container-ship end-to-end trips between Europe and East Asia (China, Japan, South Korea) in 2019, two-thirds crossed the Strait of Gibraltar between Morocco and Spain.
- Bunkering green e-fuels in Morocco (instead of only in Western/Northern Europe and East Asia) would let container ships complete 26% more journeys running on ammonia, 22% more on LNG, or 8% more on e-methanol, without needing to bunker anywhere else.
- Green e-fuels are projected to make up close to 80% of the energy consumption of ships sailing to and from the EU by 2050.
- Existing large container ships (14,500–19,999 TEU) average ~36,500 nautical miles of range on HFO/VLSFO; the same ships running on alternative fuels would have far shorter range — from ~6,500 nm (liquid H2 fuel cell) to ~15,800 nm (methanol).
- Morocco's Green Hydrogen Plan targets 0.67 million tonnes/year of green H2 by 2030 (0.22 Mt/year earmarked for EU export), but currently announced projects would only reach 0.05 Mt/year — a large gap between ambition and progress.
- Morocco's renewable technical potential is estimated at 2,792 GW solar and 223 GW wind; about 40% of Morocco's current electricity mix is already renewable, versus ~15% for Egypt.
- Notable individual projects: OCP's $7bn ammonia plant (200,000 t/year by 2026, rising to 3 million t/year by 2050); TotalEnergies' planned $10.69bn (~€9.4bn) green hydrogen/ammonia project from 2027; a €300m Germany-Morocco hydrogen investment pledge.
- A companion Ricardo study commissioned by T&E, covering 6 non-EU exporter countries (Chile, Namibia, Norway, Egypt, Morocco, Oman), found a potential 2.61 Mt/year of hydrogen exportable to the EU — well short of the EU's REPowerEU 10 Mt/year import ambition.
- About 80–95% of Morocco's water resources go to agriculture; the report flags scarce freshwater and desalination-brine disposal as risks for scaling green hydrogen production there.

- T&E's shipping fuel-corridors report (with modelling partner Imal) argues Morocco's location on the Asia–Europe shipping corridor and strong renewables potential make it a plausible green-hydrogen bunkering hub for ships needing extra refuelling stops as they switch to lower-energy-density e-fuels.
- Frames this as economically promising for Morocco but flags risks of "green colonialism" — competition with local populations for land, water and grid-decarbonisation resources — and recommends applying EU-style RFNBO additionality/sustainability rules (and Free, Prior and Informed Consent standards) to any IMO-driven green fuel demand, not just EU-bound exports.
