# How the EU can get its Net Zero Industrial Act right

*Briefing · Brussels · 2023-05-04*

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The European Commission estimates €88 billion in investment is needed by 2030 to meet Net Zero Industry Act (NZIA) objectives (€68bn of which for battery cells alone), of which €16-18bn in public funding is not yet covered by existing EU budget instruments.
- Close to 50 lithium-ion battery factories are planned for Europe by 2030, but T&E finds 68% of potential European battery production capacity (1.2 of 1.8 TWh) is at risk of being delayed, scaled down, or not realised without further action — with Tesla (Berlin), Northvolt (northern Germany) and Italvolt (near Turin) most exposed.
- T&E recommends earmarking 25% of national ETS revenues from shipping and aviation to fund green hydrogen/e-fuel production, worth an estimated €14.5bn (shipping) and €10.25bn (aviation) between 2024-2030 — rising to €18bn for green hydrogen/e-fuels alone if the ETS is extended to all departing flights from 2027 (total revenue €72bn by 2030).
- In the US, Climate Power reported 100,000 clean-energy jobs created within six months of the Inflation Reduction Act's launch; a Boston Consulting Group study cited finds the EU's BEV transition would create nearly as many new jobs (battery supply chain, power sector) as are lost in traditional manufacturing.

- This May 2023 T&E briefing assesses the EU's proposed Net Zero Industry Act (NZIA), the regulatory/skills pillar of the Green Deal Industrial Plan (GDIP) designed to counter US IRA and Chinese industrial-policy competition, arguing it currently lacks ambitious targets and sufficient dedicated funding to be effective.
- Recommends narrowing the NZIA's technology scope to genuinely clean technologies (battery cells/critical metals, wind/geothermal/heat pumps, green hydrogen/e-fuel plants for aviation and shipping, smart grid/EV charging tech) while explicitly excluding fossil-fuel-adjacent technologies (biogas, non-renewable hydrogen, biofuels).
- Proposes binding domestic production targets (maintaining 40% domestic capacity by 2030, at least 80% of Li-ion battery cell demand met domestically from 2027) and a set of "Net Zero Strategic Project" sustainability conditions (lower carbon/material footprint than industry average, science-based transition plans, minimum R&D/reskilling spend, limits on stock buybacks/dividends for beneficiary companies).
- Recommends near-term funding fixes (using Recovery and Resilience Facility resources, expanding the EU Innovation Fund's auction system beyond hydrogen to batteries/wind) and, longer-term, a new European Sovereignty Fund to match the scale of the US IRA.
- Flags the NZIA's proposed public-procurement "diversification" rule (penalising suppliers with a >65% EU market share) as too narrow in scope, recommending it instead apply to global (not just EU) market share to better reflect concentrated international supply chains — see [Ready or not: Who are the frontrunners in the global race to clean up trucks?](ready-or-not-who-are-the-frontrunners-in-the-global-race-to-clean-up-trucks.md) for T&E's related analysis of how IRA-driven investment shifts threaten specific EU manufacturing segments.
