## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Around 13% of European refining capacity closed over the last decade, leaving roughly 13.6 million barrels/day of capacity today; Argus (cited in the underlying study) forecasts a 31% decline in road fuel demand between 2021 and 2035, and models that a sharper 56% demand drop could force 43% of remaining refinery capacity to close or convert.
- European biofuel production is forecast to more than double between 2020 and 2025, reaching about 2.6% of fossil-based road fuel production; some 30 European refineries plan green hydrogen capacity, while nearly 30 new greenfield sites for advanced biofuels/e-fuels have been identified, together capable of producing up to 9.3 million tonnes/year of low-carbon liquid fuels by 2030.
- Of that ~9.3 Mt/year potential capacity, green-hydrogen/synthetic-fuel projects account for up to 3.4 million tonnes/year, versus far larger investment flowing into advanced biofuel conversions — the source of the "8 times more" headline comparison.

- This January 2023 T&E France press release, based on a Ricardo Energy & Environment study (with Argus Media data) commissioned by T&E, finds European oil refiners are investing far more heavily in converting existing capacity to biofuels than in green hydrogen/synthetic e-fuels, despite e-fuels being the more scalable long-term decarbonisation pathway.
- The underlying report examines what role conventional refineries can play in producing hydrogen, e-fuels and advanced biofuels as the EU's 2035 zero-CO2-car target and other "Fit for 55" measures reshape fuel demand, finding refineries have multiple entry points (dedicated plants, co-processing, or full conversion) with no clear preference among them.
- Frames the imbalance as a repeat of a familiar pattern: an easier, cheaper near-term option (biofuel conversion) crowding out investment in the fuel type (green hydrogen/e-fuels) T&E considers the only genuinely scalable route to full decarbonisation for hard-to-abate transport modes.
