# How EU states can tackle unsustainable biofuels and promote cleaner alternatives?

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-09-02*

> RED III implementation for Europe's member states

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- RED III raises the EU's overall renewables-in-transport (RES-T) target from 14% to 29% by 2030 (or a 14.5% carbon-intensity-reduction alternative), but only 22% (or 11% carbon intensity) is strictly binding — Member States can deduct up to 7 percentage points (3.5% in carbon-intensity terms) by capping or phasing out food/feed-crop biofuels.
- The food/feed-crop biofuels cap remains frozen at each Member State's 2020 consumption level (max 7%, with 1% flexibility); average EU food/feed biofuel share would drop from 4.5% to 3.1% if 2020-level palm and soy volumes were excluded.
- RED III's new denominator expansion (adding aviation and shipping to the transport-energy base) plus a combined 5.5% advanced-biofuels/RFNBO sub-target (minimum 1% RFNBOs, so up to 4.5% advanced biofuels with double counting) is projected to push advanced-biofuel demand higher, while the unchanged nominal 1.7% Part B (waste, e.g. UCO) cap becomes a materially larger real volume once aviation/shipping are counted in the base.
- Spain's Ministerial Decree cut its food/feed-crop biofuel cap from 4% (2020) to 2.6% by 2025; Belgium cut food/feed-crop biodiesel from 7% to a planned 2.5% by 2030 and bioethanol from 7% to 4.5% by 2030.
- The WTO ruled in March 2024 in the EU's favour on its palm-oil high-ILUC-risk classification (Malaysia's complaint); Indonesia suspended its parallel complaint the same day — removing a cited obstacle to also phasing out soy biofuels.
- A Commission Delegated Directive (May 2024) added 5 new Annex IX Part A and 4 new Part B feedstocks (mostly intermediate/degraded-land crops), for the first time splitting aviation-bound (Part A) from other-transport (Part B) feedstock accounting.
- A legal opinion commissioned by Deutsche Umwelthilfe, NABU, Rainforest Foundation Norway and T&E (law firm Geulen & Klinger) found RED III does not bar Member States from excluding/restricting individual Annex IX feedstocks nationally — citing precedents: Germany counts POME only once (vs. the EU multiplier), and France caps Crude Tall Oil at 0.1% and restricts other feedstocks unilaterally.

- T&E's recommendations for Member States transposing RED III by May 2025: reduce or zero out the food/feed-crop cap (and cut the RES-T target accordingly); immediately phase out palm and soy biofuels; limit intermediate crops from counting toward targets; keep the advanced-biofuels sub-target at 3.5% (not the new 4.5%) while raising RFNBOs to 2%; exclude category-3 animal fats, PFAD, molasses/soapstock derivatives and imported UCO/category-1&2 animal fats from targets; require more fuel-supplier-level transparency; and prioritise direct electrification (road) and hydrogen-based fuels (aviation/shipping) over further biofuel expansion.
- Companion piece to T&E's broader [How sustainable are advanced and waste biofuels?](how-sustainable-are-advanced-and-waste-biofuels.md) report and the [UCO fraud study](uco-unknown-cooking-oil-high-hopes-on-limited-and-suspicious-materials.md), applying those findings specifically to national RED III transposition choices.
