# How sustainable are advanced and waste biofuels?

*Report · Brussels · 2024-07-01*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-sustainable-are-advanced-and-waste-biofuels) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202407_TE_advanced_biofuels_report-2.pdf)

> Advanced and waste biofuels are increasingly promoted as sustainable alternatives to fossil fuels and damaging crop-based biofuels, so-called "advanced and waste" biofuels are foreseen as a key pillar of European fuel mandates.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Advanced and waste feedstocks (RED Annex IX) grew from 7% of all compliant EU biofuels in 2011 to almost 40% in 2022 — a thirteenfold volume increase — while food/feed-crop biofuel volumes have declined since 2019 to their lowest level since 2013.
- Thanks to double-counting, Annex IX biofuels made up almost 60% of biofuels' overall contribution to the EU's renewables-in-transport (RES-T) target in 2022, even though total compliant biofuels were only 6.6% of transport energy.
- Used cooking oil accounts for almost half of all Annex IX (advanced/waste) biofuels; industrial waste is the second largest category (~20%), followed by animal fats (categories 1/2) and Palm Oil Mill Effluent (POME).
- Italy, Spain and Germany together consumed more than half of all EU Annex IX biofuels in 2022; Sweden was the largest relative user, with advanced/waste biofuels covering ~13% of its transport energy.
- RED III's new combined 5.5%-by-2030 sub-target for advanced biofuels (Part A) and renewable hydrogen derivatives (RFNBOs), plus the expansion of the target's denominator to cover aviation and shipping, is projected to roughly triple demand for Part A feedstocks by 2030 versus 2022 levels, and effectively raise the existing 1.7% Part B cap by more than a third once aviation/shipping are counted.
- Palm Fatty Acid Distillates (PFAD) — not explicitly listed in Annex IX but chemically similar to conventional palm oil — have emissions estimated up to 230 gCO2e/MJ in the worst case (more than double fossil diesel); PFAD biofuel consumption rose ~30% since 2020, reaching almost a third of all palm-derived biofuels by 2022, concentrated in Sweden and Finland.
- POME direct emissions are estimated at ~27 gCO2eq/MJ (a 71% reduction vs. fossil fuel), but EU rules require less traceability for it than for crude palm oil since it's classified as a residue, despite its high fraud risk per the European Commission's own feedstock assessment.
- Forestry residues supplied only ~140 ktoe of EU biofuels in 2022 (under 5% of Annex IX Part A volumes, two-thirds in Sweden); EU forests already harvest on average three-quarters of net annual growth, and the EU land carbon sink has been declining since 2010 partly due to bioenergy-driven overlogging.
- Municipal solid waste's biodegradable fraction available for biofuels is projected (ICCT) to fall from ~78 Mt (2020) to 67 Mt (2030) and 37 Mt (2050) in the EU as recycling/reuse increase; industrial biowaste was the single largest Annex IX Part A contributor in 2022, at over 1,300 ktoe.

- T&E's position, feedstock by feedstock: only domestically collected UCO and category 1/2 animal fats, and separately-collected biowaste, are considered genuinely sustainable; POME/PFAD, category-3 animal fats, primary and secondary forestry residues, intermediate/energy crops and imported "waste" feedstocks should be restricted, excluded from targets, or prioritised for domestic non-fuel uses instead.
- Recommends the EU cap advanced biofuels (with double counting) at a maximum 3.5%, keep the Part B cap at 1.7% or lower, remove or restrict problematic Annex IX feedstocks, create a dedicated anti-fraud unit and overhaul biofuel certification, and prioritise direct electrification (roads) and hydrogen/e-fuels (aviation/shipping) over further biofuel scale-up.
