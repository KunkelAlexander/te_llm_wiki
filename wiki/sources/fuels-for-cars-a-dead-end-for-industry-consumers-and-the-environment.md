# Fuels for cars: a dead end for industry, consumers and the environment

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-11-27*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/fuels-for-cars-a-dead-end-for-industry-consumers-and-the-environment) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2024_11_Fuels_cars_dead_end.pdf)

> A T&E note outlines why allowing fuels – synthetic or bio – in cars makes no environmental, economic, or industrial sense.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Running an e-fuel car over 5 years would cost a driver about €10,000 more than a battery electric car (including in the second-hand market); it would also cost carmakers ~€10,000 per car in synthetic-petrol compliance costs to offset an efficient petrol car's 2030 emissions.
- Projected German e-petrol retail price in 2030: at least €2.8/litre, meaning a full tank would cost ~€210 versus ~€140 today (a 50% increase); T&E's broader e-fuel pricing range for 2040 spans €1.9–5.2/litre (central estimate €3.6/litre) — roughly double current pump prices.
- Supplying just 10% of new cars with e-fuels would require 26% more renewable electricity across Europe than fully electrifying the same share; direct battery-electrification is described as almost 4x more energy-efficient than hydrogen/e-fuel-powered cars.
- Under a 70% GHG-reduction threshold (RED's carbon-neutrality bar), e-petrol cars would still emit ~61 gCO2e/km in 2035 — about 5x an EV's ~13 gCO2/km (EU average grid); at an 80% threshold, e-petrol cars would emit ~41 gCO2e/km, still ~3x an EV's emissions.
- E-fuel-powered cars emit similar NOx to fossil-fuelled cars and generate roughly 3x more carbon monoxide and billions of particles per km — providing no urban air-quality benefit.
- Crop-based biofuels deliver only 50–57% average GHG reduction (RED Annex V); accounting for indirect land-use change (ILUC), average EU biodiesel is ~20% worse than fossil fuel and bioethanol provides only ~30% savings. Sustainable advanced/waste biofuels are projected to cover only ~8% of the EU's aviation energy demand by 2050 — underscoring their scarcity for competing sectors.
- GlobalData forecasts diesel cars falling to just 1–2% of EU car sales by 2030 (from ~13% today), with petrol at 35% — meaning UCO/animal-fat biodiesel (the only biofuels clearing an 80% GHG-reduction bar) are largely irrelevant to future combustion-car fuel use since they can't be blended into petrol engines.

- T&E argues against any car CO2-standards exemption for e-fuels or biofuels, on cost (both to consumers and to carmakers' competitiveness), efficiency (renewable electricity is far better used for direct EV charging), and climate-integrity grounds (only 100% CO2-neutral e-fuels — additional renewable hydrogen plus Direct Air Capture carbon — should ever qualify, with strict hardware/testing safeguards against fossil-fuel loopholes).
- Also opposes a proposed "Carbon Correction Factor" (first floated by the oil/gas industry during 2023 truck CO2 negotiations), which would credit already-mandated renewable fuels in the general fuel mix to loosen carmakers' fleet CO2 targets — arguing this would double-count RED-mandated fuels, reduce the incentive to sell EVs, and weaken European carmakers' global competitiveness versus EV-focused rivals.
