# Why we need an e-fuel mandate for ships

*Briefing · Brussels · 2023-01-23*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/why-we-need-an-e-fuel-mandate-for-ships) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202211_dashboard_green_h2_fueleumaritime.pdf)

> How to speed up the adoption of green e-fuels?

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The European Parliament's proposed 2% RFNBO (e-fuel) sub-quota for shipping by 2030 would create demand for ~21.5 PJ of green hydrogen-based fuels, needing about 205,000-237,500 tonnes of green hydrogen and ~2.2 GW of electrolyser capacity, costing an estimated €0.8-1.4 billion to produce as e-ammonia or e-methanol.
- That 2% sub-quota is modest next to RePowerEU's overall 2030 target of 40 million tonnes of renewable hydrogen (half EU-produced) — shipping's need is under 0.6% of that target; T&E's own recommended 6% sub-quota would require about 0.8 Mt of green hydrogen instead.
- Maersk's announced €10 billion e-methanol investment in Spain (ramping to 1 million tonnes by 2028, 2 million by 2030) could alone supply enough e-fuel to meet 3% of 2030 EU shipping demand — more than the Parliament's entire proposed 2% sub-quota for the whole EU fleet.
- Fully e-fuel-powered shipping would add less than 8 cents to the price of a pair of sneakers shipped from China to Europe, per an earlier T&E cost study (see [The small price to pay to clean up shipping](the-small-price-to-pay-to-clean-up-shipping.md)).

- This January 2023 T&E Q&A briefing makes the case for the European Parliament's proposed mandatory RFNBO (renewable e-fuel) sub-quota in the FuelEU Maritime Regulation, ahead of early-2023 trilogue negotiations, arguing the Commission's technology-neutral GHG-intensity-only approach would leave e-fuel uptake "at best marginal" this decade since it doesn't penalize cheaper fossil LNG or biofuels enough.
- Argues the sub-quota is technology-neutral in practice (covering e-ammonia, e-methanol, e-methane, e-diesel and direct green hydrogen use across combustion engines, fuel cells or turbines) and recommends removing a proposed exemption for shipping companies owning three ships or fewer (which would exempt over half of shipping companies, ~15% of EU shipping energy use).
- Notes byproduct e-diesel from e-kerosene production (mandated separately under ReFuelEU Aviation) could supply a meaningful share of shipping's RFNBO needs: roughly 0.6% of shipping's 2030 energy demand under the Commission's 0.7% aviation e-kerosene target, or nearly all of the 2% shipping sub-quota if the Parliament's more ambitious 2% aviation e-kerosene target is adopted instead.
- Rebuts claims that an RFNBO mandate would increase NOx emissions, arguing e-ammonia/e-methanol ships will meet existing IMO NOx Tier III standards via selective catalytic reduction, and that e-diesel/biodiesel alternatives carry similar NOx profiles to each other regardless.
- Recommends aligning RED III and the Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Regulation with FuelEU Maritime's sub-quota (matching supply-side fuel-supplier obligations to shipping's demand-side mandate) to resolve the chicken-and-egg investment problem, and notes 50+ energy suppliers, ports, shipping companies and NGOs jointly called for a binding sub-quota in June 2022.
