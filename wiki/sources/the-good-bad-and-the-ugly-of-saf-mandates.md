# The good, bad and the ugly of SAF mandates

*News · Brussels · 2022-01-31*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/the-good-bad-and-the-ugly-of-saf-mandates)

> Air France-KLM has introduced a 1% sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) mandate on all flights departing from France. With Air France's decision to add a surcharge fee for passengers for SAF, SAF mandates have come under the spotlight. So how should mandates work to actually be useful?

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Since 10 January 2022, all KLM flights from Amsterdam Schiphol use a minimum 0.5% SAF blend; Air France flights departing France use 1% SAF, in response to France's national 1% SAF mandate.
- A ticket surcharge of €1–€12 applies depending on flight distance and cabin class.
- The EU's proposed ReFuelEU blending obligation starts at 2% in 2025 and rises to 5% by 2030 (and further beyond).
- T&E's review of SAF mandates across seven European countries rated France "orange" — a step in the right direction (excluding crop-based biofuels) but flawed (sugar molasses, not classed as an advanced biofuel, can count toward the target).

- This January 2022 T&E news piece uses France's newly-live SAF mandate (and Air France-KLM's response to it) as a case study for what makes a "good," "bad," or "ugly" SAF mandate.
- T&E's criteria for a well-designed mandate: exclude all crop-based (first-generation) biofuels; limit eligible advanced biofuels to genuine wastes/residues meeting strict sustainability criteria (given limited availability and potential negative environmental impacts even among "advanced" feedstocks); and include dedicated sub-targets for e-kerosene, the fuel type T&E considers the only one able to substantially cut aviation's climate impact.
- Conclusion: France's mandate is a credible first step and a model other EU governments should look to as ReFuelEU negotiations proceed, but its molasses loophole shows mandates are "hard to get right."
