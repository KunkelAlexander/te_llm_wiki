# How to drive the uptake of sustainable fuels in European shipping

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- EU-related shipping must cut ~90 MtCO2/year this decade (down from ~140 MtCO2/year today) to deliver its fair share of the EU's -55%-by-2030 target; T&E estimates up to one-third of that can come from energy efficiency alone.
- The Commission's FuelEU Maritime proposal sets fuel GHG-intensity targets of just -2% (2025-2029), -6% (2030-2034) and -13% (2035-2039) against a 2020 baseline — but business-as-usual LNG uptake plus a shore-power (OPS) mandate would already deliver about half of each target (0.9% by 2025, 2.9% by 2030) without any genuine fuel switch.
- Under T&E's modelling, fossil LNG could reach 23% of total EU shipping energy by 2030 under the Commission's proposed targets, since several LNG engine types would remain "compliant" until the 2030s or, via banked surplus credits, into the 2040s.
- The proposal's scope (EU intra-voyages plus only 50% of extra-EU voyages) covers just 72% of the 146 MtCO2 emitted by EU-related shipping in the reference year — a 42 MtCO2 (28%) coverage gap — and exempts ships under 5,000 GT, excluding a further 19.7 MtCO2 (17.2 Mt of it from vessels 400–5,000 GT).
- Domestic used-cooking-oil (UCO) supply (1.3 Mt in 2019) would fall well short of the biodiesel volumes the proposal would otherwise push shipping operators toward, given LNG's price advantage over more sustainable e-fuels.
- T&E's headline recommendations: a minimum 6% e-fuels/RFNBO sub-target/mandate, a 5x reward multiplier for e-fuels plus exclusive compliance-pooling benefits to close the cost gap with LNG, and advancing the GHG-intensity targets by 5 years versus the Commission's timeline.

- This February 2022 T&E report (Delphine Gozillon) analyses the European Commission's FuelEU Maritime proposal — the EU's first binding law requiring ships to progressively switch fuels — and warns its technology-neutral, goal-based design would lock in fossil LNG for decades rather than drive genuine decarbonisation.
- Central problem identified: because LNG is cheap and its measured GHG intensity (under the regulation's accounting) is only modestly below the 2020 fleet average, LNG-fuelled ships can meet the proposed targets without any transition to green e-fuels or sustainable biofuels, crowding out the nascent e-hydrogen/e-ammonia pathways T&E's own shipping decarbonisation roadmap identifies as the only scalable route to 2050 climate neutrality.
- The report also flags that methane slip from LNG engines is not counted under the EU ETS's MRV reporting (CO2 only), giving LNG vessels an effective ~20% carbon-price discount versus their true well-to-wake climate impact.
- Additional recommendations: extend scope to smaller ships and full (100%) EU-related voyages, raise ambition of the zero-emission-berth mandate, and strengthen enforcement/sustainability safeguards on eligible fuel origin.
