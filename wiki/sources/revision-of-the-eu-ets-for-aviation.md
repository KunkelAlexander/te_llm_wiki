# Revision of the EU ETS for aviation

*Briefing · Brussels · 2020-09-04*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/revision-eu-ets-aviation) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/T_2024-04-29-112034_pejz.pdf)

> T&E provided feedback to the European Commission's Inception Impact Assessment for the revision of the Emission Trading System (EU ETS) rules for aviation.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- European aviation CO2 emissions rose 1.5% in 2019 even as all other EU ETS sectors (stationary installations) fell 8.9%; since 2013, aviation emissions had risen 27.6% while other ETS sectors fell 19.7%.
- European governments had approved at least €32 billion in aviation bailout support during COVID-19, mostly without binding environmental conditions.
- In the UK, 70% of all flights are taken by 15% of the population; in Germany, only 4% of the population takes five or more holiday trips per year — cited to show carbon pricing would mainly affect wealthier, frequent flyers.
- In 2019, ETS allowances traded at ~€25/tonne on average; airlines received about 32.3 million tonnes of free allowances (~half their obligation), worth roughly €900 million — a cost the paper contrasts with the ~€27 billion/year benefit airlines get from the jet fuel tax exemption (if applied to all outbound EU flights).
- The EU ETS covers only intra-EU flights, roughly 40% of the region's aviation emissions; around 50% of aircraft operators' surrendered allowances already come from the stationary ETS market (EUAs) rather than aviation-specific allowances (EUAAs).
- From 2021 the standard Linear Reduction Factor (LRF) would cut the aviation cap 27% below its historic value by 2030; T&E proposes raising the LRF from 2.2% to 3.8% for aviation.
- Proposed multiplier/"discounting factor" rationale: a factor of 5 to compensate for the minimum kerosene tax exemption (33 cents/litre in the Energy Tax Directive equals a CO2 price of €130/tonne); a factor of 3 to compensate for aviation's non-CO2 climate impact, estimated at 3–5x the CO2-only effect.
- ICAO's own CORSIA offsetting scheme was expected to cost airlines only 0.2–0.6% of international aviation revenue in 2025, and CORSIA credit oversupply was expected to run at 2–4x demand under IATA's COVID-19 scenarios.

- This August 2020 T&E paper responds to the European Commission's Inception Impact Assessment for revising the EU ETS aviation rules, arguing the ETS — not ICAO's CORSIA — must remain the EU's primary tool for pricing aviation emissions.
- Core "make it bigger and better" recommendations: abolish free allowances (using the revenue for clean-fuel CfDs), apply discounting/multiplier factors to account for aviation's non-CO2 climate effects and undertaxed kerosene, reduce the aviation emissions cap and/or limit purchases of stationary-sector allowances (to create a real hard cap), set a minimum allowance price, and allow voluntary allowance cancellation to reflect reduced demand (e.g. from modal shift to rail).
- T&E rejects the carbon-leakage rationale for free allocation to aviation, arguing (unlike industrial goods) passenger transport services cannot be relocated to a third country to avoid the scheme.
- On CORSIA integration, T&E evaluates the Commission's six IIA options and opposes any option ("options 3, 5, 6") that would let CORSIA replace or weaken ETS coverage of long-haul/extra-EU emissions, preferring instead to reintegrate extra-EU flights into the ETS ("Stop the Clock" reversal) until CORSIA's mandatory phase (not before 2027) actually delivers reductions.
- T&E flags that ICAO's decision to change CORSIA's baseline year to 2019 (rather than the 2019/2020 average) further weakens the scheme by delaying any offsetting obligation for airlines by several years.
- See [The costs of EU ETS and Corsia for European aviation](the-costs-of-eu-ets-and-corsia-for-european-aviation.md) for the companion cost-modelling study, and [Making the aviation ETS fit for purpose](making-the-aviation-ets-fit-for-purpose.md) for the follow-up Öko-Institut analysis reiterating and quantifying these same reform options.
