# Is it possible to fly to New York without emitting CO2?

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2022-04-28*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/is-it-possible-to-fly-to-new-york-without-emitting-co2)

> T&E president Arie Bleijenberg looks into whether a passenger today can to fly to New York without emitting CO2. Is e-kerosene the answer?

<!-- ENRICHED SUMMARY: hand-written, preserved across regenerations by 1_build_wiki_subset.py -->

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Roughly 400 litres of fuel is burned per passenger on an Amsterdam–New York round trip; green synthetic e-kerosene costs around €3/litre versus €0.50/litre for fossil kerosene, making a fully green ticket about €1,000 more expensive.
- Europe's only operating e-kerosene plant sells at €20/litre (small-scale, experimental, essentially sold out); a second plant starting in 2024 is priced at €2.50–4/litre and already fully sold; three more plants are planned for 2025–2027.
- The combined capacity of all five planned e-kerosene factories would supply only about 1% of the fuel burned by European aviation.

- This April 2022 op-ed by T&E president Arie Bleijenberg uses a personal anecdote (a friend wanting to fly emissions-free to New York) to illustrate both the promise and the current scarcity of e-kerosene.
- Key practical obstacle highlighted: aviation fuel can currently only be purchased by airlines, not travel agencies or individual passengers, and airlines themselves prefer cheaper biokerosene over e-kerosene — while lobbying to weaken EU policies that would mandate the shift to synthetic fuels.
- Argues bio-based aviation fuel volumes are structurally insufficient to fuel the sector sustainably, so scaling e-kerosene production (from wind/solar power plus atmospheric direct-air-capture CO2) must not be delayed in favour of biofuels.
- On hydrogen: notes it is a feedstock for e-kerosene but can be produced cleanly or dirtily and has competing uses outside aviation, so the author argues governments should mandate rising e-kerosene blending shares from oil companies/airlines rather than simply subsidising hydrogen production.
- Concludes that government-mandated blending quotas — making air travellers, not taxpayers, fund the fuel transition — are the mechanism needed to create a functioning market and let any traveller fly on green synthetic kerosene.
