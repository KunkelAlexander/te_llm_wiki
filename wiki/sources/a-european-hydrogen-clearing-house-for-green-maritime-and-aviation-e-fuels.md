# A European Hydrogen Clearing House for green maritime and aviation e-fuels

*Briefing · Brussels · 2025-02-13*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/a-european-hydrogen-clearing-house-for-green-maritime-and-aviation-e-fuels) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Hydrogen-Clearing-House.pdf)

*(No abstract was published for this document; summary below written from the source text.)*

A T&E policy paper (December 2024) proposing a European Hydrogen Clearing House to consolidate and de-risk the fragmented, nascent market for green hydrogen e-fuels used in aviation and shipping, arguing that existing EU measures (the ETS, FuelEU Maritime, and Innovation Fund/Hydrogen Bank auctions) are not enough on their own to make e-fuels cost-competitive with fossil LNG and biofuels.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The EU Hydrogen Bank's pilot auction awarded 7 European projects 10 years of funding at subsidies of €0.37-0.48/GJ — an amount T&E says would be insufficient to close the price gap between e-fuels and fossil LNG/biofuels if applied to shipping.
- Recommends a 25% share of aviation and shipping ETS revenue fund the proposed Clearing House, which T&E calculates could achieve 7% e-fuels uptake in these sectors by 2030.
- Notes more than half of the EU's Recovery & Resilience Facility funds remain unspent and could be redirected to support the initiative.
- Cites shipping giant Maersk's pivot from e-methanol to fossil LNG newbuild ships as evidence current market/policy dynamics disadvantage e-fuels.

- Proposes a European Hydrogen Clearing House (EHCH), building on the H2Global Foundation's HINTCO model, as an upgrade to the EU Hydrogen Bank: it would run double-sided auctions matching e-fuel producers with shipping/aviation offtakers, offering supply-side revenue-guarantee contracts while organising a marketplace for buyers and sellers. Initial delivery would focus on major bunker ports (Rotterdam, Antwerp, Algeciras, Piraeus, Marseille).
- Identifies three core barriers the EHCH would address: high production costs, the mismatch between producers needing long-term purchase commitments and airlines'/shippers' short-term spot-contract norms, and fluctuating bunkered/fuelled volumes that undermine demand certainty (especially for non-scheduled "tramp trade" shipping).
- Recommends complementing the EHCH with a centralised European Hydrogen Platform providing location-specific supply/demand/infrastructure data, linked to the InvestEU Advisory Hub, and initially prioritising "Made in Europe" requirements for electrolysers and key components before potentially supporting international projects.
- Stresses the EHCH would complement, not replace, regulatory mandates (FuelEU Maritime, ReFuelEU Aviation, RED) needed for mid-term mass uptake of e-fuels.
