# EU strategy for Smart Sector Integration

*Consultation response · Brussels · 2020-06-02*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/eu-strategy-smart-sector-integration) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2020_05_TE_response_smart_sector_integration.pdf)

> The paper summarizes T&E's response to the questions asked by the European Commission on its strategy for smart sector integration. T&E recommends for this strategy to focus on better integrating the electricity and transport sectors.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Decarbonising shipping and aviation with electrofuels alone would require an estimated 798 TWh and 912 TWh of renewable electricity respectively (1,710 TWh combined) — almost double the EU's total 2015 renewable electricity generation, per T&E's 2050 decarbonisation scenarios.
- There were about 1.3 million electric vehicles on European roads at the time of writing, forecast to grow roughly tenfold within 5 years and reach 33–44 million EVs in the EU by 2030.
- T&E/Renault Group/Iberdrola/Enel's "Batteries on Wheels" study found smart EV charging (vs. unmanaged/passive charging) delivers a net system benefit of €0.5–1.3bn/year by 2040 across France, Great Britain, Spain and Italy; in a Spain case study, combining storage, smart charging and V2G could cut curtailment of wind/solar by 70% and cut the grid's carbon intensity by a third.
- By 2030, an estimated 125,000 EVs would be scrapped annually, with 105,000 of their battery packs (2.25 GWh) suitable for second-life stationary storage.
- Renewable hydrogen cost roughly €4.50/kg at the time (more than double fossil hydrogen's ~€1.50/kg), but was forecast to fall below €2/kg by 2030; at a carbon price of ~€45/tonne CO2 and gas price of €4.5/MMBtu, renewable hydrogen could become cheaper than fossil hydrogen.
- Steam methane reforming (used for "decarbonised"/fossil hydrogen with CCS) captures only ~60% of CO2 today (up to 90% with advanced reformers not yet deployed at scale); CCS itself carries an energy penalty of up to 40%; 16 of 17 large-scale CCS projects in operation route captured CO2 to enhanced oil recovery.
- Advanced liquid biofuels are projected to supply only 2.3% of total transport energy (cars, trucks, aviation) by 2030; sustainable waste-based biomethane could cover only about 6.2–9.5% of projected 2030 EU transport demand.
- Fuel-cell cars are projected to make up less than 0.4% of European zero-emission vehicle production by 2025, against a sixfold increase in plug-in EV production to 4 million units.
- Targets cited for 2030/2050: wind+solar reaching at least 50% of EU power production by 2030 (~60% by 2050); total renewables reaching 75% of power production by 2030 (94% by 2050), needed to remain Paris-compliant with a ≥55% 2030 GHG reduction target.

- This May 2020 T&E consultation response to the European Commission's "EU strategy for Smart Sector Integration" argues the biggest gain from linking the power and transport sectors is direct electrification with smart demand-side management (e.g., EV smart charging, vehicle-to-grid), guided by an "energy efficiency first" principle — indirect electrification (via hydrogen/electrofuels) should be reserved for sectors that cannot be directly electrified.
- T&E's core recommendation: target electrofuels (hydrogen, e-kerosene, ammonia, etc.) specifically at aviation and shipping, where direct electrification isn't feasible, via policies like fuel taxes, a low-carbon fuel standard for aviation, an operational CO2 standard for ships, and folding both sectors into the EU ETS with revenues used to scale up electrofuels production.
- T&E argues food/feed/energy-crop biofuels should play no decarbonisation role, and that "decarbonised gases" (fossil hydrogen via steam methane reforming + CCS) should not be treated as zero-emission given fugitive methane, incomplete CO2 capture, CCS energy penalties, and reliance on enhanced oil recovery for CO2 storage.
- On market design: T&E calls for swift implementation of the EU's revised electricity Market Design rules, smart-meter rollout, standardisation for EV/charger interoperability (including access to battery state-of-health data), and mainstreaming "smart" charging requirements into the Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Directive and Energy Performance of Buildings Directive.
- On electrofuel sustainability: T&E calls for a robust additionality framework (so electrofuel production drives genuinely new renewable capacity rather than diverting existing renewable electricity) as a prerequisite for a credible renewable-electrofuels industry, particularly for aviation/shipping lead markets.
