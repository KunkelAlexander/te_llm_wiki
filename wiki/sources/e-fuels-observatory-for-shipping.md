# E-Fuels observatory for shipping

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-06-03*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/e-fuels-observatory-for-shipping) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/e-fuels-Observatory-for-shipping_briefing-FINAL.docx-2.pdf)

> An overview of clean fuels projects and their supply potential to meet the needs of the European maritime industry

<!-- ENRICHED SUMMARY: hand-written, preserved across regenerations by 1_build_wiki_subset.py -->
## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- T&E's e-fuels observatory identified 61 e-fuel projects that could supply shipping as of early 2024, of which only 17 are dedicated solely to the maritime sector (1.06 Mtoe potential capacity by 2030) — enough to meet 3.76% of EU shipping's total regulated energy demand (28.2 Mtoe).
- Projected shipping e-fuel demand under FuelEU Maritime/ETS is only 0.11 Mtoe (0.4% of regulated shipping energy) by 2030, meaning potential supply could exceed near-term demand — but only 0.13 Mtoe of the 61 projects (11 projects) have reached final investment decision (FID), and just 6 of those are shipping-dedicated (0.07 Mtoe).
- Green hydrogen, e-ammonia and e-methanol each have roughly 19–21 projects and a combined ~7 Mtoe potential production; among shipping-dedicated projects, e-ammonia dominates long-run potential (77% of maritime-dedicated volumes) despite no FIDs yet, while e-methanol and green hydrogen are further along commercially.
- Denmark leads potential production (52% of surveyed volumes, 14 projects, 3.6 Mtoe), driven largely by large-scale green hydrogen; Spain and Portugal follow with 9% and 8% respectively. For shipping-dedicated projects specifically, Spain leads (its San Roque Ammonia project: 750,000 t/year, 0.33 Mtoe, by 2027 if FID reached), followed by Finland and Norway.
- Maritime ETS revenues are projected to reach up to €8 billion/year by 2030, most retained by member states; the Commission has earmarked roughly €1.7bn for shipping via the Innovation Fund.
- FuelEU Maritime requires an 80% GHG-intensity reduction for shipping by 2050 (only 6% by 2030), with a "sunrise clause" 2% RFNBO uptake mandate triggered from 2034 if voluntary uptake stays below 1% by 2031; RED III recommends an indicative 1.2% marine RFNBO supply sub-target.
- Maersk has ordered 25 large methanol-fuelled container ships (first launched 2023, delivery through 2027); global methanol ship orders tripled in 2023 versus 2022 (138 new ships).

- T&E recommends: mandatory (not just indicative) national 1.2%+ marine RFNBO supply targets under RED III; using maritime ETS revenues to subsidise early e-fuel production; aligning FuelEU Maritime's ambition with the EU's 2040 climate target during its 2027 review; and introducing binding EU-wide marine e-fuels supply obligations.
