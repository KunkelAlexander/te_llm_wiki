# Tankering in aviation

*Report · Brussels · 2022-11-22*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/tankering-in-aviation) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202211_tankering_aviation_refuelEU.pdf)

> Assessing the impact of the ReFuelEU SAF mandate

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Today, 37% of intra-European flights would find it profitable to "tanker" (carry extra fuel to exploit cheaper prices at the departure airport), causing 457,000 extra tonnes of CO2/year — more than the combined yearly emissions of Germany's three busiest domestic routes (Berlin-Frankfurt, Berlin-Munich, Munich-Hamburg).
- ReFuelEU's SAF mandate (homogenising airport fuel prices) plus carbon pricing would cut tankering-driven extra fuel consumption by 30% (2030) and 42% (2035) — but without a fair kerosene tax, tankering would still cause 280,000-300,000 avoidable tonnes of CO2/year, roughly equal to all flights between Paris-Nice and Paris-Toulouse combined.
- 90% of flights that practise tankering are under 1,500km, emitting 75% of the extra tankering-related CO2 — meaning a proposed short-haul exemption from the "anti-tankering" fuel-uplift rule would fail to address most of the problem (and could let up to a fifth of Turkish Airlines' EU-Turkey fuel needs be tankered outside the mandate).
- Applying the Commission's proposed 90%-fuel-uplift "anti-tankering" rule at route level rather than airport level cuts tankering-driven extra fuel burn by at least 80% in all modelled scenarios, fully securing ReFuelEU's intended emissions cuts — versus the airport-level version, which leaves airlines a 10% buffer that could still enable "SAF-avoidance" tankering via non-EU airports.

- This November 2022 T&E study (Juliette Egal, Matteo Mirolo) models intra-European aircraft "tankering" — carrying extra fuel to exploit cheaper prices elsewhere — using 2019 AIS flight data, and assesses how the ReFuelEU Aviation SAF mandate and its "anti-tankering" fuel-uplift provision would affect the practice.
- Central recommendation: apply the Commission's proposed Article 5 anti-tankering rule (requiring airlines to uplift at least 90% of needed fuel at a given airport) at the route level rather than the airport level, to close a loophole where airlines could otherwise concentrate their 10% tankering allowance on extra-EU routes to specific non-mandated destinations (e.g. Turkey, Morocco, Switzerland) and avoid SAF uptake entirely.
- Also recommends against any short-haul (<1200-1500km) exemption from the anti-tankering rule, since T&E's data shows short flights account for the vast majority of both tankering incidents and the resulting extra CO2.
- Notes a nuance: since large "Union" airports (subject to the SAF mandate) typically already have cheaper fuel than smaller non-mandated airports, most tankering actually results in a net "SAF boost" (more SAF uplifted than in a no-tankering scenario) rather than SAF avoidance — but the fuel-penalty weight cost from tankering itself still adds unnecessary emissions regardless.
