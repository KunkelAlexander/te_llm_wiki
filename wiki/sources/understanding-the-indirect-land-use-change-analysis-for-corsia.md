# Understanding the indirect land use change analysis for Corsia

*Report · Brussels · 2020-02-03*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/understanding-indirect-land-use-change-analysis-corsia) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2019_12_Cerulogy_ILUC-in-CORSIA.pdf)

> This new report looks in detail at the assumptions and criteria used to determine the "indirect land use change" of biofuels under the UN's carbon offsets scheme for aviation emissions (known as Corsia).

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- CORSIA's default ILUC factors were modelled with two frameworks — GTAP-BIO (general equilibrium) and GLOBIOM (partial equilibrium) — amortised over 25 years (vs. the EU's 20-year and the US's 30-year conventions).
- Where the two models' results are within 8.9 gCO2e/MJ of each other (10% of the fossil-kerosene baseline intensity), the average is used as the default; where they diverge more, the *lower* of the two results is used, adjusted upward by only 4.45 gCO2e/MJ — a methodological choice the report says imposes "a significant optimism bias" on the final defaults.
- Food-based pathway defaults: US corn ATJ ~20+ gCO2e/MJ (similar to California LCFS); sugarcane-based fuels relatively low; vegetable-oil-based fuels highest, but still below RED II/earlier EU GLOBIOM values — e.g. Malaysia/Indonesia palm oil HEFA default cited at 139.1 gCO2e/MJ in the extracted table (soy and rapeseed oil HEFA also assessed, with GLOBIOM results for soy/palm oil HEFA significantly higher than the agreed defaults — high enough that, had GLOBIOM been used directly, both fuels would show a higher GHG intensity than fossil jet fuel).
- Cellulosic/energy-crop pathways were assigned negative default ILUC values (net carbon sequestration), with miscanthus-based alcohol-to-jet reaching about ‑54 gCO2e/MJ in the underlying modelling, described in the text as unusually large due to GTAP-BIO's assumption that most miscanthus land conversion comes from "cropland pasture."
- Palm oil peat-conversion assumptions were revised down from the earlier EU analysis: the share of new Indonesian palm expansion assumed on peat fell from 34% to 20% (vs. 25% used by the European Commission's own high-ILUC-risk analysis), and the peat carbon-loss rate fell from 61 tCO2e/ha/yr (95 for the GTAP-BIO input) to 38 tCO2e/ha/yr — itself derived by discounting an IPCC average value of 55 tCO2e/ha/yr for past disturbance, a step the report calls "somewhat controversial." Using the older peat values, the report estimates the palm oil HEFA ILUC value from GLOBIOM would have been about 95 gCO2e/MJ (materially higher than the adopted default).
- Amortising over 25 rather than 20 years alone reduces most reported ILUC factors by about 20% relative to earlier EU GLOBIOM work.

- Commissioned by T&E and authored by Dr Chris Malins of Cerulogy (December 2019), the report is a critical technical review of how ICAO's CAEP process derived the default ILUC emission factors used to assess alternative aviation fuels (AAF) under CORSIA, the UN's carbon offsetting scheme for international aviation.
- Its central argument: the CORSIA default ILUC values are systematically lower than earlier EU-commissioned GLOBIOM analysis, and this gap traces less to genuine modelling improvements than to a series of methodological choices — the "pick the lower model result" rule, reduced peat-emission assumptions, and discounting "forgone sequestration" on abandoned farmland — that consistently favour lower reported ILUC for biofuels.
- The report catalogues structural reasons the two models (GTAP-BIO vs GLOBIOM) diverge: different treatment of agricultural productivity response, food-vs-fuel consumption effects, the "livestock rebound effect" for soy co-products, trade substitution patterns, and — most importantly — different land-conversion categories (GTAP-BIO's low-carbon "cropland pasture" vs GLOBIOM's higher-carbon "other natural land").
- T&E's position/framing: CORSIA's ILUC methodology represents genuine progress (the first ICAO/global aviation scheme to include any lifecycle/ILUC accounting for alternative fuels), but the final defaults reflect a political compromise among ICAO states, industry and NGOs (via ICSA) rather than a purely evidence-based outcome, and should be treated as one alternative scenario alongside — not a replacement for — the earlier 2015 EU Commission-commissioned ILUC analysis.
- See [Aviation carbon offsetting scheme: ICAO circulates draft rules](aviation-carbon-offsetting-scheme-icao-circulates-draft-rules.md) for the earlier procedural milestone (the 2018 draft CORSIA SARP rules) that this ILUC methodology was developed under.
