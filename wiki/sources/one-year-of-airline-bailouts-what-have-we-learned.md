# One year of airline bailouts: what have we learned?

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-03-29*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/one-year-airline-bailouts-what-have-we-learned) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202103_bailouts_briefing_final.pdf)

> European airlines – some of the EU's biggest polluters – have so far received an unprecedented €37 billion in government bailouts since the beginning of the Covid-19 crisis, nearly entirely without binding environmental conditions.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- European airlines had received €37 billion in government bailouts since the start of COVID-19 (mostly spring 2020), nearly entirely without binding environmental conditions; industry was seeking about €7 billion more (including from already-rescued companies) as of publication.
- IATA estimated nearly 25 million direct aviation jobs were under threat globally.
- European aviation CO2 emissions rose 27.6% over the six years leading into the crisis (excluding non-CO2 effects, cited here as two-thirds of aviation's total climate impact).
- Cited research found 1% of people cause half of global aviation emissions.
- Aviation's tax exemptions were valued at €39.9bn/year (VAT not levied) and €26.9bn/year (kerosene tax not levied).
- Only France and Austria attempted climate conditions on bailouts (for Air France and Austrian Airlines respectively); T&E's analysis found both insufficient to meaningfully reduce emissions.
- T&E recommendations reference ending aviation's current 85% free EU ETS allowance allocation as part of a "better pricing" precondition for further bailouts.

- This March 2021 T&E briefing assesses the first year of European airline COVID-19 bailouts and sets out conditions T&E argues should govern any further state aid.
- Central argument: aviation bailouts should not be blank cheques — any further State aid must come with binding green conditionality (an aviation decarbonisation plan) rather than the largely unconditional support seen so far, of which only France's and Austria's included climate conditions, both judged inadequate by T&E.
- T&E's three-question test for governments considering a bailout: (1) is this genuinely the best way to protect workers/services, or would alternatives (retraining, fleet reduction) work better; (2) does the aid include effective, binding green conditions (temporary, repayable, tied to a science-based net-zero-by-2050 commitment including non-CO2 emissions); (3) is there a wider national aviation decarbonisation plan (SAF mandates, ETS reform, kerosene taxation, ticket taxes) that these commitments are integrated into.
- T&E calls for reforming the EU's COVID-19 State Aid "Temporary Framework" — arguing the pandemic should no longer qualify as an "exceptional occurrence" under Article 107(2)(b) TFEU more than a year in, and that the Framework should not be extended beyond December 2021 — and for not extending the 2014–2024 transition period for phasing out airport operating aid.
- T&E frames bailouts as raising a fairness question: given aviation's near-tax-free status and evidence that frequent flying and its associated emissions are concentrated among wealthier households, using public money to restore pre-COVID flight volumes risks subsidising the wealthiest to pollute at the expense of broader climate efforts.
- See [Airline beggars can't be choosers](airline-beggars-can-t-be-choosers.md) for T&E's earlier (2020) take on bailout conditionality, and [Revision of the EU ETS for aviation](revision-of-the-eu-ets-for-aviation.md) for the detailed ETS reforms (free-allowance removal, cap reduction) referenced as part of the recommended decarbonisation plan here.
