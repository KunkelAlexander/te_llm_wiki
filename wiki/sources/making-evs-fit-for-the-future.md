# Making EVs fit for the future

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-04-22*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/making-evs-fit-for-the-future) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/EV-environmental-score-briefing_FINAL_TE_IMT_BEUC.pdf)

> A T&E briefing sets out how to design an environmental score for EVs.

## Summary

*A joint briefing by T&E, IMT (IDDRI) and BEUC; see the companion op-ed,
[A streamlined EV 'eco-score' would encourage green, made-in-Europe electric cars](a-streamlined-ev-eco-score-would-encourage-green-made-in-europe-electric-cars.md), for a shorter version of the same proposal.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- 50%–60% of an EV's lifecycle emissions come from production (embedded emissions), versus only ~10% for combustion cars; batteries, steel and aluminium together account for ~70–75% of those embedded emissions.
- SUVs are 54% of new EU vehicle sales, up from 10% in 2010, driving larger batteries (some forecasts put average battery capacity above 70 kWh by 2030).
- Proposed formula: **environmental score = energy efficiency (kWh/km) × combined carbon footprint of battery + steel + aluminium (kgCO2)**.
- Worked example: a small EV made in Europe scores ~700; the same small EV made in China scores ~1,200; a large EV made in Europe scores ~1,300; a large EV made in China scores ~2,200 (lower score = better). Halving the materials' carbon footprint would roughly halve these scores (e.g. small EU EV to 363).
- Example efficiency/battery mismatch: for every extra 20 kWh of battery capacity (~33% increase), energy consumption rises only ~1 Wh/km (~5%) — showing efficiency thresholds alone barely constrain battery/vehicle size.
- Illustrative model comparison: a Dacia Spring (27 kWh battery, 1,000 kg, 13 kWh/100km) is rated identically to an Audi Q8 e-tron (114 kWh battery, 2,585 kg, 26 kWh/100km) under current EU rules, since both are simply "zero emission."
- Lifecycle CO2 for ICE and PHEVs is on average 3x and 2x higher than BEVs respectively.
- The EU's 2023 car CO2 law requires the Commission to assess minimum EV energy-efficiency thresholds and adopt a full-lifecycle-CO2 reporting methodology by end of 2025, with voluntary manufacturer reporting from June 2026.

- T&E, IMT and BEUC propose a harmonised EU "environmental-score" methodology for EVs, arguing an efficiency-only metric would fail to curb growing vehicle/battery size or embedded carbon, and that full lifecycle-analysis (LCA) is too complex to apply model-by-model (the European Commission's own 2020 Ricardo study reached the same conclusion).
- The score would let manufacturers choose their own compliance path (smaller vehicles/batteries, low-carbon steel/aluminium/batteries, or efficiency gains) and could be used for consumer labelling (Car Labelling Directive review), national vehicle taxation/subsidies (referencing France's existing eco-bonus conditionality), corporate fleet targets, or manufacturer EU fleet-average requirements.
- Recommends the next European Commission adopt a common scoring methodology by December 2025 and mandate reporting from June 2026.
