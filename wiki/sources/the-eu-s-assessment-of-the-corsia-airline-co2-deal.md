# The EU's assessment of the Corsia airline CO2 deal

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-03-17*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/eus-assessment-corsia-airline-co2-deal) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Ares20211459392.pdf)

> T&E has obtained the EU Commission's assessment of the UN's aviation offsetting scheme (Corsia), which it received in September 2020 but has delayed publishing. The study finds major flaws in the scheme, calling into further question whether it can be in any way compatible with the EU Green Deal.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- As of July 2020, 81 States had indicated they would participate in CORSIA from 1 January 2021, together accounting for ~77% of international aviation Revenue Tonne Kilometres (RTKs); 5 non-exempt States that had not opted in (China, Russia, India, Brazil, Vietnam) account for ~18% of RTKs.
- ICAO projected international aviation fuel efficiency would improve by only 1.37%/year even in its most optimistic scenario — far outpaced by ICAO's own pre-COVID projection of 4.1%/year growth in passenger traffic (Revenue Passenger Kilometres) and an expected 220–310% increase in international emissions by 2045 (vs. baseline); ICCT data showed a 32% increase in all aviation emissions between 2013–2018 (a 5.7%/year compound rate, 70% higher than ICAO's own growth assumption).
- Fuel-switching abatement costs cited range from ~€217/tonne CO2e (UCO-based HEFA) to over €4,000/tonne CO2e (corn grain alcohol-to-jet) — both far above the likely cost of CORSIA offset credits, meaning it is systematically cheaper for airlines to buy offsets than cut their own emissions.
- ICAO's own 2016 CAEP analysis estimated 142–174 million tonnes of CO2 emitted in 2025 would need to be offset for "carbon neutral growth" from 2020, rising to 288–376 million tonnes by a later year in the pilot phase.
- The EU's "Stop the Clock" mechanism (Directive 2008/101/EC, suspended since April 2013, extended twice, running to end-2023) currently limits EU ETS aviation coverage to intra-EEA flights only.

- This is the European Commission's own commissioned technical study (ICF Consulting, Air Transportation Analytics, NewClimate Institute, Cambridge Econometrics, HFW, and Sven Starckx; delivered September 2020) assessing ICAO's CORSIA offsetting scheme under Article 28b of the EU ETS Directive, obtained and published by T&E after the Commission delayed releasing it — the fulltext captured for this page is the EC-commissioned study itself, not T&E's own analysis.
- The study's own conclusion: CORSIA's ambition is "misaligned with, and weaker than" the level required by the Paris Agreement on several dimensions — target type/stringency, coverage of climate-impact sources, policy timeframe, and enforcement mechanisms — and is less ambitious than the EU ETS's existing aviation regulation on most of these same dimensions.
- The study warns that EU participation in CORSIA in place of (rather than alongside) EU ETS coverage of international aviation risks undermining the European Green Deal's goal of a 90% transport-emissions cut by 2050 and the EU's broader claim to global climate leadership.
- On environmental integrity, the study flags that a meaningful share of large emitters (China, Russia, India, Brazil) remain outside CORSIA's initial voluntary phases, and that COVID-19 substantially disrupted both the traffic baseline (ICAO shifted to a 2019-only baseline) and near-term offset demand projections.
- T&E's framing (in its accompanying press coverage) is that this EC-commissioned assessment independently corroborates T&E's long-standing critique of CORSIA as environmentally weak and calls into further question whether the scheme is compatible with the EU's own climate law.
