# Aviation's CO2: use it or bury it?

*Report · Brussels · 2021-08-06*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/aviations-co2-use-it-or-bury-it) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2021_08_TE_study_efuels_DACCS.pdf)

> T&E commissioned a study by the Öko-Institut to compare, based on cost and climate benefits, two possible uses for captured CO2 emissions from aviation.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- E-fuel production costs are currently 3–4x higher than fossil kerosene.
- Under the study's standard scenarios, Direct Air Capture with Carbon Storage (DACCS) — offsetting fossil kerosene emissions by capturing and storing an equivalent amount of CO2 — induces lower additional costs than replacing fossil kerosene with e-fuels, over the 2020–2050 period; but this cost advantage shrinks or disappears once non-CO2 climate impacts and moderately rising kerosene prices are factored in (sensitivity scenarios).
- Additional costs to defossilise European aviation (under either option) stay below 1 €cent per passenger-km through 2050, and below 5% of the average ticket price.
- Estimated cumulative CO2 storage need under the DACCS option, 2020–2050: 1.1–2.4 Gt — a small fraction of the 1,200 Gt some IPCC 1.5°C scenarios suggest may need storing; estimated global storage capacity ranges from 8,000–55,000 Gt, so site availability is not seen as a near-term constraint, though competition for the best/cheapest sites could raise costs.

- This August 2021 T&E-commissioned Öko-Institut study compares two ways of using captured CO2 to address aviation's climate impact: producing e-kerosene (synthetic fuel from renewable electricity + captured CO2) versus Direct Air Capture with Carbon Storage (DACCS, i.e. capturing and permanently storing CO2 to offset continued fossil kerosene use).
- Central finding: under standard assumptions DACCS is cheaper than e-fuels as a way to "defossilise" European aviation, following an earlier UK Climate Change Committee (2019) finding to the same effect — but this conclusion is sensitive to assumptions about aviation's non-CO2 effects and future kerosene prices, and could reverse if e-kerosene reduces non-CO2 effects more than assumed.
- Key caveat T&E draws from the study: even the cheaper DACCS pathway does not make aviation climate-neutral by 2050 on its own — it addresses only the CO2 that would otherwise still be emitted from fossil kerosene, so demand reduction, zero-emission aircraft, and e-fuels are all still needed alongside any CO2 removal/storage strategy.
- The study notes both options depend on ambitious, largely unproven-at-scale technology development, meaning strong technology-push and demand-pull policy support is needed regardless of which pathway is prioritised — and that DAC capacity is a shared bottleneck for both options.
- See [Why direct air capture holds one of the keys to sustainable aviation](why-direct-air-capture-holds-one-of-the-keys-to-sustainable-aviation.md) for T&E's companion study on how DAC itself could be scaled up for e-fuel production, and [FAQ: the what and how of e-kerosene](faq-the-what-and-how-of-e-kerosene.md) for T&E's broader position favouring e-kerosene over pure offsetting/storage approaches.
