# How to ensure the sustainability of electrofuels

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-01-19*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-ensure-sustainability-electrofuels) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/T_2024-04-29-111426_btbg.pdf)

> Hydrogen and other electrofuels like ammonia or ekerosene are vital for supplying zero-emission fuels to long-distance transport sectors like shipping and aviation. By the end of 2021, the EU will decide on new rules for the production of hydrogen and other electrofuels, the so-called Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- To meet RED II's 70% GHG-savings threshold for RFNBOs, the electricity input needs to be roughly ≥80% renewable/zero-emission, whether complemented by gas-turbine or average-EU28-grid electricity.
- If e-fuels relied entirely on average EU grid electricity, their carbon footprint would be about 3x higher than the fossil comparator (~300 gCO2eq/MJ).
- Grid electricity used for hydrogen electrolysis would need a carbon intensity well below ~149 gCO2/kWh (break-even vs. fossil natural gas) and below ~90 gCO2/kWh for synthetic hydrocarbons (break-even vs. fossil counterparts) — a threshold essentially no EU grid meets except Sweden, Norway and Iceland.
- T&E's own projection: sustainable advanced aviation biofuels could total ~7,500 ktoe by 2050, meeting only 11.4% of European aviation fuel demand — the remainder must come from electrofuels.
- A cited T&E freight study: ~2,800 full-load hours give a ~30% load factor for a megawatt-scale electrolyser, at a hydrogen cost of €2.33–4.00/kg (excl. transport/distribution); North Sea offshore wind can already exceed 3,600 full-load hours/year.
- Blue hydrogen (steam methane reforming + CCS) lifecycle emissions estimates range 30–99 gCO2/kWh; SMR captures only ~60% of CO2 today (up to 90% with advanced reformers); CCS carries an energy penalty of up to 40% (with CO2 compression/liquefaction alone using up to 12%); 16 of 17 operating large-scale CCS projects route captured CO2 to enhanced oil recovery.
- Electrofuel water use: ~9 litres of purified water per kg of hydrogen produced (0.07 litres per MJ of stored energy, requiring 0.17 litres of seawater if desalinated) — around 500x lower than the 33–476 litres per MJ needed for first-generation biofuels; total water for the EU transport sector's RFNBOs is estimated at 270 billion litres, versus ~68,000 litres used by a typical fossil/nuclear power plant (for cooling).
- Land use for renewable-electricity generation to meet one-third of a "base case" hydrogen/electrofuels demand scenario: ~16,664 km² for solar PV (about half the size of Belgium), ~86,891 km² for onshore wind (just under the size of Portugal), or ~43,579 km² for offshore wind (about the size of Denmark).
- The "Congo Green Hydrogen" project cited as a cautionary example: a proposed 44 GW hydropower project in the DRC, double the size of China's Three Gorges Dam.

- This January 2021 T&E briefing sets out recommendations for the European Commission's forthcoming (end-2021) delegated act defining sustainability/additionality rules for Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBOs — e-hydrogen, e-ammonia, e-methanol, e-diesel, e-kerosene) under RED II.
- Core recommendation: use Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) for new, unsupported renewable capacity — not Guarantees of Origin (GOs) or the proposed "GO+" — as the mechanism to prove additionality, because GOs lack the price certainty needed to finance new renewable capacity and can't establish the required "temporal correlation" between generation and RFNBO production.
- T&E argues for strict temporal correlation (intraday matching between renewable generation and electrolyser operation) and geographic correlation (same electricity bidding zone) from the outset, rejecting a multi-year transition period with looser rules as distortive and technically unnecessary.
- T&E reiterates that Direct Air Capture should be the sole long-term carbon source for carbon-based RFNBOs (e-kerosene etc.), with fossil CCU phased out as an eligible source by 2025 (pre-2025 investments grandfathered) — the same position as [Which renewable transport fuels deliver substantial carbon reductions?](which-renewable-transport-fuels-deliver-substantial-carbon-reductions.md).
- T&E argues fossil ("blue") hydrogen via steam methane reforming + CCS should not be treated as a genuine low-carbon fuel or promoted under RED, citing fugitive methane, incomplete capture rates, CCS energy penalties, and reliance on enhanced oil recovery for storage.
- On water and land impacts, T&E argues RFNBO production is far less resource-intensive than biofuel production, but still calls for mandatory local water-availability assessments and public consultation for large RFNBO projects.
- On RFNBO imports from outside the EU, T&E calls for strict additionality safeguards (direct connection or very-low-carbon grid only, initially) and warns against a "resource curse" dynamic — citing a proposed 44 GW DRC hydropower-to-hydrogen mega-project as a cautionary example — recommending that RFNBO export projects also help decarbonise and improve energy access in host countries.
- See [Which renewable transport fuels deliver substantial carbon reductions?](which-renewable-transport-fuels-deliver-substantial-carbon-reductions.md) for T&E's companion technical letter to the JRC on the same GHG-accounting methodology, and [EU strategy for Smart Sector Integration](eu-strategy-for-smart-sector-integration.md) for the broader direct-electrification-first framing this briefing's aviation/shipping electrofuel case sits within.
