# Aviation: International scientists warn of warming impact of contrails and risks of delaying action

*Letter · Brussels · 2024-11-20*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/aviation-international-scientists-warn-of-warming-impact-of-contrails-and-risks-of-delaying-action)

> A coalition of leading aviation and climate scientists come together in the closing days of the COP29 conference in Azerbaijan to call for more action on reducing warming contrails.

## Summary

*Open letter citing the findings of T&E's briefing, [Aviation: Réduire l'impact climatique des traînées de condensation coûterait moins de 4 euros par vol](aviation-r-duire-l-impact-climatique-des-tra-n-es-de-condensation-co-terait-moin.md) — see that page for the full quantitative analysis (cost estimates, benefit ratios, per-flight figures) referenced below.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Contrails have a net warming effect estimated to be at least as significant as aviation's total CO2 emissions; only 3% of flights generate 80% of contrail warming.
- T&E research (cited in the letter) finds targeted rerouting of this small share of flights could cut global contrail warming by more than half before 2040, at an estimated cost of under €4 per flight.
- The climate benefit of reducing contrails is estimated to be at least 15 times larger than the CO2 cost of the extra fuel burned from rerouting.
- The IPCC first recognised contrails' warming effect 25 years before this letter (i.e. in the late 1990s).

- Signed by over 50 scientists across atmospheric science, climate policy and transport research (institutions including ETH Zürich, University of Cambridge, University of Oxford, DLR, Imperial College London, and others), timed to the closing days of COP29 in Baku.
- Calls on policymakers to: raise public awareness of contrails' climate impact (including in flight-booking information and corporate travel reporting); establish a policy framework backed by robust monitoring to reduce contrails and other non-CO2 effects; and support large-scale contrail-avoidance trials.
- Stresses that addressing contrails is not a substitute for cutting aviation's CO2 emissions, which remain equally urgent.
