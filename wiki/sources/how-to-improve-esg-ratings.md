# How to improve ESG ratings

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Global ESG-related assets under management are projected to grow from $18.4 trillion (2021) to $33.9 trillion by 2026 (PwC), representing 21.5% of total global assets under management.
- Vehicle lifecycle emissions ("use of sold products") represent 98% of a car's total emissions, yet this factor receives under 1% weight in major ESG ratings (e.g. just 0.6% in S&P's methodology) — the central example T&E uses to illustrate ESG ratings' disconnect from real climate impact.
- Transport accounts for 21% of global emissions; the maritime sector alone contributed about 2.8% of global GHG emissions in 2020, yet container shipping giant Maersk received similar ESG ratings to cleantech firms like Vestas Wind Systems in 2022.
- A referenced academic study ("Aggregate Confusion: The Divergence of ESG Ratings") finds measurement-methodology differences account for 56% of the divergence between different agencies' ESG scores for the same company.

- This June 2023 T&E position paper responds to the European Commission's June 2023 proposed regulation on ESG rating transparency and integrity, arguing current ESG ratings systematically fail to capture real sustainability performance — especially for polluting transport companies (carmakers, shipping lines, aircraft manufacturers) that receive misleadingly high scores.
- Central diagnosis: ESG ratings mostly measure "inward materiality" (risk to the company from ESG factors) rather than "outward" or "double materiality" (the company's actual impact on climate/environment/society) — meaning a carmaker's exposure to regulatory risk is scored, but not the climate impact of the cars it sells.
- Case studies: Toyota scores above 50/100 on environmental ratings despite ICCT research showing it lags on EV transition; major container shippers (Hapag-Lloyd, Maersk, Cosco) show inconsistent, non-transparent scores across providers; aircraft manufacturers (Airbus, ThyssenKrupp, Safran) show similarly disconnected high scores.
- Seven core recommendations to the EU: mandate double materiality in the legal definition of ESG ratings; set minimum sectoral quality thresholds and materiality weightings (e.g. lifecycle emissions weighted 60% instead of 0.6% for carmakers); weight absolute performance alongside peer-relative risk; explicitly link the "E" score to 1.5°C-pathway alignment and fossil-fuel phase-out; ensure coherence with CSRD/SFDR/EU Taxonomy; improve market transparency/governance (ESMA-led registration and supervision); and require independent verification rather than reliance on self-reported company data.
