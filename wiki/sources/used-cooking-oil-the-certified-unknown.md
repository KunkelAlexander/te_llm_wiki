# Used Cooking Oil: The Certified Unknown

*News · Brussels · 2024-12-11*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/uco) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Used_Cooking_Oil_The_Certified_Unknown1.pdf)

*(No abstract was published for this document; summary below written from the source text.)*

This briefing examines how EU-endorsed "voluntary scheme" certification for used cooking oil (UCO) biofuels — now over a third of European biofuel consumption — relies on paper-based audits and a fraud-prone "mass-balance" accounting system rather than physical testing, and argues the EU should stop counting imported UCO and other hard-to-verify biofuel feedstocks toward its renewable energy targets.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The International Sustainability & Carbon Certification (ISCC) scheme — the largest UCO certifier — has over 1,275 valid UCO certificates in its database, including at least 426 from China, Malaysia and Indonesia (the top three UCO exporters to Europe).
- A restaurant supplying UCO only needs to be individually audited if it supplies over 5 tonnes/month — equivalent to cooking more than 50 tonnes of fries/month, or the average monthly output of about 18 fast-food restaurants combined — meaning almost no actual points-of-origin ever face audits.
- T&E's own analysis of ISCC-certified UCO collectors in China, Malaysia and Indonesia found only 9% of certified collecting points had any of their points of origin verified via an actual audit; for the remaining 91%, a phone call, email or online search of a sample was enough to certify all of a collector's listed sources.
- ISCC has at least 46 different certification bodies (CBs) conducting UCO audits on its behalf, creating competitive pressure ("race to the bottom") between CBs competing for business from the operators they audit.
- ISCC's governing board has 4 of 7 seats held by biofuels-industry representatives; of its 296 association members, very few are NGOs not aligned with industry interests.

- T&E identifies three fraud mechanisms: mixing virgin oil (e.g. palm oil) into UCO ("adulteration"), mislabelling virgin oil as UCO outright, and "multiple claiming" — certifying the same volume of fuel as sustainable more than once across different schemes/bodies.
- Argues the EU's mass-balance accounting (which tracks paperwork rather than requiring physical segregation of sustainable/non-sustainable feedstocks) and the inherent industry-funded structure of voluntary certification schemes make fraud very difficult to detect or prevent, and that the new EU Union Database for Biofuels — still reliant on the same certification data — won't fix this.
- Recommends the EU stop letting imported UCO (and other hard-to-verify feedstocks like animal fats, intermediate crops, crops on degraded land) count toward renewable energy targets, restrict UCO/POME imports more broadly, and pursue certification-system reforms: a dedicated EU/UK fraud investigation unit, a formal grievance/ombudsman procedure, mandatory multi-stakeholder scheme governance, and public, detailed audit reporting.
- Companion analysis to T&E's broader [UCO (Unknown Cooking Oil): High hopes on limited and suspicious materials](uco-unknown-cooking-oil-high-hopes-on-limited-and-suspicious-materials.md) study, focusing specifically on the certification/verification mechanics rather than trade-flow economics.
