# Ultrafine particles from aircraft pose a health risk to 11 million French people

*Original title (French): "Les particules ultrafines des avions font peser un risque sur la santé de 11 millions de Français"*

*Report · France · 2024-06-25*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/te-france/articles/les-particules-ultrafines-des-avions-font-peser-un-risque-sur-la-sante-de-11-millions-de-francais) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/CEDelft_Final_Study.pdf)

> A new study conducted by Transport & Environment (T&E) indicates that thousands of cases of high blood pressure, diabetes and dementia in France and Europe could be linked to the tiny particles emitted by aircraft.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The study estimates 280,000 cases of high blood pressure, 330,000 cases of diabetes, and 18,000 cases of dementia may be linked to ultrafine particle (UFP) emissions among the 52 million people living within 20km of the 32 busiest European airports.
- Estimated increased health risks near airports (within 5km): diabetes and dementia risk at least 20% higher, high blood pressure risk about 7% higher, than the general population.
- UFP concentrations measured 4,000–30,000 particles/cm³ within 5km of Amsterdam Schiphol, 3,000–6,000 particles/cm³ at 5–10km, and 1,000–4,000 particles/cm³ at 10–20km — comparable to measurements at Paris Charles de Gaulle and Copenhagen airports, and higher than typical city-centre UFP levels (3,000–12,000 particles/cm³).
- 3.8 million people live within 5km of the 32 studied airports (the most exposed group), with average UFP concentrations of ~5,000 particles/cm³, rising to ~10,000 near Paris Charles de Gaulle or London Heathrow.
- Switching to 100% hydrotreated jet fuel (very low sulphur/aromatics) could cut UFP particle-number emissions by up to 70% (and particle mass by ~80% including lubrication-oil contributions), proportionally reducing the associated health impacts.
- Long-term exposure to aviation particulate matter (PM) overall is estimated to cause 14,000–21,200 premature deaths/year; aviation PM spreads over a wider area around airports than road-transport PM. SAF made up only 0.2% of jet fuel in 2023, and more than half of jet fuel in Europe may still be fossil well into the 2040s.
- A 2010 EASA-commissioned study found reducing jet fuel sulphur alone could yield €130–430 million/year in monetised health benefits in Europe — findings that were never acted on.
- Standard jet fuel (ASTM D1655) allows up to 25% aromatics, 3% naphthalene and 3,000 ppm sulphur; hydrotreatment can cut sulphur/aromatics at an estimated cost of under €0.05/litre.

- Study (by T&E, extrapolating RIVM's Amsterdam Schiphol research to Europe's 32 busiest airports) argues aviation's ultrafine particle pollution is a serious, under-regulated health issue distinct from — and additive to — its climate impact, disproportionately affecting lower-income populations living near airports.
- T&E recommendations: ban further airport expansion, cap flights, shift to rail, reduce business travel and tax aviation to curb traffic growth; install UFP monitoring around airports and set target values in the next Ambient Air Quality Directive review; and create an EU jet-fuel standard progressively cutting aromatics/sulphur to prepare for zero-aromatic, zero-sulphur SAF.
- The French-office source page frontmatter title cites "11 million French people" specifically (a national subset of the EU-wide 52 million figure, drawn from the French airports — Paris Orly, Paris CDG, Nice, etc. — included in the 32-airport study); the fulltext captured here is the underlying English-language T&E briefing ("Can living near an airport make you ill?"), whose France-specific figures are referenced but not separately broken out in this text.
