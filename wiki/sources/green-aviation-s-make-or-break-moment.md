# Green aviation's make or break moment

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2024-12-20*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/green-aviations-make-or-break-moment)

*(No abstract was published for this document; summary below written from the source text.)*

An op-ed by T&E arguing that 2025 is the decisive year for green aviation: e-kerosene, hydrogen planes and contrail avoidance are all technically ready, but need immediate major investment from oil majors and EU policymakers or the sector risks losing public trust and facing harsher measures like higher taxes and airport caps.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- E-kerosene currently makes up less than 10% of airlines' future SAF agreements, despite 56 e-kerosene projects existing in Europe — none of which will proceed without a final investment decision.
- Contrail avoidance could cost as little as €4 per flight ticket, since only 3% of flights generate 80% of contrail warming — these could be rerouted around cold, humid atmospheric regions.

- Written by a T&E aviation policy staffer, the piece frames 2025 as a "make or break" year, drawing an analogy to the fluorescent-to-LED lighting transition: technological alternatives exist, but require industry investment and strong policy backing to displace the incumbent technology.
- Calls on oil majors (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies) — so far absent from e-kerosene investment, unlike small start-ups — to fund e-fuel production, and on EU lawmakers to channel more money via the upcoming Clean Industrial Deal.
- Notes Airbus's 2019 hydrogen-plane pledge has since seen start-up bankruptcies and warnings of delays from Airbus's own CEO, requiring both further Airbus investment and a regulatory framework to succeed.
- Calls for a 10-year plan to phase out warming contrails, building on the EU's newly introduced airline contrail-monitoring requirement.
- Warns that failure on e-kerosene, hydrogen planes or contrails risks a public/policy backlash (higher taxes, airport caps, traffic limits) as scrutiny of "overtourism" and high-emitting sectors grows.
