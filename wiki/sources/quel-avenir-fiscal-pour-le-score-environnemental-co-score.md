## Summary

*The scraped fulltext for this page is the underlying legal memorandum (by law firm Baldon Avocats, dated 26 November 2024) commissioned by T&E, analysing the WTO/EU-law feasibility of French/EU electric-vehicle subsidies based on the "éco-score" (Environmental Score) — not a T&E-authored briefing text.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Up to 60% of an EV's total lifecycle emissions occur before the vehicle is driven (mostly battery production) — a share expected to grow as electricity grids decarbonise.
- France's current éco-score sets a single 60-point threshold for eligibility to the "bonus écologique" purchase subsidy; T&E proposes a tiered system with multiple thresholds (e.g. up to 100 points) offering higher subsidies for cleaner-scoring EVs.
- Since France's 2024 éco-score reform excluded China-made EVs, Chinese EV sales in France reportedly dropped in 2024 while European-made EV sales rose significantly.
- The EU itself argued the scheme could cut France's carbon footprint by an average 800,000 tonnes of GHG/year between 2024 and 2027.
- Average compact-EV prices remain high — €34,000 (segment A), €37,200 (segment B), €48,200 (segment C) — while many combustion cars sell for under €20,000, a gap the memo says the current single-threshold éco-score worsens by excluding small imported EVs from subsidy eligibility.

- The memo assesses whether France/EU electric-vehicle incentives based on lifecycle-carbon scoring (including T&E's proposed tiered version) comply with WTO law (GATT Articles I, III and the Article XX general exceptions) and EU law, given that non-EU-made EVs (typically produced with more carbon-intensive grids and shipped further) score systematically worse.
- Concludes such measures likely constitute discriminatory "less favourable treatment" of imported EVs under GATT Article III, but that they have a reasonable chance of being justified under Article XX(b) (protecting human/animal/plant health, covering climate mitigation) or Article XX(g) (conservation of exhaustible natural resources, covering GHG/atmosphere), provided the necessity test and chapeau requirements are met — citing the 2024 WTO EU-Palm Oil ruling as a key precedent for how climate-motivated, origin-neutral-on-its-face measures can survive such scrutiny.
- Flags that a single-threshold éco-score design risks undermining EV adoption among price-sensitive buyers by disqualifying small, affordable imported EVs — an argument for T&E's proposed tiered system, which would let compact imported EVs still qualify (at a lower subsidy tier) rather than being excluded outright.
