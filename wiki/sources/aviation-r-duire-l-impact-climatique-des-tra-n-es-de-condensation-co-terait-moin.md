# Aviation: reducing the climate impact of contrails would cost less than 4 euros per flight

*Original title (French): "Aviation : Réduire l'impact climatique des traînées de condensation coûterait moins de 4 euros par vol"*

*Briefing · France · 2024-11-13*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/te-france/articles/aviation-reduire-limpact-climatique-des-trainees-de-condensation-couterait-moins-de-4-euros-par-vol) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Briefing-Evitement-des-trainees-de-condensation.pdf)

> By slightly altering the flight paths of a small portion of the global fleet, the climate impact of contrails could be cut in half before 2040. This measure would cost at most 4 euros per passenger for a transatlantic flight, according to a new study by Transport & Environment (T&E).

## Summary

*See also the companion open letter, [Aviation: International scientists warn of warming impact of contrails and risks of delaying action](aviation-international-scientists-warn-of-warming-impact-of-contrails-and-risks-.md), in which over 50 scientists cite this same T&E research to call for policy action.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Contrails' warming effect is estimated to be at least as large as all of aviation's CO2 emissions combined; in 2019, fewer than 3% of flights globally caused 80% of contrail warming (5% of flights in Europe).
- Targeted contrail avoidance (rerouting the small share of flights causing the most warming) could halve global contrail warming before 2040, per T&E's own scenario analysis.
- Net climate benefit ratio: across all scenarios modelled, avoiding contrails from the worst-offending flights delivers climate benefits 2.35 to over 300 times larger than the CO2 cost of the extra fuel burned from rerouting (real-world tests by American Airlines/Google found ratios as high as 40:1; a cited academic simulation found >100:1).
- Real-world trial: American Airlines/Google 2023 flight tests cut contrail formation by 54% via minor route adjustments, at a cost of ~2% extra fuel burn on the diverted flights.
- Estimated per-flight cost of contrail avoidance: under €1.60 extra per passenger for intra-European flights (e.g. €1.20 for Barcelona–Berlin) and under €4.30 for transatlantic flights (e.g. €3.90 for Paris–New York) — roughly a 1% or smaller fare increase.
- Projected 2040 abatement cost: under €20/tonne CO2e (T&E, using GWP100), notably cheaper than solar/wind power (~€60/tCO2e) or direct air capture and storage (DACCS, ~€360/tCO2e once mature, currently €600–1,000/tCO2e). Using shorter time horizons lowers the cost further (below €12/tCO2e at GWP50, below €6/tCO2e at GWP20).
- Comparison figure: fuel tankering (carrying extra fuel to avoid pricier refuelling stops) already causes an estimated 900kt of CO2/year in Europe (Eurocontrol, 2018) — about the same scale of emissions increase T&E projects from large-scale contrail avoidance.
- The EU's aviation carbon market began monitoring, reporting and verifying (MRV) non-CO2 emissions from 1 January 2025, though initially limited to intra-EU flights for 2025–2026; this data is meant to inform EU non-CO2 standards from 2027.

- T&E recommends: extending non-CO2 MRV globally and closing data gaps via humidity sensors/satellites; adapting European air traffic management to enable systematic contrail avoidance; and revising jet fuel standards (aromatics/sulphur content) to cut residual soot-driven contrail formation, alongside Innovation Fund financing for early adopters and satellite monitoring.
- Frames contrail avoidance as aviation's "climate opportunity of the decade" — a rare low-cost, fast-acting climate lever, distinct from (and complementary to) continued CO2 reduction efforts.
