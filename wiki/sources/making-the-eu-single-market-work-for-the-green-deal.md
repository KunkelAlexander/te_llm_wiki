# Making the EU single market work for the Green Deal

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-06-24*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/making-the-eu-single-market-work-for-the-green-deal) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2024_06_Single_Market_paper.pdf)

> T&E's paper on how to harmonise EU regulations to accelerate transport decarbonisation.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Between 2018 and 2025, member states must draw up at least 16 mandatory (plus 3 optional) EU climate/energy plans — T&E proposes merging 12 of these standalone plans into the National Energy and Climate Plans (NECPs).
- CSRD/ESRS sustainability-reporting cost-benefit analysis (EFRAG, Nov 2022): initial compliance cost is ~0.002% of EU turnover in the first year, rising to ~0.01% by 2025 and 2028 — described as marginal, contrary to industry cost complaints.
- Electrified road transport is projected to raise EU electricity demand by almost 60%, to more than 5,200 TWh/year; cars and vans alone are projected to need 450 TWh/year by 2040, trucks and coaches 240 TWh/year.
- Corporate fleets make up 60% of all new EU car sales, yet lag private buyers on EV uptake: 14.5% of corporate cars registered as ZEVs in 2023, versus 15.6% of private cars. Germany and France (43% of all new EU cars combined) lag particularly badly.
- For zero-emission trucks, Germany and the Netherlands accounted for 60% of EU sales in 2023 despite only 30% of new truck registrations; incentives cover 40–60% of the price gap with diesel trucks in the Netherlands versus up to only €14,000 in Italy — resulting in 7% ZE truck share in the Netherlands versus 0.25% in Italy.
- Large fleets (2–3% of companies) buy about one-third of all new vehicles in the EU.
- T&E estimates recycling could supply 30% of EU cobalt, 18% of nickel, and 10% of lithium/manganese needs by 2035 — but this requires roughly a 25x increase in battery-recycling (material recovery) capacity versus 2023 levels.
- European rail: 11 different signalling systems, 3 different track gauges, and 5 major electrification types across the EU are cited as the core interoperability barriers.
- Steel, aluminium and batteries account for 70–75% of EV production emissions, underpinning T&E's proposed single "environmental score" (see [Making EVs fit for the future](making-evs-fit-for-the-future.md)).

- T&E's 10 recommendations for the next European Commission to harmonise/simplify EU rules (without deregulating) to accelerate transport decarbonisation and strengthen the EU Single Market, responding to Enrico Letta's April 2024 single-market report: consolidate national climate plans into NECPs; harmonise sustainability-reporting KPIs (CSRD/Taxonomy/SFDR); create a single EU funding rulebook (replacing IPCEIs with Innovation Fund money); standardise grid-connection rules and timelines for EV chargers; apply consistent RFNBO/hydrogen certification EU-wide; introduce binding 100%-ZEV purchase targets for large corporate car/van fleets by 2030 and truck fleets by 2040; merge vehicle carbon-footprint rules into one "environmental score" and standardise the EU car label; build a single market for battery/material recycling; align due-diligence reporting (CSDDD, EU Battery Regulation, national laws); and empower the European Railway Agency to fast-track rail interoperability and ticketing harmonisation.
