# EU sets historic target to reduce emissions by 2040 but transport sector puts target at risk

*Press release · Brussels · 2024-02-06*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/eu-sets-historic-target-to-reduce-emissions-by-2040-but-transport-sector-puts-target-at-risk)

> New 90% target gives European carmakers, airlines and shipping companies the investment certainty that clean technology is here to stay, whilst Europe can sigh a breath of relief at the prospect of becoming energy independent.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The Commission's 2040 communication targets a 90% EU emissions cut but still projects 46 million tonnes of oil equivalent (Mtoe) of "unabated" oil and gas use in 2050 — enough, T&E calculates, to power 900,000 transatlantic flights and add 140 million tonnes of needless CO2.
- The Commission projects 60% of cars will be electrified by 2040.
- More than 60% of the EU's aviation climate impact (extra-EU emissions plus non-CO2 warming effects) is excluded from the scope of the new 2040 target, based on the 2023 intra-EU/extra-EU emissions split.

- This February 2024 T&E press release welcomes the European Commission's newly announced 90%-emissions-cut-by-2040 target as historic industrial-policy signal, while criticising it for failing to mandate a fossil-fuel phase-out despite the EU's own role in securing "transition away from fossil fuels" language at COP28.
- On cars, welcomes the 60%-electrification-by-2040 projection but argues it requires additional policy beyond existing CO2 standards — specifically a binding 100%-electric mandate for new corporate cars/vans by 2030, combustion-car scrappage schemes, and ICE-to-EV retrofitting — rather than diverting the existing fleet to expensive e-fuels/biofuels (which T&E argues should instead be reserved for aviation/shipping).
- On shipping, welcomes the inclusion of international shipping emissions in scope (required under the EU Climate Law, and coming 9 years after the Paris Agreement flagged national responsibility for shipping/aviation emissions) and the recommendation to regulate small-vessel emissions, but says follow-up policy is still needed.
- On aviation, criticises the target's scope for ignoring extra-EU emissions and non-CO2 effects — arguing the target doesn't even match the ambition of the existing ReFuelEU Aviation regulation (which already covers all departing flights) — while welcoming a stated intention to increase ReFuelEU's ambition further.
