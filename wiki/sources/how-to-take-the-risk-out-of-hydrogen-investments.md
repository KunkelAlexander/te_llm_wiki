# How to take the risk out of hydrogen investments

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2025-02-03*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-to-take-the-risk-out-of-hydrogen-investments)

> 'Hydrogen's moment is here at last' proclaimed the Economist back in 2021. Today, the bubble has burst.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- T&E had identified 56 e-kerosene projects for aviation and 61 e-fuel projects for shipping in development in Europe, but only a fraction have reached final investment decision (FID) — and even FID-secured projects can still be cancelled (e.g. Ørsted's Flagship One).
- The EU Hydrogen Bank's first auction round selected 7 projects for a total of €720 million, with winning subsidies averaging only ~€0.50/kg of hydrogen — far below the roughly €6/kg price expected in Spain (one of the best-positioned countries) by 2030, suggesting bids were unrealistically low ("race to the bottom").
- The EU's shipping and aviation carbon markets are together likely to raise over €10 billion/year, mostly held by national governments rather than earmarked at EU level for clean fuels.
- Germany's H2Global initiative has attracted an additional €300 million in Dutch funding and is opening its double-auction tendering model to European projects.

- Op-ed by T&E's Executive Director William Todts arguing hydrogen remains indispensable for aviation, shipping and fertilisers (where electrification isn't possible and bioenergy isn't scalable) even as "electrification first" should be the EU's broader energy priority, and that green hydrogen costs will stay higher for longer than earlier optimistic (e.g. von der Leyen's 2021 "€2/kg") projections suggested.
- Diagnoses the EU Hydrogen Bank's core flaw as an offtake-risk problem, not just a cost problem: shipping/airline fuel purchasing runs on short-term spot contracts, and (outside aviation's e-kerosene quota) there's no binding mandate forcing buyers to commit long-term, so low fixed-subsidy bids don't translate into bankable projects.
- Proposes replacing/upgrading the Hydrogen Bank with a "double-auction" model like Germany's H2Global: one auction guarantees long-term offtake to a producer (giving it confidence to raise capital), a second auction later resells the fuel to end-use industries — with the goal of making multi-billion-euro investments less risky, not eliminating the green premium taxpayers/consumers ultimately must still absorb.
- Recommends using EU/national shipping and aviation ETS revenue to fund a "Hydrogen Bank 2.0" along these lines, and calls for binding fuel-supply quotas (as ReFuelEU already does for aviation) to reduce reliance on subsidy alone.
