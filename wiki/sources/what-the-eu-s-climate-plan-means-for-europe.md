# What the EU's climate plan means for Europe

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2021-07-15*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/what-the-eus-climate-plan-means-for-europe)

> July has been a big month for Europe and not just because of the Euro Championships. After two years of excruciatingly hard work the Commission has published its epochal new climate package. The EU's ambition is unmistakable. Its goal is to slash emissions by 55% compared to 1990 in the next nine years. To achieve this, the Commission proposes 12 new laws including a carbon border tax, a revamped carbon market for industry, as well as ambitious and binding new targets for renewable energy and national climate action.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- New car CO2 standards: -55% by 2030 and -100% (i.e. all new cars zero-emission) by 2035.
- Proposed road-transport carbon price (separate, "toned down" ETS) targeted at around €20/tonne, estimated to raise fuel prices by about 5 cents/litre by 2026-28.

- This T&E opinion piece (William Todts) gives an overall assessment of the EU's July 2021 "Fit for 55" package, scoring it as "a good result" but incomplete, with the heavy lifting placed on auto, oil, aviation and shipping industries rather than citizens directly — which T&E frames as fairer and more effective.
- On cars: welcomes the 55%-by-2030/100%-by-2035 CO2 targets as a "poster child" of the approach, while criticising the continued crediting of plug-in hybrids, the 2030 target being too low, and the lack of interim targets before 2030.
- On aviation: welcomes the proposed kerosene tax (intra-EU) and stronger aviation ETS as a small but important step, and calls the ReFuelEU blending mandate "the world's first serious attempt to require fuel shift in the aviation industry" despite over-reliance on biofuels and insufficient e-kerosene safeguards.
- On shipping: welcomes the first-ever cap-and-carbon-market coverage of shipping emissions as insufficient for Paris-Agreement-compliant shipping but still "the best news so far," while strongly criticising the accompanying FuelEU Maritime proposal for promoting fossil LNG infrastructure at ports rather than genuinely clean fuels like ammonia.
- On the Renewable Energy Directive: criticises it for retaining support for unsustainable crop biofuels (palm, soy), discriminating against electricity, and setting difficult-to-achieve advanced-biofuel/hydrogen blending requirements — though notes the Netherlands, Germany, France and Belgium's own reformed green-fuel laws (favouring electricity) make it likely they won't accept the RED proposal without major changes.
- On road transport carbon pricing: confirms the Commission dropped its plan to fold road transport into the main EU ETS, instead proposing a separate, deliberately modest road/buildings carbon price (~€20/tonne, ~5 cents/litre fuel-price impact by 2026-28) — still politically controversial despite its limited scale.
