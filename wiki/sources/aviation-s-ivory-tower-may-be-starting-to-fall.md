# Aviation's ivory tower may be starting to fall

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2022-12-16*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/aviations-ivory-tower-may-be-starting-to-fall)

> How much progress was made in 2022 to clean up flying?

<!-- ENRICHED SUMMARY: hand-written, preserved across regenerations by 1_build_wiki_subset.py -->

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Long-haul flights represent 60% of all EU aviation emissions, yet remain excluded from the EU ETS (limited to intra-EEA flights) under the December 2022 aviation deal.
- France's ban on three short-haul domestic routes (Paris-Orly to Bordeaux, Nantes and Lyon) affects just 0.3% of emissions from flights departing mainland France (3% of domestic emissions) — a largely symbolic but precedent-setting measure, per T&E's own calculations.
- Non-CO2 effects account for two-thirds of aviation's total climate impact and will be monitored from 2025 under the year's regulatory agreements.

- This December 2022 T&E op-ed reviews a year of incremental progress chipping away at what it calls aviation's historic regulatory "impunity," while stressing the sector's largest emissions source (long-haul flights) remains untouched.
- Cites several 2022 milestones as evidence the industry's "ivory tower" is cracking: public backlash and proposed regulation of private jets (following Kylie Jenner's viral 17-minute flight); France's short-haul flight ban; the EU's first-ever green jet fuel mandate (ReFuelEU Aviation); some airlines and NGOs jointly pushing for strict SAF sustainability criteria; and a first-ever move toward regulating non-CO2 effects.
- Argues the biggest structural failure remains long-haul flights' exclusion from carbon pricing, attributing this to EU governments continuing to defer to ICAO/CORSIA — a scheme T&E calls "cheap and dubious" and ineffective (see [UN body ICAO hails empty goal and cheap offsetting scheme to 'green' aviation](un-body-icao-hails-empty-goal-and-cheap-offsetting-scheme-to-green-aviation.md)).
- Frames 2023 as a "post-Fit for 55" period where continued industry resistance to addressing its full pollution footprint risks the sector losing its "social license to operate."
