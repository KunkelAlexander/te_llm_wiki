# The return of the Climate Chancellor?

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2020-12-19*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/return-climate-chancellor)

> Angela Merkel has long been known as the Climate Chancellor for her leading role in the fight against climate change. But in recent years German climate leadership was sorely lacking. Whether in the domestic discussion on a coal phase-out, European discussions on higher climate ambition or indeed car CO2 standards, Germany had its foot on the break. Even though Merkel has grudgingly lent her support to the EU Green Deal, the country's response to the crisis was to prove a climate leadership litmus test, not just for Germany but also for Merkel who is about to step down. Will she be remembered as the Auto Chancellor or the Climate Chancellor?

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- 11% of new cars sold in Germany that year were electric, which the piece attributes to purchase subsidies ("Kaufprämien," restricted to zero-emission and plug-in hybrid models) and binding 2020 car CO2 targets.
- Tesla began building its Gigafactory in Berlin, planned to produce 500,000 EVs/year.
- Germany plans a binding e-kerosene blending target for aviation, part of its hydrogen strategy.

*(Otherwise largely a qualitative opinion piece — most of its content is narrative/political commentary rather than quantified findings.)*

- This T&E opinion piece (December 2020) argues Germany's COVID-19 recovery choices marked a turning point in its climate credibility: Chancellor Merkel backed EU fiscal stimulus over austerity, and — despite auto-lobby (VDA) pressure for a purchase subsidy covering combustion-engine cars — the government limited purchase subsidies to zero-emission and plug-in hybrid vehicles, a decision T&E frames as a "massive blow" to the German car lobby's post-Dieselgate credibility.
- T&E credits this decision, plus binding 2020 car CO2 targets, with accelerating German EV adoption and drawing large battery-manufacturing investment (including Tesla's Berlin Gigafactory) to Germany.
- T&E also welcomes Germany's finalised coal phase-out and its hydrogen strategy (including a planned binding e-kerosene blending target for aviation, framed as helping decarbonise heavy industry, shipping and aviation).
- The piece frames the upcoming revision of car CO2 standards and Germany's 2021 federal election as the tests of whether Merkel will be remembered as the "Auto Chancellor" who long protected combustion-engine industry, or the "Climate Chancellor" who helped end both coal and combustion engines.
