# On Thin Ice: Norway's Fossil Ambitions and the EU's Green Energy Future

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- In a scenario where the EU meets its climate pledges (IEA Announced Pledges, 1.7°C), gas supply from EU/Norway/Algeria's existing fields plus already-contracted supply exceeds EU demand by 2035; in DNV's 1.5°C-aligned Pathway to Net Zero scenario, existing supply/contracts could meet all EU gas demand by the early 2030s, letting the EU end Norwegian gas imports entirely by 2035 when current contracts expire.
- Norway has issued 141 exploration licences in the Arctic Barents Sea since 2010, but Barents Sea production to date remains small, with an average 17-year lag from discovery to first production and peak production not expected before 2040 — too late to help the current European energy crisis.
- EU LNG import capacity is set to rise 78% by 2030 versus 2021 levels (having already risen 40% between 2021 and 2023), a build-out the report argues risks locking in fossil-gas overcapacity as demand structurally declines.
- Norwegian oil production is projected to peak in 2025 and decline 66% by 2050 versus 2021 levels even accounting for new field development; existing Norwegian gas fields' output is set to decline 32% by 2030 and 98% by 2050 without new exploration.
- Green hydrogen is forecast to become cheaper than Norwegian blue (gas-plus-CCS) hydrogen by 2028, with BloombergNEF projecting German green hydrogen 24-45% cheaper than blue by 2030 — undermining the economic case for Norway's push to export blue hydrogen to the EU.

- This February 2024 report — co-published by Oil Change International, T&E, Zero Carbon Analytics, WWF Norway and Greenpeace Norway — models EU oil and gas supply/demand under three scenarios (IEA Stated Policies, IEA Announced Pledges, DNV Pathway to Net Zero) to assess whether new Norwegian (including Arctic) oil and gas exploration is actually needed to meet future EU demand.
- Central finding: under any scenario where the EU meets its stated climate commitments, no new oil or gas supply — from Norway or elsewhere — is needed; expanding production instead risks stranded assets and locks in emissions incompatible with 1.5°C, while Norway's own Arctic exploration in the Barents Sea has so far proven costly and underwhelming.
- Also debunks the case for carbon capture and storage (CCS) sustaining higher EU gas demand, noting that even the Announced Pledges scenario would require a nearly tenfold increase in current global CCS capacity by 2030 (441 Mt CO2 captured) — a scale industry is nowhere near track to deliver.
- Policy recommendations: the EU should resist Norway's push for further Arctic exploration and avoid signing new long-term gas supply contracts; Norway should halt new oil/gas field licensing and develop a phase-out plan; and the US should make permanent its pause on new LNG export terminal approvals given falling EU gas demand.
