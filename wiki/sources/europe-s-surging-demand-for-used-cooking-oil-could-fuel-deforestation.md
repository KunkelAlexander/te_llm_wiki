# Europe's surging demand for used cooking oil could fuel deforestation

*Report · Brussels · 2021-04-19*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/europes-surging-demand-used-cooking-oil-could-fuel-deforestation) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/CE_Delft__200247_UCO_as_biofuel_feedstock_in_EU_FINAL20-20v5_0.pdf)

> The EU promotes used cooking oil under its green fuels law which is pushing up demand for the 'waste' product. And although Europe can increase the amount of UCO that can be sourced locally, this is limited by both the capacity of local authorities to collect it and how much used cooking oil Europeans and EU industries can produce. This means it is increasingly reliant on dubious imports. According to the study carried out on behalf CE Delft on behalf of T&E, the demand for UCO could double in 2030.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Current (2019) EU+UK Used Cooking Oil-based biodiesel (UCOME) consumption is ~2.85 Mt, 19% of total EU biodiesel consumption — but concentrated in only ~10 member states (Germany, Ireland, Hungary, Netherlands, Portugal, UK, Slovenia, Bulgaria and others), several with 80%+ UCOME shares, generally where double-counting or other policy incentives exist.
- About half of UCO used for EU biodiesel was imported in 2018, rising to more than half in 2019 (main origins: China, Indonesia, Malaysia, Russia, US, Saudi Arabia); total biodiesel net imports reached 3.2 Mt in 2019 (Argentina, China, Indonesia, Malaysia as main sources), with Netherlands, Spain and Belgium handling 97% of those imports.
- Current EU+UK UCO collection is estimated at 0.7–1.2 Mt/year, plus ~1.4 Mt/year imported, giving 2.1–2.6 Mt/year total supply — already below current 2.8 Mt UCOME consumption, illustrating existing data uncertainty/potential fraud.
- Total EU+UK UCO/UCOME supply potential (domestic + imports) is estimated at 3.1–3.3 Mt/year, of which 30–50% would need to come from outside the EU.
- Projected 2030 EU+UK UCOME demand could reach 6.1–6.4 Mt/year under a high-demand scenario (UCOME hitting the RED II's 1.7% transport-fuel cap in every member state) — roughly double current consumption and significantly exceeding the estimated 3.1–3.3 Mt/year supply potential; about 17–19% (1.0–1.1 Mt) of that demand would be for EU aviation.
- If the EU's UCOME share of renewable transport fuels were replicated globally in 2030, global UCO/UCOME demand would reach 27–37 Mt/year — some literature cites a much higher (up to 34 Mt/year) global supply potential, but without clearly specifying underlying assumptions or competing-use adjustments.
- UCOME traded at a 40–65% price premium over virgin-oil-based FAME biodiesel in North-West Europe (end 2019/early 2020), an incentive effect compounded by double-counting policies that increases fraud risk (e.g. "laundering" virgin oil as UCO).

- This December 2020 CE Delft study, commissioned by T&E, examines the EU's growing reliance on Used Cooking Oil (UCO) as a biodiesel/biofuel feedstock — driven partly by RED II incentives and expected further growth from aviation/shipping alternative-fuel policies — and assesses supply, demand, and fraud/verification risks.
- Central finding: projected EU demand for UCO/UCOME will likely far outstrip sustainably available EU+import supply by 2030, creating strong incentives for fraud (declaring virgin oil as "used" oil, or artificially inflating UCO output) given the current lack of a reliable, cheap, accurate detection method and a non-transparent certification process.
- T&E's framing (via its accompanying article) is that this dynamic — genuine sustainable UCO supply already falling short of demand even before EU policy pushes it further — risks indirectly displacing UCO's other existing (non-fuel) uses and incentivising it to be substituted with less scrupulously-sourced material, with a deforestation risk if that substitution runs through virgin vegetable oils like palm oil.
- The study recommends stronger monitoring, verification, and certification reforms specifically for UCO given its outsized fraud incentives relative to other RED-eligible feedstocks.
