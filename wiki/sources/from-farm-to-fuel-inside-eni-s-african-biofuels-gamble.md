# From Farm to Fuel: inside Eni's African biofuels gamble

*Report · Brussels · 2024-02-24*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/from-farm-to-fuel-inside-enis-african-biofuels-gamble) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2024_From-Farm-to-Fuel_min.pdf)

> An in-depth look behind the scenes of Eni's new green strategy: developing new agricultural production channels from scratch in six African countries to supply its refineries and the emerging 'green' aviation market. All against the backdrop of the Meloni Government's new influence strategy in Africa, the Mattei Plan, which gives pride of place to energy deals

## Summary

*This is T&E's full investigative report; see the companion press release,
[Uncovered: Italian oil giant's African biofuels gamble falls short](uncovered-italian-oil-giant-s-african-biofuels-gamble-falls-short.md), for the shorter announcement version of the same investigation.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- In Kenya, Eni exported just 24.5% of its 2023 production target (30,000 tonnes) — customs data shows 7,348 tonnes of castor oil shipped from Kenya to Italy between January–November 2023, in two shipments (July and August only).
- Eni's Kenya target of 200,000 tonnes/year by 2026 would require a 27-fold increase over the volume actually shipped in 2023.
- Eni claims to have enrolled 80,000 farmers over 50,000 hectares in Kenya via four recruitment campaigns (11,000 → 15,000 → 22,000 → 34,000 farmers), but T&E could not verify these figures; government records in Nakuru county showed only ~700 contracted farmers in 2022, and the two main Makueni cooperatives reported 80 and 1,000 farmers respectively.
- Prices paid to Kenyan farmers: KSh25/kg for shelled castor beans and KSh7/kg for croton nuts in 2022, rising to KSh35/kg for castor in 2023 (a farmer cooperative leader said it would need to more than double, to KSh80/kg, to be worthwhile). At KSh25/kg, a 10kg crop of castor beans earned a farmer about €1.42.
- Individual farmer examples: one earned KShs300 (~€1.70) from castor after two harvests of beans worth KShs72,000 (~€420) on the same land; another spent KShs5,000 (~€30) just to uproot unproductive castor stumps.
- In the Republic of Congo, Eni's targets are 170,000 tonnes/year by 2026 rising to 200,000 tonnes/year by 2030, via an agri-hub in Loudima (30,000 tonnes/year capacity); as of the report, the project had not moved beyond pilot trials on 300 hectares (Agri Resources) and 800 hectares (Tolona), and no vegetable oil had yet been shipped from Congo to Italy.
- Land concessions in Congo: Agri Resources holds 22,000 hectares (220 km²) and Tolona holds 20,000 hectares (200km²) — combined, larger than Basel and Liverpool combined. A 2022 legal settlement saw Agri Resources agree to compensate 57 displaced farmers a total of 15 million CFA francs (~€20,000).
- Eni's 2023-26 capital budget earmarks €25bn for oil and gas exploration/development versus less than €3.4bn for biofuels.
- Eni aims to produce over 1 million tonnes/year of sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) by 2030 out of 5 million tonnes total biorefining capacity; T&E estimates that if all of this SAF went to the European market it would meet 46% of European aviation's SAF mandate requirement under a business-as-usual traffic growth scenario, and about 7% of IATA's global 2030 SAF target.
- The IFC (World Bank) was considering a $210m direct loan to Eni Kenya (plus underwriting a further $60m loan portfolio) at time of publication; no decision had been made.

**T&E's argument and context:**
- Eni, one of the world's biggest oil and gas producers, has made biofuels — grown on "drought-resistant," "non-food-competing" crops like castor, croton and cotton — central to its net-zero-by-2050 strategy, aiming to be a global leader in "sustainable fuels" by 2035 with a fivefold increase in biofuel production.
- The investigation (field visits and farmer interviews in Kenya and Congo, plus customs/company-disclosure analysis) finds Eni significantly under-delivering in both countries, for different structural reasons: in Kenya, a fragmented network of brokers/cooperatives leaves smallholders under-supported and underpaid, compounded by the worst drought in 40 years; in Congo, reliance on large agribusiness concessions has produced land-expropriation disputes and stalled pilot trials.
- T&E frames this as evidence that scaling biofuels from African agriculture to the volumes needed for meaningful transport/aviation emissions cuts is not credible at the pace or scale claimed, and questions why public development finance (IFC/World Bank) should subsidise a highly profitable oil major's own strategy.
- The report sits within T&E's broader scepticism of biofuel feedstock scalability and its concern that biofuels marketed as "guilt-free" aviation solutions crowd out more urgent demand-reduction measures (flying less). See [Biofuels](../topics/biofuels.md).
