# Running a truck on e-diesel costs 47% more than its battery-electric counterpart

*Press release · Brussels · 2022-11-15*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/running-a-truck-on-e-diesel-costs-47-more-than-its-battery-electric-counterpart) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202211_trucks_BET_vs_e-fuels-1.pdf)

> Expensive, scarce and less green - e-fuels for trucks will slow down the transition to zero-emission road transport, a new study by NGO Transport & Environment (T&E) shows.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- A new diesel truck bought in 2035 and fuelled entirely with e-diesel would cost 47% more in total cost of ownership than an equivalent battery-electric truck (BET); e-diesel itself is projected to cost 52% more than fossil diesel in 2035.
- A BET charged with average EU grid electricity in 2035 cuts lifecycle GHG emissions by 86% versus a fossil diesel truck; a RED II-compliant e-diesel truck cuts emissions by only 60% — meaning the e-diesel truck would still emit almost 3x as much GHG over its lifetime as the equivalent BET. Even under a 100%-renewable-electricity best case for both, e-diesel trucks would still emit 41% more than BETs.
- If all EU e-fuel production (per Concawe's industry modelling: 9 Mtoe by 2035, two-thirds allocated to road transport) were diverted from trucks to aviation and shipping instead, it could meet about 13% of 2035 aviation energy demand and 6% of shipping energy demand — enough to put both hard-to-abate sectors on track for net-zero by 2050.
- Overall efficiency of power-to-liquid (e-fuel) in trucks is just 23% (29% by 2050) versus 77% (81% by 2050) for direct electrification.

- This November 2022 T&E report/press release argues against oil- and automotive-supplier lobbying to include CO2 credits for e-fuels and biofuels in the EU's truck CO2 standards review (due December 2022), comparing e-diesel and battery-electric trucks (BETs) head-to-head on cost and lifecycle emissions for trucks purchased in 2035.
- Central argument: e-fuels make no economic or environmental sense in trucks given a mature, cheaper, cleaner zero-emission alternative (BETs) already exists, whereas aviation and shipping have no such alternative and urgently need the same scarce green hydrogen/e-fuel supply.
- Warns that introducing a fuel-crediting mechanism into truck CO2 standards (letting truckmakers buy e-fuel credits to offset tailpipe emissions) would create market uncertainty, slow BET investment, and be difficult to enforce, since truckmakers cannot control how their vehicles are actually fuelled after sale.
- Also flags that "sustainable" biofuel volume claims by the fuel industry (FuelsEurope, citing Concawe-commissioned research) rely on unsustainable practices (e.g. cutting down trees, harvesting stumps) and feedstocks with existing competing uses (agricultural residues, used cooking oil), meaning genuinely sustainable biofuel supply is far more limited than claimed.
- Concludes T&E urges the European Commission not to propose any fuel-crediting mechanism in its revision of the heavy-duty vehicle CO2 standards — see [A drop of e-fuel in an ocean of oil](a-drop-of-e-fuel-in-an-ocean-of-oil.md) for T&E's parallel case against e-fuel carve-outs in car CO2 standards.
