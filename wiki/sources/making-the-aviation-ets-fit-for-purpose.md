# Making the aviation ETS fit for purpose

*Report · Brussels · 2020-11-29*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/making-aviation-ets-fit-purpose) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2020_11_Oko_Institute_analysis_potential_reforms_aviation_inclusion_ETS.pdf)

> As the European Green Deal commits Europe to revising the ETS rules for aviation but also for the stationary ETS, T&E publishes new analysis from the Öko-Institute assessing potential options to strengthen the ambition of the scheme to incentivise aviation to decarbonise.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Airlines had received about €33 billion in COVID-19 financial relief so far in 2020, without binding green conditions.
- Abolishing free ETS allowances for aviation is estimated to raise about €1 billion/year (at current CO2 prices and cap levels) for a dedicated decarbonisation/e-fuels fund, modelled on the stationary ETS's existing Modernisation and Innovation Funds.
- Around 50% of surrendered aviation-sector allowances already come from the stationary ETS market (EUAs) rather than dedicated aviation allowances (EUAAs); pre-COVID projections had this share rising to ~70% by 2030.
- Raising the aviation Linear Reduction Factor (LRF) from 2.2% to 3.8% would only reduce the overall EU ETS allowance supply by about 0.2% — described as a comparatively weak lever next to removing free allocation entirely.
- In 2019, ETS allowances traded at ~€25/tonne on average; airlines received about 32 million tonnes of allowances free, worth an estimated €900 million — against an estimated €27 billion/year jet-fuel tax exemption if applied to all European outbound flights.
- A proposed CO2 "discounting factor" of 5 (to compensate for the untaxed kerosene, whose minimum tax rate of 33 cents/litre implies a CO2 price of €130/tonne) and a factor of ~2–3 (for aviation's non-CO2 warming effects, estimated at up to three times its CO2-only effect) are modelled; a factor of 5 alone is estimated to raise ticket prices by roughly 15–30% if fully passed through.
- The study proposes rebasing the stationary ETS cap to align with a 55–60% economy-wide 2030 GHG reduction target (vs. the current cap's implied ~43% cut vs. 2005), which would require the ETS sector itself to cut emissions by roughly 60–65% below 2005 levels.
- The Market Stability Reserve's intake rate (which absorbs surplus allowances) is proposed to rise to at least 24% to maintain price resilience as emissions continue falling.

- This December 2020 T&E report presents new Öko-Institut analysis of options to strengthen the EU ETS for aviation, commissioned as the Commission prepared to revise both the aviation and stationary ETS under the European Green Deal.
- Central finding: at current carbon prices, the ETS provides "little to no incentive" for airlines or manufacturers to begin the transformation needed for clean aviation technologies and fuels — meaning that even the reform options considered, unless adopted at "the highest level of environmental ambition," would not deliver aviation decarbonisation on their own.
- The report evaluates two hard-cap mechanisms — limiting airlines' ability to buy allowances from the stationary market, and further reducing the aviation-specific cap/Linear Reduction Factor — concluding that, given the current dominance of stationary-market allowance purchases, only removing free allocation and/or limiting stationary-EUA purchases delivers a meaningful cap.
- On free allocation, the study finds no carbon-leakage justification survives now that the aviation ETS covers only intra-EU flights (since a passenger flight, unlike industrial production, cannot simply relocate to a third country), and argues abolishing free allocation is administratively straightforward given airlines already buy roughly half their required allowances.
- The report also examines a possible carbon border adjustment "ticket tax" on flights outside ETS scope (to counter perceived unfair competition/leakage risk, e.g. stop-over routings via non-EU hubs), but concludes current carbon prices make this unnecessary and politically complex to implement.
- T&E's own policy recommendations, reiterated from its companion papers: remove free allowances and fund SAF/e-fuel deployment with the revenue; apply discounting factors for non-CO2 effects and undertaxed kerosene; reduce the cap and limit stationary-allowance purchases; set a minimum allowance price; allow voluntary allowance cancellation for reduced demand; reject any weakening of the ETS via CORSIA integration; and introduce kerosene taxation and SAF mandates alongside ETS reform.
- See [Revision of the EU ETS for aviation](revision-of-the-eu-ets-for-aviation.md) and [The costs of EU ETS and Corsia for European aviation](the-costs-of-eu-ets-and-corsia-for-european-aviation.md) for the earlier T&E papers whose cost estimates and reform proposals this Öko-Institut study builds on and cites directly.
