# A streamlined EV 'eco-score' would encourage green, made-in-Europe electric cars

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2024-04-24*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/a-streamlined-ev-eco-score-would-encourage-green-made-in-europe-electric-cars)

> By setting up a framework to foster resource, climate and energy efficiency in EVs, the EU can reduce the impact of cars beyond tailpipe emissions

## Summary

*Op-ed version of the T&E/IMT(IDDRI)/BEUC briefing [Making EVs fit for the future](making-evs-fit-for-the-future.md), by Lucien Mathieu (T&E), Jean-Philippe Hermine (IMT) and Robin Loos (BEUC) — see that page for the full methodology and worked-example figures.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Compared to the Dacia Spring, the Audi Q8 e-tron carries 4x more battery, consumes twice the energy per km, and weighs an extra 2 tonnes.
- 50–60% of EV lifecycle emissions come from production, versus ~10% for combustion cars; SUVs are 54% of new EU car sales.
- Battery/steel/aluminium together make up 70–75% of an EV's embedded carbon footprint.
- Example efficiency mismatch: Tesla Model 3 Long Range (78 kWh battery) uses 14 kWh/100km, while the Ford Mustang Mach-E (75 kWh, similar size) uses 19.6 kWh/100km; the Mercedes-Benz Vision EQXX (100 kWh, 1,800kg, 5m long) achieves 8.3 kWh/100km, showing weight/size correlate weakly with efficiency for EVs.
- Illustrative eco-scores (lower = better): small EU-built EV = 726; same model built in China = 1,242 (~70% worse); large EU-built EV = 1,324; same large model built in China = 2,209.

- Argues the EU's 2023 car CO2 law treats all EVs as equally "zero emission," creating a regulatory gap that national rules (e.g. France's eco-bonus) are filling inconsistently.
- Proposes an EU "eco-score" combining energy efficiency (kWh/km) with the combined carbon footprint of battery, steel and aluminium (kgCO2e), using data already required under the EU Batteries Regulation and CBAM.
- Argues this is preferable to both an efficiency-only threshold (which favours longer, more aerodynamic, more expensive cars) and full lifecycle-analysis (too complex to apply per model).
- Frames the score as pro-consumer (favours smaller, more affordable EVs) and pro-industry (rewards EU-made batteries/steel/aluminium, which are less carbon-intensive than Chinese equivalents).
