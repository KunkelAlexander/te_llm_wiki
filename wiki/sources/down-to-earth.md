# Down to earth

*Report · Brussels · 2025-01-13*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/down-to-earth)

> Why European aviation needs to urgently address its growth problem

## Summary

*See the companion press release, [Aviation industry plans for growth 'irreconcilable' with Europe's climate goals](aviation-industry-plans-for-growth-irreconcilable-with-europe-s-climate-goals.md), whose scraped fulltext happens to contain this report's full body — used as the source for the figures below.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Airbus/Boeing's own market outlooks imply EU passenger traffic more than doubles between 2019 and 2050 (T&E's "Industry High Growth Scenario," averaging ~3.3%/year to 2050).
- Despite efficiency gains, EU-departing flights would burn 59% more fuel in 2050 than in 2019 under this growth path; by 2049 the sector could burn as much fossil kerosene as in 2023, even while meeting ReFuelEU's 42% SAF mandate.
- European aviation emissions would fall by less than 3% by 2049 versus 2019 under this scenario — and would deplete Europe's 1.5°C-aligned aviation carbon budget as soon as 2026 (2033 for 1.7°C, 2043 for 2.0°C).
- By 2050, meeting ReFuelEU's 35% e-kerosene blend mandate under this growth scenario would require 585 TWh of renewable electricity — more than Germany's entire 2023 electricity demand (506 TWh), and about 10% of all EU wind/solar output projected for 2050.
- From 2035 onward, biofuel demand would exceed genuinely sustainable feedstock supply; by 2050, T&E estimates 4 of every 5 litres of SAF supplied could come from unsustainable feedstocks, putting up to 385 Mt of assumed CO2 savings (2025-2050) at risk.
- Even the European Commission's own more conservative 2040 modelling (60% lower traffic growth than Airbus/Boeing) would still see aviation emissions rise 46-64% versus 1990, while the rest of the economy is supposed to cut emissions 90% — and if industry growth projections materialise instead, 960 Mt more CO2 could be emitted than the Commission's 2040 scenario plans for.
- Cited examples of airport expansion risk: Madrid Barajas expansion could increase flights 21% (to 500,000+/year by 2030) and CO2 by 35%; Barcelona airport expansion could raise CO2 by 33%; Amsterdam Schiphol's own commissioned study found it would need to cap flights at 460,000 (a 7% emissions cut) to meet climate goals, but political pressure raised the actual 2025 cap to 478,000.

- T&E's core argument: SAF/e-kerosene uptake alone cannot offset industry-projected traffic growth, so growth itself must be constrained — via ending airport expansion, removing aviation's fossil-fuel subsidies (untaxed jet fuel, VAT exemptions) and ETS exemptions, prioritising e-kerosene (not biofuels) in public funding, and strengthening rail as a substitute for short-haul flights.
