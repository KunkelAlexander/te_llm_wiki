# Why airline net zero commitments aren't green at all

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2021-10-28*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/why-airline-net-zero-commitments-arent-green-at-all)

> Airlines know they need to ditch polluting jet fuel, but relying on offsets is not the answer

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- IATA committed in 2010 to halving aviation emissions by 2050 and using 10% SAF by 2017; twelve years later, sector emissions are still expected to triple by 2050 and EU SAF use is estimated at just 0.05% of total jet fuel consumption.

- This T&E opinion piece argues the gap between airlines' net-zero rhetoric and their actual lobbying record shows the industry cannot be trusted to self-regulate, and that binding legislation — not voluntary pledges — is what's needed.
- T&E accuses IATA of using ICAO's CORSIA offsetting scheme as a shield: pushing the EU to replace its own carbon market with CORSIA, opposing national climate measures, ticket taxes and fuel mandates, while simultaneously benefiting from being one of the only sectors not paying fuel tax in Europe.
- T&E argues offsetting lets airlines pursue "minimum effort, maximum reward" — clearing their "climate conscience" cheaply rather than actually cutting emissions — and criticises IATA for both claiming commitment to SAF and lobbying against the EU blending mandates that would actually drive SAF production.
- T&E draws a direct parallel to the car industry, arguing binding EU vehicle CO2 standards — not voluntary commitments — are what actually drove carmakers to commit billions to electric vehicles, and calls for similarly binding aviation regulation.
- See [Aviation's climate pledges contradicted by huge growth forecasts](aviation-s-climate-pledges-contradicted-by-huge-growth-forecasts.md) for T&E's companion critique of the same underlying IATA net-zero commitment, and [Making aviation fuel mandates sustainable](making-aviation-fuel-mandates-sustainable.md) for T&E's detailed case for binding SAF mandates over industry self-regulation.
