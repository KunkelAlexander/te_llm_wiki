## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- E-petrol is projected to cost at least €2.80/litre in Germany in 2030 versus €1.84/litre for fossil petrol today — 50% more expensive — meaning roughly €210 to fill a 75-litre tank and about €2,300/year for an average driver, versus about €140 today.
- If e-petrol were used in new cars instead of the existing fleet, this would displace 26-46 million cumulative battery-electric-vehicle (BEV) sales by 2050 with no additional CO2 savings, while the existing fleet would burn an extra 135 billion litres of fossil petrol and emit an extra 320 MtCO2e by 2050.
- Germany will need to import 72% of its projected 90-110 TWh 2030 hydrogen demand (with only ~28 TWh from its planned 10 GW domestic electrolysis capacity) — even before any additional demand from cars running on e-fuels is counted.
- If 46 million new combustion/hybrid cars were sold by 2050 and fuelled with pure e-petrol, they would still emit 160,000 tonnes of NOx — more than Italy's entire 2019 vehicle fleet.

- This March 2023 T&E France press release/briefing rebuts German Chancellor Scholz's push (backed by the FDP) to allow new combustion-engine car sales after the EU's 2035 phase-out date if they run on e-fuels, arguing this would cost drivers dearly, deliver no net climate benefit, worsen air pollution, and create new energy dependencies (e.g. on Saudi Arabia, a signed 2021 Germany-Saudi green hydrogen MOU).
- Central argument: e-fuels used in new cars would merely displace BEV sales at no climate benefit, whereas using the same scarce e-fuel supply in the existing car fleet instead could save an estimated 0.3 GtCO2 by 2050 (covering ~61% of existing-fleet fuel consumption, ~42 million cars) — making the existing fleet, not new-car sales, the economically and environmentally sound use case.
- Flags that no EU legal framework currently guarantees imported or EU-produced e-fuels are actually "carbon neutral" — citing the RED's grandfathering clause (allowing pre-2028 e-fuel plants to use non-additional renewables until 2038) and permitted use of fossil-source captured carbon until 2036-2041 — and proposes T&E's own phased DAC-carbon-feedstock mandate (10% by 2030 rising to 100% by 2050) as the fix.
- Warns the political uncertainty risks up to €30 billion in German battery-plant investment, citing carmaker (Audi, Volvo) statements that regulatory clarity on the 2035 phase-out is essential for their EV investment plans.
