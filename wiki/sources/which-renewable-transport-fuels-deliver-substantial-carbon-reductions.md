# Which renewable transport fuels deliver substantial carbon reductions?

*Consultation response · Brussels · 2020-09-22*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/which-renewable-transport-fuels-deliver-substantial-carbon-reductions) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/T_2024-04-29-112025_zmug.pdf)

> The European Commission is devising a methodology to assess which renewable liquid and gaseous transport fuels of non-biological origin (RFNBOs) deliver substantial carbon reductions. Here's T&E's views on the methodology.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Methane's Global Warming Potential: the JRC's proposed GWP100 value of 25 understates methane's short-term impact — T&E cites the IPCC AR5 range of methane being 84–86x more warming than CO2 over 20 years vs. 28–34x over 100 years, and calls for dual GWP20/GWP100 accounting.
- Direct Air Capture (DAC) cost estimates cited: roughly €145/tonne (declining toward 2030) in one estimate, vs. others putting current costs around €270/tonne falling to €165/tonne by 2030 — compared to Carbon Capture and Utilisation (CCU) from industrial point sources at roughly €30/tonne, a cost gap T&E says risks locking in fossil CO2 sources over DAC.
- The EU Innovation Fund was expected to have about €10 billion available (over its funding period, depending on ETS allowance prices) to invest across CCU, CCS, renewables, storage and industrial decarbonisation — with funding specifically for DAC described as uncertain.
- A cited T&E freight-decarbonisation study found ~2,800 full-load hours realistic for a megawatt-scale electrolyser (a ~30% load factor), giving a hydrogen production cost of €2.33–4.00/kg (excluding transport/distribution); North Sea offshore wind can already reach 3,600+ full-load hours/year.
- RED II's existing multiplier for renewable electricity supplied to road vehicles is 4x, used to incentivise member states to count electricity toward the binding 7% advanced renewable-fuels target.

- This is a July 2020 letter from T&E's Electricity & Energy Manager (Geert De Cock) responding to a JRC/European Commission stakeholder meeting on the GHG accounting methodology for Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBOs, e.g. e-kerosene, e-methanol, e-diesel, green hydrogen) and Recycled Carbon Fuels (RCFs) under RED II.
- T&E supports the JRC's general "major/minor/de minimis inputs" simplification approach to lifecycle GHG accounting but objects specifically to the proposed methane GWP value, arguing dual 20-year/100-year GWP accounting is needed to properly reflect short-lived climate pollutants like methane.
- On additionality: T&E distinguishes "elastic" electricity inputs (genuinely additional new renewable capacity, or otherwise-curtailed renewable electricity) — which alone should count as zero-emission — from "rigid" inputs (grid electricity diverted from other renewable claims), which should be assigned the carbon intensity of the national grid average including upstream and transmission losses. T&E flags Power Purchase Agreements and a proposed "Guarantee of Origin+" (GO+) mechanism as candidate ways to demonstrate additionality.
- On carbon sourcing for carbon-based RFNBOs (e-kerosene etc.), T&E insists Direct Air Capture is the only source fully compatible with EU net-zero-by-2050, and proposes phasing out fossil/industrial point-source CO2 (Carbon Capture and Utilisation, CCU) as an eligible carbon source by 2025, with pre-2025 CCU investments grandfathered, plus a rising minimum DAC-share requirement reaching 100% by 2050.
- T&E opposes allowing CCU from the power/heat sector specifically (arguing faster full decarbonisation options exist there) but is more open to point-source CO2 from industries like pulp/paper, steel and cement as a short/medium-term bridge, provided ETS allowance obligations for the CO2 source aren't circumvented ("CO2 laundering").
- T&E opposes counting biogenic CO2 from new facilities as an RFNBO carbon feedstock (citing land/feedstock constraints and double-counting risk with bioenergy carbon capture and storage, BECCS), with a narrow exception for existing plants already using sustainably-sourced waste/residue biomass.
- See [T&E's response to roadmap consultation on the inception impact assessment for the Revision of Directive (EU) 2018/2001](t-e-s-response-to-roadmap-consultation-on-the-inception-impact-assessment-for-th.md) for T&E's broader RED II revision positions (including on RFNBOs) from the same period, and [How EU legislation can drive an uptake of sustainable advanced fuels in aviation](how-eu-legislation-can-drive-an-uptake-of-sustainable-advanced-fuels-in-aviation.md) for the aviation-specific application of the additionality/DAC principles argued here.
