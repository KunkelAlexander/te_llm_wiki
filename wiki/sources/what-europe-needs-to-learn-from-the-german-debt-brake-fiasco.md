# What Europe needs to learn from the German debt brake fiasco

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2024-02-01*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/what-europe-needs-to-learn-from-the-german-debt-brake-fiasco)

> Financing the green transition and on-shoring a robust industrial base in Europe will require massive public and private investments. Germany's recent budget crisis forebodes a similar budget crisis at European level. It is high time to change track and develop an ambitious climate investment plan in Europe.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Germany's constitutional "debt brake" restricts the federal budget deficit to just 0.35% of GDP; the German Supreme Court's November 2023 ruling blocked a €60 billion transfer of unused Covid-era emergency funds to a fund backing EV battery/microchip investment.
- The EU's €700 billion Recovery and Resilience Facility funding is set to run dry in 2026, a cliff-edge the op-ed warns could trigger an EU-wide version of Germany's crisis.
- European leaders were poised (as of writing) to cut the proposed €10 billion STEP Platform green-industrial top-up fund to just €1.5 billion, redirecting the difference to defence spending.

- This February 2024 T&E op-ed uses Germany's constitutional debt-brake ruling — which forced emergency 2024 budget cuts including to EV subsidies and e-kerosene production support — as a warning sign for the EU's broader public-investment capacity as Green Deal-era emergency funding expires.
- Central argument: if the EU's fiscal rules (the Stability and Growth Pact) aren't reformed to accommodate green investment, a Germany-style simultaneous pullback across all EU member states could stall grids, rail, EV and building-renovation spending right when investment needs to accelerate.
- Proposes fiscal rules should require, not merely permit, governments to allocate a minimum GDP share (analogous to NATO's 2% defence-spending requirement) to the green transition, and calls for a long-term EU climate investment plan funded by common/joint EU debt issuance as the top priority for the incoming European Commission.
- Frames the climate challenge explicitly as "an investment challenge," warning that continued fiscal fragmentation between "frugal" states and larger economies pursuing national cleantech strategies (France, Germany) risks EU-wide competitiveness in the global clean-tech race.
