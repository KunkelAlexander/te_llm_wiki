# The easy fix to air pollution linked to planes

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2023-02-28*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/the-easy-fix-to-air-pollution-linked-to-planes)

> Jet fuel is a dirty liquid. But what if we could make it cleaner?

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Up to 33 million people living within 20km of Europe's top-20 airports face elevated risks of high blood pressure, heart attack and diabetes from aviation-linked ultrafine particle pollution.
- Hydrotreatment could cut fossil jet fuel's aromatic content from a current average of 15-20% down to the ASTM safety minimum of 8% — a 200%-scale reduction — without compromising engine/fuel-tank safety compatibility.
- Preliminary ICCT estimates (cited) suggest hydrotreated jet fuel would cost only about 5% more than conventional jet fuel, translating to less than a 1% increase in ticket prices.

- This February 2023 op-ed argues jet fuel quality (specifically its aromatic and sulphur content) is a fast, cheap, already-available fix for both aviation's non-CO2 climate effects (two-thirds of its total climate impact) and the serious local air-quality/health harms from ultrafine particle emissions near airports.
- Highlights that non-CO2 effects, unlike CO2, are short-lived in the atmosphere, meaning fuel-quality fixes could reduce aviation's warming impact much faster than waiting for fleet turnover or SAF scale-up.
- Notes ReFuelEU Aviation will introduce a Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) scheme tracking aromatics/naphthalene/sulphur in jet fuel — welcomed as a foundation for future binding EU fuel-quality legislation, though not yet a binding standard itself.
- Frames Sustainable Aviation Fuels (naturally low in aromatics/sulphur) as a complementary long-term solution, but stresses their slow scale-up makes near-term hydrotreatment of conventional fossil jet fuel the more immediately impactful lever.
- References a T&E webinar bringing together industry, policymakers and researchers to make the case that fixing jet fuel quality is technically, economically and legally feasible now.
