# Can Airbus deliver guilt-free flying?

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2021-04-29*

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Airbus claims hydrogen aircraft carrying 100-200 passengers over 1,000-2,000 nautical miles (e.g. Berlin–Lisbon, Warsaw–Madrid) by 2035.
- A 100-passenger, 1,000-mile-range hydrogen aircraft would eliminate only about 4% of EU aviation CO2 emissions; a 165-passenger, 2,000-mile aircraft would cover about 20%.
- Fuel makes up roughly 40% of aircraft operating costs; T&E's own calculations (adapted from the "Destination 2050" report) estimate a 165-seat hydrogen aircraft would cost 14–33% more to operate than an equivalent kerosene aircraft, even before counting development or hydrogen-supply-chain costs.
- Assumed 2050 fuel prices used in T&E's cost comparison: kerosene €690–1,000/tonne, hydrogen €2,200/tonne, power-to-liquid (e-kerosene) €1,900/tonne.
- T&E's recommended hydrogen fuel mandate for aviation: 1–2% by 2030, rising thereafter.

- This T&E opinion piece assesses whether Airbus's September 2020 hydrogen-aircraft announcement (backed rhetorically by French President Macron and adopted as an EU objective by the Commission) is technically and commercially realistic.
- T&E's assessment: hydrogen aircraft are technically feasible (citing 1950s-80s US/Soviet experiments and ZeroAvia's 2020 hydrogen-electric flight) but face major cost and engineering hurdles — hydrogen's lower energy density (roughly 4x the volume of jet fuel for equivalent energy) constrains range, and redesigned aircraft, storage tanks and airport refuelling infrastructure would all add cost.
- T&E doubts Airbus will actually hit the 2035 target, arguing no board would commit to developing a speculative aircraft with no clear market (invoking the Concorde and A380 as cautionary precedents), which is why Airbus instead emphasizes what "others" (governments, airports) need to do.
- T&E's recommended sequencing — targets, then regulation, then funding — argues governments should first commit to banning short-haul fossil-fuel flights by the early 2030s (mirroring combustion-car sales bans), then introduce a hydrogen fuel mandate to create a credible market (drawing an explicit analogy to California's Zero Emission Vehicle credits benefiting Tesla), and only then direct EU R&D/recovery funding (Clean Skies Undertaking) toward zero-emission technology, with the EU taxonomy recognising only zero-emission aircraft as sustainable.
- See [Aviation industry's net zero plan over-reliant on future technologies](aviation-industry-s-net-zero-plan-over-reliant-on-future-technologies.md) for T&E's related critique of industry over-reliance on unproven aircraft technology, and [FAQ: the what and how of e-kerosene](faq-the-what-and-how-of-e-kerosene.md) for T&E's parallel case for e-kerosene as the more near-term deployable alternative fuel.
