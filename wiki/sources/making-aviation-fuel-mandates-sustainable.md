# Making aviation fuel mandates sustainable

*Briefing · Brussels · 2020-12-16*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/making-aviation-fuel-mandates-sustainable) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2020_12_Aviation_SAF_mandates_rating_final.pdf)

> The EU and many European governments are adopting or proposing mandates to bring about the uptake of alternative fuels in the aviation sector.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Seven national/EU aviation SAF mandates assessed: Finland (proposed 30% SAF by 2030, from ~2023), France (in force from January 2022: advanced-biofuel-only energy targets of 1% in 2021, ~2% in 2025, 5% in 2030), Germany (proposed e-fuels-only targets of 0.5% in 2026, 1% in 2028, 2% in 2030), Netherlands (proposed 14% SAF by 2030 rising to 100% by 2050, from 2023), Norway (in force from 1 January 2020: 0.5% in 2020 rising to a proposed but not-yet-legislated 30% by 2030), Spain (targets/timing not yet known, law expected mid-2021), Sweden (proposed energy-based targets of ~1% in 2021, 5% in 2025, 30% in 2030, agreement expected by 30 June 2021).
- Of the seven, none reached T&E's "green" rating; Germany came closest for excluding crop-based biofuels and focusing on e-fuels, though sustainability criteria for those e-fuels remained undefined.
- Finland, Spain, Sweden and the Netherlands were rated red for likely/confirmed inclusion of crop-based biofuels or unrealistically high targets; France and Norway were flagged (amber) for excluding crop biofuels but setting high advanced-biofuel-only targets that risk over-demanding limited sustainable feedstocks like used cooking oil.
- Finland's mandate risk is compounded by classifying palm fatty acid distillate (PFAD, a palm oil by-product) as a "residue" rather than a crop-biofuel — relevant because the Finnish government is the largest shareholder in Neste Oil, the world's largest renewable diesel producer and a major PFAD user.

- This December 2020 T&E briefing assesses seven European countries' (and the EU's own forthcoming ReFuelEU) approaches to mandating sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) uptake, rating each on inclusion of crop-based biofuels, target realism, and promotion of e-fuels versus advanced biofuels.
- Central argument: government-mandated SAF uptake is the right strategy (voluntary/incentive-based approaches like the ETS having failed to shift kerosene use), but mandates done wrong — via high, energy-based targets that pull in crop-based or otherwise unsustainable feedstocks — risk repeating the road-transport biofuels experience under the original Renewable Energy Directive.
- T&E's recommendations: exclude all crop-based biofuels from mandates; include a specific e-fuels sub-target to direct investment toward the fuels with the largest emissions benefit; set targets based on realistic, impact-assessed feedstock/renewable-electricity availability rather than aspirational levels; and base targets on GHG intensity rather than simple energy content, to reward the cleanest fuels.
