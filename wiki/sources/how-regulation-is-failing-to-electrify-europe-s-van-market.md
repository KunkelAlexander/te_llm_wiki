# How regulation is failing to electrify Europe's van market

*Report · Brussels · 2021-05-10*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-regulation-is-failing-to-electrify-europes-van-market) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202105_van_CO2_report_final_compressed-1.pdf)

> Vans are the EU's fastest growing source of road transport emissions, and, with home deliveries on the rise, van sales are soaring. At the same time, emissions of new vans - on a per km average - have not decreased in three years.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Vans caused 13% of EU road-transport CO2 emissions in 2019; van CO2 emissions grew 58% since 1990 (113.2 Mt in 2019 vs. 71.5 Mt in 1990) — by far the largest increase of any road segment (cars and trucks each rose ~20% since 1990) — while van sales rose 57% between 2012–2019.
- The EU's 2020 van CO2 target of 147 gCO2/km (NEDC) was weakened by a generous "mass-adjustment" formula to an effective 158.5 gCO2/km (8% higher) — and average new-van emissions actually stagnated/rose slightly, from 157.6 gCO2/km (2017) to 159.3 gCO2/km (2019), before a marginal 2020 dip to ~158.8 gCO2/km.
- Only 2% of van sales were electric in 2020 (vs. 11.4% for cars including PHEVs); just 6 e-van models exceeded 1,000 sales.
- Preliminary 2020 data suggest Ford-Volkswagen and Iveco missed their 2020 CO2 targets (by 2.4 and 1.5 gCO2/km respectively).
- T&E/ICCT cost-curve analysis: the current -31% 2030 CO2 target could technically be met with zero EV sales, just by exhausting cost-effective diesel efficiency improvements (down to ~100 gCO2/km at <€2,500/vehicle).
- Bloomberg NEF (commissioned by T&E) projects e-vans reaching purchase-price parity with diesel vans by 2026 at the latest, with market potential of 15–17% e-van sales in 2025 and 46–73% in 2030; a 2017 CE Delft TCO study found smaller electric vans already reached cost parity in 2018, with medium/large vans reaching parity by 2022/2023.
- T&E's recommended revised CO2 targets: -20% by 2025, -31% target moved forward to 2027, at least -60% by 2030, -86% by 2033, and a 100% (0 gCO2/km) target by 2035 — alongside a separate ZEV sales target of 15% (2025), 25% (2027), 50% (2030), 80% (2033).
- T&E's modelling shows its recommended trajectory would cut 2030 van emissions to 69 MtCO2 (a 27% cut vs. 2019), versus 73–75 MtCO2 under weaker current-policy scenarios.

- This May 2021 T&E study analyses 2020 van-maker CO2 compliance data ahead of the EU's 2021 review of van CO2 standards, finding the weak 2020 target failed to drive any meaningful electrification and van CO2 emissions have effectively stagnated since 2017.
- Central argument: van CO2 regulation, unlike car regulation, has been too weak to compel electrification despite favourable total-cost-of-ownership economics already existing for smaller vans — manufacturers are supplying only the minimum electric vans required by law, not what the market/business case could support.
- Key regulatory-design recommendations: split vans into two weight categories (dividing line 1,760kg reference mass) rather than using the current mass-adjustment formula (which rewards heavier, more polluting vans); avoid a PHEV compliance loophole; and pair CO2 targets with an explicit ZEV sales-share target (since a CO2 target alone leaves room for compliance via ICE efficiency gains rather than electrification).
- T&E frames vans as a "neglected" area of EU transport policy relative to cars and trucks, disproportionately affecting urban air quality and enabled by the e-commerce boom.
- See [Unlocking electric trucking in the EU: recharging along highways](unlocking-electric-trucking-in-the-eu-recharging-along-highways.md) for T&E's parallel electrification analysis of the heavy-duty segment, and [Advanced renewable fuels in EU transport](advanced-renewable-fuels-in-eu-transport.md) for how van electrification feeds into T&E's broader RED renewable-transport-target modelling.
