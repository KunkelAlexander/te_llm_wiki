# Growth in air transport will destroy the sector's climate targets

*Original title (French): "La croissance du transport aérien anéantira les objectifs climatiques du secteur"*

*Report · France · 2025-01-13*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/te-france/articles/la-croissance-du-transport-aerien-aneantira-les-objectifs-climatiques-du-secteur)

> T&E shows that the aviation industry's own growth forecasts would translate into a doubling of European passenger numbers by 2050 and the depletion of the sector's carbon budget as soon as 2026. Airport expansion, the sector's under-taxation and the growing number of trips taken by the wealthiest are all issues lawmakers must urgently address.

## Summary

*French-office coverage of T&E's ["Down to Earth" report](down-to-earth.md) — see that page for the full quantitative analysis (traffic-growth scenarios, carbon-budget depletion, e-kerosene electricity demand); this page repeats a subset of the same figures for a French audience.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- If Airbus/Boeing's growth projections materialise, EU passenger air traffic doubles by 2050 versus 2019; European aviation would still burn 21.1 million tonnes of fossil kerosene in 2050 and, by 2049, could burn as much fossil kerosene as in 2023, despite a mandated 42% SAF blend.
- Producing enough e-kerosene by 2050 to meet ReFuelEU's 35% synthetic-fuel mandate under this growth scenario would require more renewable electricity than Germany's entire current electricity demand.
- European aviation emissions would fall only 3% by 2049 versus 2019 under this scenario, and the sector could deplete its 1.5°C-aligned carbon budget as early as 2026.
- Of the 24.2 million tonnes of biofuels aviation plans to consume by 2050, up to 80% risks coming from unsustainable feedstocks such as palm-oil derivatives.

- Summarises T&E's argument that SAF/e-kerosene uptake cannot offset unchecked industry traffic growth, and that legislators must urgently address airport expansion, aviation's tax exemptions, and the concentration of flights among frequent/wealthy travellers.
