# Hydrogen hype: Why the EU should be cautious about uncertain imports from far-flung places

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Across six case-study export countries (Egypt, Morocco, Oman, Norway, Chile, Namibia), combined potential hydrogen exports to the EU total only ~2.6 Mt/year — far below RePowerEU's 10 Mt/year import ambition.
- Reprioritising renewables toward green hydrogen production instead of grid decarbonisation would require transforming some countries' grids by up to 10x their current size (Chile, Namibia); in Egypt and Morocco, reprioritising renewables toward hydrogen (rather than grid decarbonisation) could otherwise have cut grid emissions by 7.2% and 42% respectively; in Oman, by up to 88%.
- Norway is the outlier: with 100% electricity access and an already low-emissions grid, it could pursue green hydrogen without the same grid-decarbonisation trade-off seen in the other five countries.

- This February 2024 T&E report (research by Ricardo) examines six countries with stated plans to export hydrogen to the EU, assessing each country's electricity-grid context, hydrogen strategy, avoided/consequential emissions, and environmental/social/economic factors, plus a counterfactual for the EU relying on domestic production instead.
- Central finding: the EU's import ambitions are running well ahead of what these exporter countries can realistically deliver, given limited existing renewables capacity (outside Norway), water scarcity constraints on electrolysis, and the absence of long-distance hydrogen transport infrastructure.
- Assesses supply-chain risk by modelling a cutoff of Chile (treated as the single largest prospective exporter) and finds the risk to the EU is "extremely minimal" given expected domestic production and storage capacity — undercutting the energy-security rationale sometimes used to justify pursuing imports.
- Recommends the EU prioritise building out domestic green hydrogen/electrolyser capacity and target hydrogen use toward genuinely hard-to-electrify sectors first, treating longer-term imports as a secondary option contingent on exporter countries meeting stricter environmental/social conditions.
