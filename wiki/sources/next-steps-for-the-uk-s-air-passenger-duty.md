# Next steps for the UK's Air Passenger Duty

*News · Brussels · 2021-06-24*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/next-steps-for-the-uks-air-passenger-duty) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2021_06_Aviation_tax_consultation_TE_response.pdf)

> This is Transport & Environment's response to the questions posed by the UK Government's consultation on aviation tax reform. The response highlights our concerns around the UK government's proposals to decrease tax levels for domestic flights. It further highlights the fact that the aviation sector is undertaxed: it does not 'pay its way'. VAT is not applied to airline tickets, nor is there any tax on jet fuel. This is the direct opposite of the 'polluter pays principle'.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Aviation's UK tax breaks were estimated (pre-pandemic) at £7bn/year.
- VAT is applied to domestic air tickets in 23 EU countries plus Australia, Canada and the US — but not in the UK; the UK also does not apply fuel duty to jet fuel (unlike Canada, the US, Australia, Japan, Thailand, Vietnam).
- A PwC study (for Airlines UK) found fully abolishing Air Passenger Duty (APD) would increase flight demand by only 10%, with 70% of that increase coming from more use of existing routes rather than new routes.
- A single private jet emits as much CO2 in 4 hours as an average European emits in a year (cited again here re: APD exemptions for business jets).
- Illustrative distance discrepancy in APD's banding: London–Hawaii (11,656km) is roughly double the distance of London–Washington DC (5,898km), yet both currently fall under comparable APD treatment based on capital-city distance.

- This is T&E's June 2021 response to the UK government's Aviation Tax Consultation, opposing the government's proposed cut to domestic Air Passenger Duty (APD) and calling for a broader package of aviation tax reform.
- Central argument: aviation is fundamentally undertaxed (no VAT on tickets, no kerosene duty, generous free EU/UK ETS allowances, no coverage of long-haul emissions), so reducing the sector's one explicit tax (APD) sends the wrong signal ahead of COP26 and contradicts the UK's net-zero-by-2050 commitment.
- T&E disputes the consultation's core premise — that lower domestic APD would boost regional "connectivity" — citing Eurocontrol findings that economic growth, not tax levels, drives flight demand, and noting Northern Ireland's earlier APD cut to zero on long-haul routes had no demonstrable effect on passenger numbers.
- T&E's alternative recommendation: exempt zero-emission aircraft from all domestic aviation tax (rather than cutting APD broadly) to signal a clear technology direction; apply VAT to plane tickets (at least for business/premium fares); introduce kerosene taxation (enabled by the post-Brexit UK-EU air services agreement); reform the UK ETS to phase out free allowances by 2030 and extend its scope to all destinations including long-haul; and reform international APD toward a distance-based (price-per-km) structure rather than banded-by-capital-city distance.
- T&E specifically calls for private/business jets to face higher, not lower, tax rates given their outsized per-passenger climate impact.
- See [Private jets: can the super-rich supercharge zero-emission aviation?](private-jets-can-the-super-rich-supercharge-zero-emission-aviation.md) for the detailed private-jet emissions data cited in this response, and [The UK's green jet fuel mandate needs robust sustainability criteria](the-uk-s-green-jet-fuel-mandate-needs-robust-sustainability-criteria-to-set-avia.md) for T&E's parallel UK aviation-policy advocacy from the same period.
