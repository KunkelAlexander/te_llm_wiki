# Implementing the EU's e-SAF mandate

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-10-01*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/implementing-the-eus-e-saf-mandate) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202410_ReFuelEU_Penalties.pdf)

*(No abstract was published for this document; summary below written from the source text.)*

This briefing analyses the penalty system that will apply to jet fuel suppliers under the EU's ReFuelEU Aviation regulation from 2025, finding that paying penalties for missing sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) quotas would always be costlier than complying with the mandate, provided penalties are based on accurate price references. It warns that legacy jet fuel suppliers are not yet involved in major e-kerosene projects in Europe despite this, and estimates that collective non-compliance penalties for e-kerosene could reach up to 292 billion euros between 2030 and 2035, compared with an estimated 37 billion euros to produce enough e-kerosene to meet the mandate over the same period.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- ReFuelEU requires fuel-supplier penalties of at least 2x the price difference between fossil kerosene and SAF; using 2023 prices (fossil kerosene €800/t, bio-SAF €2,774/t), the minimum bio-SAF penalty works out to ~€3,948/tonne.
- T&E estimates e-kerosene production costs (Fischer-Tropsch, green H2, three electricity-price scenarios) at €3,042–5,594/tonne, implying a sale price of €4,867–8,951/tonne (60% margin) and a corresponding minimum e-SAF penalty of €8,134–16,302/tonne; T&E recommends Member States set the actual penalty above €9,000/tonne.
- If e-kerosene supply is completely absent from 2030–2035, EU fuel suppliers collectively would owe €145–292 billion in penalties — versus an estimated €20–37 billion to actually produce enough e-kerosene to comply, roughly 5–7x cheaper than paying penalties.
- Under a "delayed by two years" scenario (e-kerosene starting 2032 instead of 2030), collective penalties would still reach €87–175 billion by 2035; in 2032 alone, penalties (€13–26bn) would be enough to fund 8–16 e-kerosene production facilities.
- Individual case study: Air bp (~28% of EU jet fuel supply share, ~12.2 Mt/year in the EU) risks paying up to ~€9bn in penalties by 2032 and ~€19bn more by 2035 if two years late on e-kerosene supply — versus just €6bn to comply — or up to ~€50bn if it supplies no e-kerosene at all through 2035.
- ReFuelEU's e-kerosene sub-target rises from 0.7–1.2% (2030–31) to 2% (2032–34) to 5% (2035) of total SAF share; non-compliance carries a "follow-up obligation" — unmet quotas roll over to the following year, compounding the cost of delay.
- As of the briefing, none of the at least 36 large-scale industrial e-kerosene projects identified in Europe had reached Final Investment Decision.
- The UK's SAF mandate uses a different enforcement design — a buyout price (£7,264/€8,611 per tonne for SAF, £7,728/€9,161 for e-SAF) with no follow-up obligation, making UK non-compliance costs far lower than under ReFuelEU.

- T&E argues ReFuelEU's penalty mechanism is well-designed in principle (non-compliance should always cost more than compliance) but is undermined by continued uncertainty, since Member States had not yet disclosed their actual penalty levels by the required end-2024 deadline, and by legacy oil companies' continued non-involvement in e-kerosene project financing.
- Recommends Member States publish penalty levels immediately, base them on EASA's forthcoming ReFuelEU technical report using realistic (not overly optimistic) price assumptions, update them regularly for price fluctuations, and improve jet-fuel-market transparency (e.g. EASA reporting supplier-level SAF volumes).
