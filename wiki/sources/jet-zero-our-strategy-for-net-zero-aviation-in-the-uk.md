# Jet Zero: our strategy for net zero aviation in the UK

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-09-20*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/jet-zero-our-strategy-for-net-zero-aviation-in-the-uk) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2021_09_TE_consultation_response_UK_Jet_Zero_aviation.pdf)

> T&E responds to questions posed by the UK government's consultation on decarbonising UK aviation

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- UK aviation carried over 300 million passengers in 2019 — far more than any other European nation (only Germany and Spain exceeded 200 million).
- UK aviation emitted 38.2 MtCO2e in 2018, making the UK the third-highest emitting nation for aviation globally (behind only the US and China); UK aviation emissions rose 125% versus 1990, while the UK's overall territorial emissions fell 43% over the same period.
- 2019 was the sector's highest-ever emissions year.

- This September 2021 T&E response to the UK Department for Transport's "Jet Zero" net-zero aviation strategy consultation welcomes several proposals (reviewing free ETS allowances, consulting on extending the ETS to non-CO2 gases, mandatory environmental information for consumers, and a UK SAF mandate) but identifies three major structural flaws.
- Flaw 1: the consultation frames government as merely "one of a number of interested parties" in decarbonising aviation, when T&E argues the UK's democratic net-zero mandate requires government to be the primary driver — including ensuring 100% of jet fuel supplied is SAF by 2050 (since the consultation's own scenarios assume "residual" CO2 emissions will remain in 2050, which T&E says is a policy choice, not an inevitability).
- Flaw 2: an "ambition-to-action gap" — unlike the SAF mandate (which requires suppliers to act), there are no proposed requirements for the industry to adopt zero-emission aircraft or invest in greenhouse gas removals, leaving T&E to ask why the consultation has "many carrots, and few sticks."
- Flaw 3: the consultation implicitly places the burden on government (i.e. taxpayers) to ensure enough greenhouse gas removal capacity exists by 2050 to offset residual airline emissions, when T&E argues the industry itself should fund that market since it's the industry that wants to keep polluting past the net-zero deadline.
- T&E also flags the complete absence of demand-management (traffic growth) policies as a major gap, and calls for the UK to ban non-zero-emission aircraft for domestic use from 2040 and end fossil kerosene uplift from 2050, alongside a kerosene tax (which the UK, unlike the EU's Fit for 55 proposal, had not proposed).
