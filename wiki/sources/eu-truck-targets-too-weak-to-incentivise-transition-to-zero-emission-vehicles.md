# EU truck targets too weak to incentivise transition to zero-emission vehicles

*Report · Brussels · 2021-10-10*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/eu-truck-targets-too-weak-to-incentivise-the-production-of-enough-zero-emission-vehicles) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202108_truck_CO2_report_final.pdf)

> T&E's study on truckmakers' progress towards EU truck CO2 targets shows that most are already on track to overachieve the target set for 2025, without increasing their production of zero-emission vehicles.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Truck CO2 emissions rose 29% since 1990; trucks are under 2% of vehicles on EU roads but ~23% of road-transport CO2 emissions (2019); long-haul (LH) trucks make up 85.8% of ~170,000 regulated truck registrations in the reference period (July 2019–June 2020), with the 5-LH sub-group alone at 61.9%.
- Reference-period average emissions: 52.7 gCO2/tonne-km — the baseline for the EU's -15% (2025) and -30% (2030) truck CO2 targets. Scania led all manufacturers (emissions 3.2% below average, thanks to ~20% lower aerodynamic drag), while Volvo (+3.1%) and Daimler (+2.4%) trailed.
- Best-in-class models/manufacturers already available today could deliver a 4.9–6.1% emissions cut versus the baseline — meaning truckmakers could largely meet the 2025 target using current technology and regulatory flexibilities (ZLEV bonus, banking-and-borrowing) alone, without any additional zero-emission vehicle (ZEV) sales.
- Only 3 ZEVs were registered in the entire reference period (DAF, Volvo, MAN models) out of ~170,000 trucks; gas trucks made up 2.3% of registrations (~3,900 vehicles) and delivered only 4.5% lower emissions than diesel on average (ranging from -12% to +6% depending on sub-group) — with methane not counted in official CO2 figures.
- Truckmakers' own public ZEV announcements imply ~7% ZEV sales by 2025 and ~43% by 2030 — far above what the current CO2 standards require — meaning under a "full flexibility + announcements" scenario, the 2025 target could be overachieved by 3.2 percentage points overall (0.9pp for IVECO to 6.9pp for Scania).
- New long-haul trucks show only a 15% emissions range between EU countries (vs. 26% for vans), and Central/Eastern European countries' long-haul trucks are on average 3% less emitting than Western Europe's — the opposite pattern to vans.

- This October 2021 T&E report (first in an annual series) analyses the EU's first-ever truck CO2 emissions dataset (the "reference period" baseline for 2025/2030 targets) and concludes the current standards are too weak to meaningfully drive zero-emission truck uptake in the 2020s.
- Central finding: because best-in-class technology and regulatory flexibilities alone can nearly meet the 2025 target, and truckmakers' own voluntary ZEV announcements already exceed what's required by a wide margin, the 2022 review of the CO2 standards must introduce a meaningful intermediate 2027/2028 target and a much higher mandatory ZLEV benchmark (T&E recommends 30% by 2028) to avoid a "stagnation" in ZEV supply through the late 2020s.
- T&E's other key recommendation: set a 100% CO2 reduction target for the vast majority of new heavy-duty vehicles by 2035 (mirroring the car/van approach) and significantly raise the 2030 target.
- See [Unlocking electric trucking in the EU: recharging along highways](unlocking-electric-trucking-in-the-eu-recharging-along-highways.md) for T&E's companion analysis of the charging infrastructure needed to support this scale of truck electrification, and [LNG trucks: a dead-end bridge](lng-trucks-a-dead-end-bridge.md) for T&E's real-world testing showing gas trucks' claimed emissions benefits (referenced in this report) don't hold up.
