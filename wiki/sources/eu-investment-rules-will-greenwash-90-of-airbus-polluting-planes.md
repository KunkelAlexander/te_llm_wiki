# EU investment rules will greenwash 90% of Airbus' polluting planes

*Press release · Brussels · 2023-02-17*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/eu-investment-rules-will-greenwash-90-of-airbus-polluting-planes) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/TE-analysis-aviation-taxonomy-February-2023.pdf)

> The European Commission is set to rubber stamp thousands of polluting aircraft as sustainable, under the EU taxonomy rules.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Under the draft EU taxonomy "best-in-class" (BIC) efficiency criteria, 90.4%-99.7% of Airbus's 7,255 pending aircraft orders (as of 31 January 2023) would qualify as taxonomy-compliant, despite running exclusively on fossil kerosene.
- Once current orders are delivered, up to 58% of easyJet's future fleet could be considered taxonomy-compliant; Ryanair, easyJet and Wizz Air's order books could be up to 100% "best-in-class" since they only buy new-generation aircraft.
- At Airbus's 2019 delivery rate (863 aircraft/year), the full 7,255-order backlog could be delivered within about 8.5 years — meaning the "green" label would apply almost immediately to a huge share of near-term deliveries.

- This February 2023 T&E briefing/press release calculates how many Airbus aircraft and how much of major European airlines' fleets (Lufthansa Group, Air France-KLM, IAG, Ryanair, easyJet, Wizz Air) would qualify as "environmentally sustainable" investments under the EU taxonomy's draft aviation criteria, using Airbus/Boeing public order books and an EASA-provided efficiency compliance list.
- Central criticism: the taxonomy's "best-in-class" standard only requires aircraft to be marginally more fuel-efficient than older models — a bar most new aircraft clear regardless of the taxonomy's existence, since airlines already renew fleets to cut fuel costs — making the label a "rubber stamp" on business-as-usual rather than a genuine sustainability filter.
- Notes the criteria do not require proof that a taxonomy-compliant new aircraft is actually replacing an older, less efficient one (the "replacement ratio" needed for such verification has not yet been defined), leaving room for a large share of pure fleet growth to also qualify.
- See [EU taxonomy for aviation: Will Von der Leyen rubber stamp the biggest act of aviation greenwashing in decades?](eu-taxonomy-for-aviation-will-von-der-leyen-rubber-stamp-the-biggest-act-of-avia.md) for T&E's accompanying op-ed making the political case against adopting these criteria.
