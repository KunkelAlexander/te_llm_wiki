# A drop of e-fuel in an ocean of oil

*Report · Brussels · 2022-10-04*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/a-drop-of-e-fuel-in-an-ocean-of-oil) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2022_10_Briefing_A_drop_of_e-fuel_in_an_ocean_of_oil_final.pdf)

> New analysis of the refining industry's own data finds there will not be enough synthetic fuels to significantly decarbonise the existing car stock.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Using the oil-refining industry's own (Concawe) best-case e-fuel production forecast, only about 5 million of the EU's projected 287 million cars (1.8%) could run fully on e-fuel in 2035 — rising only to ~3% even if the whole existing fleet shifted to hybrids.
- A petrol car running on a blend of e-fuel cuts lifecycle emissions by only 5% versus running on petrol; even on 100% sustainability-compliant e-fuel, a 2030 battery electric car would still be 53% cleaner over its lifecycle.
- Direct electrification is about 77% efficient versus roughly 16% for petrol cars running on e-fuel — electric cars can travel 5-6x farther than an equivalent e-petrol car on the same 100 kWh of renewable electricity.
- Shifting 10% of cars to e-fuels and another 10% to hydrogen would raise electricity demand by 36%; a driver using e-fuel in a petrol car would be about €10,000 worse off than a BEV owner over 5 years, even under optimistic 2030 e-fuel price assumptions.

- This October 2022 T&E report rebuts oil- and auto-supplier-industry claims that "carbon neutral" e-fuels can meaningfully decarbonise Europe's existing combustion-engine car fleet, using Concawe's (the European Petroleum Refiners Association's own research arm) production projections as the input data.
- Notes Concawe's scenario doesn't even guarantee the e-fuel produced is genuinely carbon-neutral, since it allows both fossil-sourced CO2 (not just DAC) and grid electricity "claimed" as renewable via non-additionality-guaranteeing certificates (Guarantees of Origin) rather than direct connection or hourly-matched power purchase agreements.
- Highlights an opportunity-cost argument: Concawe's own scenario allocates most e-fuel to road transport and reserves relatively little for aviation and shipping — sectors T&E says need 6x more e-fuel by 2035 than Concawe assumes, since they, unlike cars, have no viable direct-electrification alternative.
- Also raises a climate-justice objection to importing e-fuels from sunnier/windier regions (North Africa, Middle East, Chile): those regions' own transport/electricity decarbonisation should take priority over exporting renewable electrons to the EU for an inefficient use case.
- Concludes EU car CO2 standards should not carve out an "e-fuels" exemption from the 2035 100%-CO2-reduction target for new cars, since doing so would delay BEV scale-up and divert scarce renewable capacity and DAC-produced carbon away from aviation and shipping, where e-fuels are the only realistic decarbonisation option — see [Scaling up Direct Air Capture](scaling-up-direct-air-capture.md) and [Getting hydrogen right from the start](getting-hydrogen-right-from-the-start.md) for T&E's parallel analysis of DAC and additionality constraints on renewable-fuel supply.
