# Decarbonising long-haul trucking in Germany

*Original title (German): "Die Dekarbonisierung des Lkw-Fernverkehrs in Deutschland"*

*Report · Germany · 2021-04-06*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/te-deutschland/articles/how-decarbonise-long-haul-trucking-germany) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2021_04_TE_Dekarbonisierung_des_Lkw_Fernverkehrs_in_Deutschland_kurzfassung_final.pdf)

> Efficiency measures — improving conventional diesel trucks' fuel efficiency, incentivising a shift of freight to rail and inland waterways, and improving logistics efficiency — can help reduce Germany's freight-transport emissions, but are not enough on their own to meet the country's 2030 and 2050 climate targets. Trucks must be fully decarbonised by 2050 at the latest.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- German transport emitted 196 Mt CO2e/year in 2019 (23% of Germany's total GHG emissions, including international aviation/shipping); road transport accounts for 80% of transport emissions, of which around 28% is trucking.
- Germany's Federal Climate Change Act requires a 55% GHG cut vs. 1990 by 2030 and net-zero by 2050; the government's 2030 climate programme targets around a third of heavy-duty vehicle-kilometres being electric or running on electricity-based fuels by 2030.
- About 750,000 trucks/semi-trailers over 3.5 tonnes were registered in Germany in 2019 (roughly 85,000 newly registered each year); more than 99% of the current fleet runs on conventional diesel.
- 76% of Germany's total road-freight distance is covered by single trips of up to 800km; the study defines "long-haul" as single trips over 400km and uses 800km as the minimum range requirement for the technologies assessed.
- Five zero-emission technologies compared on system cost (excluding taxes/subsidies) and total cost of ownership (TCO, including them): battery-electric trucks (BEV), overhead-catenary battery-electric trucks (OC-BEV), hydrogen fuel-cell trucks (FCEV), and internal-combustion trucks running on power-to-liquid e-diesel or power-to-methane e-methane.
- Finding: BEV and OC-BEV are, on a system-cost basis, most likely the cheapest zero-well-to-wheel-emission options — cheaper even than FCEV/e-fuel ICEVs that import renewable hydrogen or e-fuels produced overseas (e.g. North Africa), because additional conversion/transport/distribution costs for imported hydrogen more than offset its lower production cost, except for at-scale local overseas production compared to domestic hydrogen refuelling-station production.
- Germany's vehicle tax includes a one-time €26.30 registration fee and an annual tax of €556.

- This April 2021 T&E Germany study (author Fedor Unterlohner, external review by Öko-Institut) compares the system and total-cost-of-ownership economics of five zero-emission long-haul trucking technologies to identify the most cost-effective decarbonisation pathway for Germany's road-freight sector.
- Central finding: direct electrification (battery-electric and overhead-catenary trucks) is very likely the most cost-effective zero-emission long-haul trucking option, ahead of hydrogen fuel cells and e-fuel-powered combustion engines, even under scenarios importing cheaper renewable hydrogen/e-fuels from regions like North Africa.
- T&E's argument: efficiency measures alone (fuel-efficient diesel, modal shift to rail/inland waterways, logistics optimisation) cannot deliver Germany's climate targets — full decarbonisation of the truck fleet by 2050 at the latest is necessary, and the choice of technology pathway has major cost implications for who bears the burden (society vs. individual operators, given differing tax/toll/subsidy treatment).
- Policy recommendations (per the report's structure) cover stimulating demand for zero-emission trucks, building charging/refuelling infrastructure, reforming energy taxation, and ensuring supply of zero-emission trucks and urban zero-emission freight delivery.
- See [Unlocking electric trucking in the EU: recharging along highways](unlocking-electric-trucking-in-the-eu-recharging-along-highways.md) for T&E's EU-wide (not Germany-specific) modelling of long-haul truck charging infrastructure needs, and [LNG trucks: a dead-end bridge](lng-trucks-a-dead-end-bridge.md) for T&E's parallel critique of gas-based truck technologies not covered in this cost comparison.
