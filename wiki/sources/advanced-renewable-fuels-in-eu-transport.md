# Advanced renewable fuels in EU transport

*Report · Brussels · 2021-03-31*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/advanced-renewable-fuels-eu-transport) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202103_Advanced_renewable_fuels_EU_Transport_Final.pdf)

> This study assesses the feasibility of the European Commission's proposed Climate Target Plan targets for renewable energy in fuels in 2030, focusing specifically on the suggested 24% for renewable energy in transport. The goal is to assess whether this target is realistically feasible, taking into account the expected availability of different sustainable advanced fuels in 2030.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- The EU27 reached a Renewable Energy Share in Transport (RES-T) of 8.9% in 2019 (equivalent to 6.6% of energy); compliant biofuels accounted for 90% of the renewable energy content and 82% of the RES-T, while renewable electricity in road transport contributed just 1.7% of RES-T even after multipliers.
- Renewable electricity's share of road transport RES-T grew 674% from 2011–2019, but this only raised its RES-T contribution by 0.5 percentage points, illustrating how small a base it started from; meanwhile Annex IX ("advanced") biofuel consumption grew more than 6-fold to 167 PJ by 2019, partly driven by a tripling of used cooking oil (UCO) use for biodiesel over the same period.
- Under T&E's modelling, an "Enhanced Current Policies" (ECP) scenario delivers a RES-T of only 15.9% (~690 PJ) by 2030 — well short of the Commission's proposed 24% target — while T&E's more ambitious "Road2Zero" scenario (65% car CO2 cut by 2030, high truck/van/bus electrification, 2% aviation e-kerosene, 7% shipping e-ammonia) reaches 23.2%, still short of 24%.
- To hit 24% without these additional measures would require relying on unsustainable crop biofuels — T&E estimates the Commission's own Climate Target Plan scenarios assume ~5% conventional crop biofuels persist through 2030.
- T&E's estimated maximum sustainable advanced-biofuel availability for the EU27 in 2030 is 5.8 Mtoe (245 PJ), with the bulk needing to go to aviation given competing road-transport demand.
- In the Road2Zero scenario, aviation would use 2.8% advanced biofuels and 2% e-kerosene by 2030, while shipping would need 7% e-ammonia — requiring roughly 7.5 GW of electrolysers for shipping alone.
- Road transport is projected to contribute 70–80% of the overall RES-T target, with passenger cars alone contributing 7.4 percentage points in the ECP scenario.
- Both modelled 2030 scenarios remain above 1990 transport emissions levels; only rail and shipping emissions fall below or match 1990 levels. The Road2Zero scenario cuts emissions by 216 Mt CO2e versus 132 Mt CO2e for ECP — both short of the economy-wide 55% reduction target applied to the transport sector.

- This April 2021 T&E study (by Thomas Earl) models two scenarios for the EU's 2030 Renewable Energy Directive (RED) transport target ahead of its expected June 2021 revision, testing whether the Commission's proposed 24% RES-T target (from its Climate Target Plan) is realistically achievable without relying on unsustainable crop biofuels.
- Central finding/recommendation: T&E recommends against setting the target at 24%, since that level is very unlikely to be met sustainably; instead it recommends setting the RED transport target at around 16% for advanced renewable fuels only (excluding crop biofuels and fossil-based fuels like blue hydrogen), with flexibility to increase it at member-state level only where genuinely ambitious CO2 standards and e-fuels uptake are already delivering it.
- T&E's key structural argument: it is vehicle CO2 standards (for cars, vans, trucks, buses) — not the RED's RES-T target itself — that actually drive the bulk of achievable renewable-energy uptake in transport, since electrification via CO2 standards is what shifts the vehicle fleet, while a higher RES-T target alone would simply pull in more unsustainable biofuels to make up the shortfall.
- T&E recommends the RED explicitly exclude crop-based biofuels and fossil-based fuels (recycled carbon fuels, blue hydrogen) from its scope, adopt stricter/narrower advanced-biofuel safeguards, ensure additionality for electrofuels, and create a dedicated mechanism to reward renewable electricity used directly in EVs (rather than only rewarding blended fuels).
