# Double or quits for climate president Von der Leyen

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2024-05-31*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/double-or-quits-for-climate-president-von-der-leyen)

*(No abstract was published for this document; summary below written from the source text.)*

This op-ed argues that the outcome of the June 2024 European Parliament elections will determine whether the EU continues to lead on climate and industrial policy — citing the 2035 combustion-engine phase-out, hydrogen and e-kerosene targets, and carbon pricing — or cedes ground to China and the United States, which are investing heavily in electric vehicles and clean energy under pressure from a possible far-right-influenced coalition. It contends that European carmakers, having already invested in EV production, would lose out to Chinese competitors if these green policies were rolled back.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- China accounts for 90% of the world's additional emissions since 2015, per the op-ed's framing of why the EU's own climate ambition, while important, won't alone determine the planet's fate.
- China's EV sales share had just hit 44%; the US is spending $188 billion on electric vehicles; the EU has spent €80 billion on e-mobility since 2021.
- EU carmakers were expected to soon offer entry-level EVs priced at €20,000–€25,000.

*(No further quantitative findings — this is an opinion piece by T&E's Executive Director William Todts, not a data-driven report.)*

- Argues the 2024 European elections are less about whether the EU's climate laws survive at all (the author expects a centrist EPP/liberal/S&D majority to hold) and more about whether Ursula von der Leyen, if reappointed Commission President, defends her Green Deal legacy against pushback from farmer protests and a CDU–Le Pen–Meloni-aligned coalition targeting the 2035 combustion-engine phase-out, green fuel quotas, and shipping carbon pricing.
- Frames five open questions determining the outcome: whether the centrist majority holds; whether von der Leyen secures head-of-state backing; whether the Socialists (via Teresa Ribera as prospective climate commissioner) prioritise the Green Deal; whether von der Leyen defends rather than waters down her own legacy; and whether she mobilises financing (joint borrowing, front-loaded Innovation Fund revenue, ECB rate support) to turn the Green Deal into an industrial strategy.
- Concludes industry itself (citing the "Antwerp declaration") sees "no way back" from the green transition, framing a rollback as most likely to just hand market share to Chinese competitors rather than change the underlying trajectory.
