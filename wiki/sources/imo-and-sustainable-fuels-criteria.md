# IMO and sustainable fuels criteria

*Briefing · Brussels · 2024-10-24*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/sustainable-fuels-criteria) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Briefing-Sustainable-Fuel-Criteria_2024-10-24-121232_xqfk.pdf)

> Alternative marine fuels come with different well-to-wake (WtW) GHG footprint. Currently there are no fuels in production that can deliver zero GHG on a life-cycle basis. But some renewable fuel pathways do have the potential to eventually reach that target, while others will always have substantial residual emissions in the production chain.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- T&E proposes transitional well-to-wake (WtW) GHG-reduction thresholds for a fuel to qualify as "sustainable" under the IMO's Global Fuel Standard (GFS): at least 90% reduction (max 9.4 gCO2e/MJ) from 2030, 95% (max 4.7 gCO2e/MJ) from 2040, and 100% from 2050 — versus a fossil baseline of 94 gCO2e/MJ.
- Grid-connected e-fuel production would need renewable/low-carbon electricity to make up over 90% of the power mix just to meet a 70% GHG-reduction threshold; a 90% threshold effectively requires either a fully decarbonised grid or fuel plants directly connected to dedicated renewable power — in practice infeasible via grid electricity in almost any country today.
- At an 80% reduction threshold, biomethane from biowaste (best case ~77.9% reduction, even with the lowest-methane-slip ship engines) would narrowly fail to qualify; used cooking oil (UCO) biodiesel (~82.6% reduction) would narrowly pass, despite the fuel's known fraud and scarcity risks (see the [UCO fraud study](uco-unknown-cooking-oil-high-hopes-on-limited-and-suspicious-materials.md)).
- China alone collected 3.4 Mt of UCO in 2023 with an estimated 2.5 Mt of additional collectable potential — a combined ceiling of 5.9 Mt/year — far short of combined global road, aviation and shipping demand for the feedstock.

- T&E's IMO policy team argues the Global Fuel Standard's "zero and near-zero" fuel definition must use a strict, technology-neutral GHG-intensity threshold (rather than picking specific engine/conversion technologies) to avoid stranded assets and ensure only genuinely scalable, electricity-derived fuels get promoted.
- Warns that setting the bar too low (e.g. 70-80%) would qualify UCO-based fuels whose supply is already fraud-prone and inadequate to meet demand, diverting the IMO framework toward feedstocks T&E considers unsustainable at scale.
