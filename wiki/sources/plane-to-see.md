# Plane to see

*Op-ed · Brussels · 2024-05-02*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/plane-to-see)

> Why is the monitoring of contrails from planes causing controversy?

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Contrail warming is at least as large as the warming from all of aviation's CO2 emissions combined, but far more geographically concentrated: around 80% of contrail warming comes from just 2% of flights, mostly long-haul routes to the US and Asia.
- More than 20,000 flights cross Europe daily, and their persistent contrail clouds cause significant warming, especially at night (contrails have a slight cooling effect during the day but a much stronger warming effect after dark).
- Contrail avoidance (rerouting around ice-supersaturated regions) has an estimated carbon abatement cost of under €10/tonne, versus roughly €500/tonne for SAF — with extra fuel burn from rerouting estimated at under 1%.
- The EU's reformed aviation carbon-pricing scheme will start monitoring contrails by 2025 and requires the EU to introduce mitigation measures by 2028.
- The warming effect of contrails on climate was first described in a 1999 IPCC report — 25 years before this piece was published, during which regulators took no action.

- Op-ed argues that legacy airlines and IATA are lobbying against even monitoring contrails on long-haul flights (which they mostly operate and which cause most contrail warming), citing cost concerns over fitting humidity sensors/reporting data.
- Argues contrail avoidance is the cheapest and fastest climate lever available to the aviation industry — cheaper than SAF — enabled by prediction models (cited providers: Satavia, Flight Keys) that can reroute flights around "big hit" contrail-forming regions with negligible cost or delay.
- Frames airline opposition to monitoring as a repeat of decades-long "doubt and delay" tactics, and calls on airline executives to embrace contrail mitigation as a reputational opportunity.
