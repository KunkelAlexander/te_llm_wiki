# Europe's hydrogen plans reliant on uncertain imports – report

*Press release · Brussels · 2024-02-13*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/europes-hydrogen-plans-reliant-on-uncertain-imports-report)

> Most of the countries Europe is relying on for imports are not ready to scale up production, new study shows

<!-- ENRICHED SUMMARY: hand-written, preserved across regenerations by 1_build_wiki_subset.py -->

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- RePowerEU targets 20 million tonnes of renewable hydrogen by 2030, half from imports (10 Mt) — but a Ricardo study for T&E of six major planned exporters (Norway, Chile, Egypt, Morocco, Namibia, Oman) finds they could combined deliver only about a quarter of that 10 Mt import target (2.6 Mt), and just 1% of these countries' planned green hydrogen production has actually secured financing.
- Namibia would need renewable generation capacity more than 10x its own projected 2030 electricity demand to meet its planned hydrogen exports to the EU — despite half of Namibians currently having no electricity access at all.
- Producing the 2.6 Mt of hydrogen planned for EU export would require 55-80 million tonnes of water per year — equivalent to 32,000 Olympic swimming pools annually — in countries (all except Norway) that already face significant future water scarcity.
- The EU could produce 6-7.5 million tonnes of renewable hydrogen domestically by 2030 — enough to meet actual sectoral needs (aviation, shipping, fertilisers) if demand is properly targeted; T&E argues the EU's current hydrogen ambition is roughly 5x what's actually needed to hit its 2030 climate goals.

- This February 2024 T&E press release summarises a Ricardo-authored study assessing whether six countries with major hydrogen-export ambitions to the EU can realistically deliver, finding structural obstacles (limited existing renewables capacity, water scarcity, absent long-distance transport infrastructure) mean most are far from ready.
- Notes hydrogen pipelines to distant exporters (e.g. North Africa) would take years to build, meaning near-term imports would have to arrive as shippable e-fuels (e-ammonia, e-methanol, e-kerosene) rather than pure hydrogen.
- Highlights a positive counterpoint: hydrogen supply chains could create up to 2 million EU jobs by 2030 based on already-announced investments, reinforcing T&E's call to prioritise developing a genuine domestic hydrogen/electrolyser market before scaling up imports.
- See [Hydrogen hype: Why the EU should be cautious about uncertain imports from far-flung places](hydrogen-hype-why-the-eu-should-be-cautious-about-uncertain-imports-from-far-flu.md) for the full underlying Ricardo report with detailed country-by-country analysis.
