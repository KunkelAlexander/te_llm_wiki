# Unlocking electric trucking in the EU: recharging along highways

*Report · Brussels · 2021-04-21*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/unlocking-electric-trucking-eu-recharging-along-highways) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202102_pathways_report_final.pdf)

> Limits to battery range and fast charging infrastructure have previously made the electrification of long-haul trucking difficult. However, technological improvements have made this not just a possibility but the best option for decarbonising long-haul trucking. As charging remains one of the biggest challenges, T&E's study assesses the charging infrastructure needed for long-haul trucking to become carbon neutral.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Long-haul trips (over 400km) make up 38% of European truck activity (tkm) but only ~5% of trips; at least 8 of 10 long-haul trips touch the TEN-T core network, and TEN-T-connected regions account for 80% of all EU road-freight activity and 88% of long-haul activity.
- Three battery-electric truck (BET) uptake scenarios modelled: Industry-Baseline (based on OEM announcements: 293,000 EU+UK BETs by 2030), EV-Leaders (458,000 by 2030), and Road-2-Zero (T&E's own climate-compatible scenario: 734,000 by 2030, 100% zero-emission sales by 2035).
- Prioritising charging infrastructure on the ~25,000km TEN-T core corridors lets electric trucks cover 12%, 18% and 25% of total long-haul freight activity by 2030 in the Baseline/EV-Leaders/Road-2-Zero scenarios respectively, cutting long-haul road-freight CO2 by 11%, 16% and up to 23%.
- T&E calculates the Road-2-Zero scenario needs 4,400 high-power public chargers and 6,600 destination chargers EU27-wide by 2030 (about 1 public charger per 35 electric trucks, 1 destination charger per 21) — a cumulative €1.9 billion investment over the decade (~€190m/year, just 0.2% of the EU's annual €100bn transport-infrastructure investment).
- Combining this long-haul analysis with T&E's earlier urban/regional trucking study (Vol. 1): 236,000–617,000 battery-electric trucks on EU27 roads by 2030, needing up to 31,400 destination and 17,400 public chargers, €23 billion cumulative investment, and avoiding up to 59 Mt CO2 (a 32% cut vs. current heavy-duty truck emissions).
- Heavy-duty trucks made up less than 2% of vehicles on EU roads but 22% of road-transport CO2 emissions in 2019, growing 9% between 2014–2019.

- This T&E report (published 2021, part of the "Unlocking Electric Trucking in the EU" series' long-haul volume) uses a novel EU freight-flow model to quantify the charging infrastructure needed to electrify long-haul (>400km) trucking, arguing this segment — previously seen as battery-range-constrained — is now on track to be dominated by battery-electric trucks (with 500km-range models arriving by mid-decade) rather than hydrogen fuel cells, which lag behind by several years and are expected to find niche roles at ports/industrial clusters instead.
- Core recommendation: prioritise charging-infrastructure rollout along the TEN-T core network's ~25,000km of main corridors, since a small number of key routes (e.g. Hamburg–Berlin) capture a disproportionate share of long-haul freight activity.
- Specific infrastructure targets proposed for the revised Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Directive (recommended to become a "Zero Emission Infrastructure Regulation," dropping natural gas from scope): at least one public charging station (4+ points, 700–800kW) every 100km of TEN-T Core network by 2025, tightening to every 50km by 2030; binding urban-node and logistics-hub charger minimums; and doubling the number of TEN-T urban nodes via the TEN-T Regulation revision.
- T&E frames hydrogen trucks as a niche complement rather than the primary long-haul pathway, arguing battery cost declines (driven by passenger-car-market scale effects) will outpace fuel-cell/hydrogen-storage cost reductions through the 2020s, making BETs the more cost-competitive choice for most operators.
- See [Decarbonising long-haul trucking in Germany](die-dekarbonisierung-des-lkw-fernverkehrs-in-deutschland.md) for T&E Germany's parallel cost-comparison finding that battery-electric (including overhead-catenary) trucks are the cheapest zero-emission long-haul option, and [LNG trucks: a dead-end bridge](lng-trucks-a-dead-end-bridge.md) for T&E's critique of gas-based alternatives not covered in this charging-infrastructure analysis.
