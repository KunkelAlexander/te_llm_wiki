# "Pigs can fly after all": use of animal fats to power cars and planes increasingly unsustainable

*Original title (German): "'Schweine können doch fliegen': Einsatz von Tierfetten zum Betrieb von Autos und Flugzeugen immer unhaltbarer"*

*Press release · Germany · 2023-05-31*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/te-deutschland/articles/schweine-konnen-doch-fliegen-einsatz-von-tierfetten-zum-betrieb-von-autos-und-flugzeugen-immer-unhaltbarer)

> Biofuels made from waste products such as animal fats were long seen as a cure-all for emission-free transport in Europe. But a single flight from Frankfurt to New York alone requires 10,400 dead pigs. The growing use of animal fats in transport is raising increasing concerns, since their availability is limited and their labelling carries risks of fraud.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- A Frankfurt-New York flight fully fuelled by animal-fat-derived SAF would require an estimated 10,400 dead pigs' worth of fat (a longer route than the Paris-New York example used in T&E's English/French releases, hence the higher figure).
- Animal-fat use in biodiesel has doubled in the past decade and is 40x higher than in 2006; nearly half of all European animal fats already go into biodiesel despite extensive competing uses.
- Category 3 animal-fat biofuel use grew 160% since 2014 versus 36% for category 1&2 fats, alongside a roughly 2x gap between reported EU consumption and industry-reported supply of category 1&2 fats — signalling likely mislabelling/fraud.

- This is the German-office (T&E Germany) press release on the same May 2023 Cerulogy-authored study covered in [Pigs do fly: the rise of animal fats in European transport](pigs-do-fly-the-rise-of-animal-fats-in-european-transport.md) — see that page for the fuller summary, including the double-counting fraud mechanism and T&E's recommendation to exclude category 3 animal fats from eligible biofuel/SAF feedstocks.
- Quotes T&E Germany's Sebastian Bock warning that scaling up animal-fat biofuels for aviation will squeeze supply for other industries (e.g. pet food), pushing them toward less sustainable substitutes like palm oil.
