# Airlines call for an end to loopholes in carbon market and back European climate measures

*Press release · Brussels · 2022-02-04*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/airlines-call-for-an-end-to-loopholes-in-carbon-market-and-back-european-climate-measures) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Europe-needs-all-airlines-to-join-the-fight-against-climate-change-2022-3-1-1.pdf)

> Four airlines – easyJet, Ryanair, Jet2 and Wizz Air – together with clean mobility group T&E are calling for equal rules to apply to all flights departing from European airports, regardless of their destination, in order for European measures to effectively decarbonise the aviation industry by 2050.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Long-haul flights generate 60–75% of European aviation's emissions but are largely excluded from the EU ETS (which only covers intra-EEA flights), while short-haul/intra-EU carriers already have 80–90% of their emissions covered.
- Long-haul carriers purchase ETS credits for only around 19% of their (much larger) emissions; example given: Wizz Air's 2019 emissions were 3.74 MtCO2, of which 56% was ETS-covered.
- An EU-Commission-commissioned study found extending the ETS to cover all flights would have the greatest positive impact on EU27 employment and Gross Value Added (GVA) versus partial-scope alternatives.

- This February 2022 joint statement from T&E, easyJet, Ryanair, Wizz Air and Jet2 argues "Fit for 55" needs no new carbon-leakage carve-outs and should apply equally to all flights departing European airports regardless of destination.
- Central argument: current exemptions for long-haul (mostly non-EEA) flights already distort competition in favour of long-haul over short-haul flying, so weakening "Fit for 55" for long-haul routes on carbon-leakage grounds would increase, not fix, that distortion.
- The signatories also back the ReFuelEU SAF mandate for being the first EU rule to cover all departing flights, and cite the T&E "Would airlines benefit from evading EU climate measures?" study (see [Would airlines benefit from evading EU climate measures?](would-airlines-benefit-from-evading-eu-climate-measures.md)) to argue the industry's carbon-leakage claims are not backed by credible data.
- Frames full-scope carbon pricing as a social-fairness issue: a low-fare short-haul passenger currently pays into the ETS while a business-class long-haul passenger emitting far more does not.
