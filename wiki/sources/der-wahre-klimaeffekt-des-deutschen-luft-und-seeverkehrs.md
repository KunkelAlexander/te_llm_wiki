## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- German aviation and shipping together emitted 45.2 MtCO2 in 2019, but Germany's national Climate Protection Act (Klimaschutzgesetz) counts only 3.8 MtCO2 of it (8%) — just domestic flights and domestic shipping routes; international flights and voyages fall into an unregulated "grey zone."
- International (intra- and extra-EEA) aviation and shipping already made up 20% of Germany's total transport emissions in 2019, and — on a business-as-usual path — is projected to rise to 35.2–38.4% (46.2–50.3 MtCO2) of Germany's transport emissions by 2030, jeopardising the country's legally binding 2045 net-zero target.
- Extra-EEA flights alone account for 71% of German aviation emissions (22.9 Mt of 32.2 Mt); including intra-EEA flights, international flights make up 93% of German aviation emissions. Just 7% of flights (the longest-haul) generate 50% of Germany's aviation emissions.
- International shipping accounts for 87% of Germany's shipping emissions (11.4 of 13 Mt). Total 2019 German transport CO2 (road + national/international air/sea) was 205.2 MtCO2, of which international air and sea contributed 41.4 MtCO2 (20%).

- This March 2022 T&E Germany report (Silke Bölts) argues Germany's climate law and the EU's regulatory framework both systematically undercount aviation and shipping emissions by excluding most international routes, understating Germany's true transport climate impact by around 25%.
- Three main decarbonisation levers identified: green hydrogen-based fuels (RFNBOs) for aviation and shipping; efficiency improvements; and fair carbon pricing plus demand reduction for aviation.
- Key policy recommendations: bring international aviation/shipping emissions into Germany's national climate law reporting; push at EU level for extra-EEA flights to be included in the EU ETS and for free allowance phase-out to accelerate; price non-CO2 effects with an additional emissions factor; raise ReFuelEU's e-kerosene sub-target with a 10% direct-air-capture carbon-feedstock share by 2030; cap advanced biofuels given limited sustainable scalability; and apply EU ETS carbon pricing to shipping immediately rather than phasing in from 2026, tightening FuelEU Maritime's targets to reach full climate neutrality by 2050.
- Echoes T&E's broader LNG warnings (see [LNG trucks: a dead-end bridge](lng-trucks-a-dead-end-bridge.md), [How to drive the uptake of sustainable fuels in European shipping](how-to-drive-the-uptake-of-sustainable-fuels-in-european-shipping.md)): investment in LNG for shipping is called a dead end that prolongs fossil-fuel dependency and adds methane-slip emissions.
- Notes the UK as the sole positive precedent, having committed to fully include international aviation and shipping emissions in its national carbon budgets from 2033.
