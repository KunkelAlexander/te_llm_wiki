# Would airlines benefit from evading EU climate measures?

*Press release · Brussels · 2022-01-06*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/would-airlines-benefit-from-evading-eu-climate-measures) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/TandE_Assessment_of_carbon_leakage_Jan_2022.pdf)

> EU regulators have little to fear from airlines evading the bloc's climate measures, a new study by Transport & Environment (T&E) finds.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Only East and South East Asia were found to have meaningful carbon-leakage potential via a Dubai/Istanbul stop-over: 45 MtCO2 of emissions (15.4% of the study's 291 MtCO2 scope), covering 92,000 of ~3.7 million annual EU27+UK return flights (2.5%).
- No direct flight has a financial incentive to evade: a direct East Asia return flight costs €212,000 vs. €257,000 (Dubai stop-over) or €234,000 (Istanbul); a direct South East Asia return costs €250,000 vs. €252,000/€251,000 with a stop-over — and passengers' value-of-travel-time makes stop-overs a further €145–€159 more costly per ticket.
- If every eligible flight nonetheless evaded, total EU+UK return-flight aviation emissions would rise by only 2.0–3.8% (5.7–10.8 MtCO2); intra-EU/UK flights (24% of emissions) and Middle East, South/Central Asia, Africa, South America and Oceania routes (17% combined) were judged to have essentially no leakage risk given proximity or lack of a viable hub.
- Key modelling assumptions for 2030: ETS price €100/tCO2, fossil kerosene €898/t, SAF (5% blend, 4.3% biofuel + 0.7% e-kerosene) €1,640/t (advanced biofuel) or €2,540/t (e-kerosene), fuel tax €309/t, value of travel time €27.47/hour; evasion only pays off if total journey time increases by less than 17% (roughly, only direct flights under ~11h50 are theoretically at risk).

- This is T&E's January 2022 study (Thomas Earl, Jo Dardenne) modelling whether EU airlines would reroute long-haul flights through non-EU hubs (Dubai, Istanbul, or London for North America) to dodge the EU ETS extension and SAF mandate proposed under "Fit for 55."
- Conclusion: the aviation industry's carbon-leakage claims are "unfounded" for this leakage type (stop-over evasion) — extra fuel, crew, aircraft and passenger time costs consistently outweigh any saving from avoiding EU carbon costs, so there is no economic case against extending EU climate measures to all departing flights.
- The Annex separately tests a scenario with a kerosene tax (Energy Taxation Directive) applied only to intra-EU flights; results are broadly unchanged for East Asia but show narrower cost gaps for South East Asia routes.
- The report notes today over 80% of some airlines' emissions are outside the ETS's scope (since it only covers intra-EEA flights), which is the underlying rationale for extending measures to all departing flights.
