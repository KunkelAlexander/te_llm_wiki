# Private jets: can the super-rich supercharge zero-emission aviation?

*Report · Brussels · 2021-05-27*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/private-jets-can-the-super-rich-supercharge-zero-emission-aviation) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202209_private_jets_FINAL_with_addendum_2024-05-07-140647_xczq.pdf)

> Aviation's climate impact is disproportionate and growing fast. But it is caused by a very small group of people. Just 1% of people cause 50% of global aviation emissions. This report exposes the outsized role played by the super rich hopping on private jets for super short distances.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- European private jet CO2 emissions rose 31% between 2005–2019 (vs. 25% for commercial aviation) and, unlike commercial aviation, fully recovered to 2019 traffic levels by August 2020 despite COVID-19.
- Just 1% of people cause 50% of global aviation emissions (all flight types); a private jet can emit 2 tonnes of CO2 in one hour, versus the average EU resident's 8.2 tCO2eq for an entire year.
- Private jets are on average 10x more carbon-intensive per passenger than commercial flights (range: 5–14x depending on aircraft model) and 50x more polluting than trains; the average private jet owner has a net worth of €1.3 billion.
- France and the UK dominate the European market: private jets departing each country emit more CO2 than 20 other European countries combined (36% of European private-flight emissions between them); 1 in 10 flights departing France in 2019 were private jets, half travelling under 500km.
- Private jets are twice as likely as commercial flights to be used for trips under 500km within Europe (nearly 50% of intra-EU private flights vs. ~25% for commercial); 72% of private flights have a direct commercial-flight alternative available.
- A jet fuel tax applied proportionately to flight distance is estimated to raise €325 million/year if applied to all flights departing the EU+UK.
- A single private jet engine (Dassault Falcon 900EX) was found to emit non-volatile particulate matter at 72x the per-passenger rate of a Boeing 737.
- If the top 50 European private-jet airports were supplied with SAF, 50% of the sector's European emissions could be covered, requiring 510kt of e-kerosene (under 1% of total 2019 EU aviation kerosene use).

- This T&E study (published 2021, funded partly by Benjamin Firmenich, using European Business Aviation Association traffic data) quantifies private jets' disproportionate climate impact across Europe and proposes a path for the sector to help fund and pioneer zero-emission aviation technology.
- Central argument: private jets are the most carbon-intensive form of transport per passenger, disproportionately used for short leisure hops (not the "time-saving business travel" the industry claims) rather than for genuine connectivity gaps, but the sector's wealth and small aircraft size make it well-suited to be an early adopter of hydrogen/electric aircraft.
- T&E's recommendations: from 2030, permit only green-hydrogen or electric-powered private jets for flights under 1,000km within Europe (with large operators required to sign e-kerosene power purchase agreements); until then, impose a ticket/fuel tax scaled by distance and aircraft weight (at least €3,000/flight, similar to Switzerland's rates); and encourage substantial voluntary reduction in private jet use in the interim.
- T&E notes private jets are currently exempt from the EU ETS and untaxed on kerosene in most countries (Switzerland being an exception), and highlights that most short private-jet routes have viable high-speed-rail alternatives.
