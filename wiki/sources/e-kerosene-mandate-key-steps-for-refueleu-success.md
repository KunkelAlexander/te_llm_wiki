# E-kerosene mandate: key steps for ReFuelEU success

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-05-11*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/e-kerosene-mandate-key-steps-for-refueleu-success) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/ReFuelEU-technical-briefingMay2021-1.pdf)

> Europe's forthcoming ReFuelEU initiative, which aims to bring about an uptake of sustainable advanced fuels (SAFs) in the aviation sector, has the potential to finally start decarbonising the emissions of a carbon intensive industry.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- T&E's recommended ReFuelEU target: a 2.9% GHG-intensity reduction by 2030 (equivalent to ~5% by volume: minimum 1% volumetric e-kerosene sub-target plus ~4% advanced sustainable biofuels), per analysis commissioned from consultancy Cerulogy.
- A 1% e-kerosene sub-target in 2030 requires 550 Ktoe (against expected 2030 aviation fuel demand of 54 Mtoe); an aggressive global deployment scenario (40 plants built, two-thirds of supply to the EU) could raise this to 730 Ktoe (~1.3%).
- Cerulogy estimates 3.2 Mtoe of advanced biofuels could be used sustainably (excluding HEFA/used-cooking-oil-based fuels, which Cerulogy argues would just divert existing road-sector HEFA rather than create new supply); T&E's own more conservative estimate is 2.8% advanced biofuels for aviation in 2030 (1.3 Mtoe), with 4.5 Mtoe still used in road transport.
- A T&E-commissioned decarbonisation-pathway study finds aviation would need 535 TWh of renewable electricity by 2050 (525 TWh e-kerosene, 10 TWh battery-electric aircraft) — 22% of all 2050 transport renewable-electricity demand, more than the ~500 TWh needed for all EU battery-electric passenger cars; aviation's renewable-electricity demand would grow roughly 38-fold between 2030 (14 TWh) and 2050 (526 TWh). Total EU27 renewable potential (20,430 TWh) far exceeds total 2050 transport energy demand (2,400 TWh).
- Recommended Direct Air Capture (DAC) minimum share: at least 30% from the start for publicly-supported e-kerosene projects, rising over time; under an optimistic 2030 scenario, 100% DAC would require 4.8 Mt of CO2 and raise costs by 6% (optimistic DAC price €105/t CO2) to 57% (conservative €425/t CO2) versus a €70/t CO2 point-source cost baseline; a 50% DAC share would need 2.4 Mt CO2 for a 3–29% cost increase.
- T&E-commissioned e-kerosene cost estimate: production costs of ~3,000 €/tonne in the near term, informing a recommended non-compliance penalty of €70/gigajoule (matching Germany's own proposed penalty level).
- Scope illustration: under an EU ETS-scope mandate, Ryanair would face a mandate on 83% of its fuel burn vs. 19% for KLM and 17% for Air France; under an intra-EU27+UK-only scope, Ryanair would have just 3% of fuel burn exempt vs. 71% for KLM — illustrating why T&E insists the mandate cover all departing flights.
- A 1% e-kerosene mandate at large airports (>1m passengers) is estimated to raise fuel prices by about 2% (assuming 2.2 €/litre e-fuel in 2030), materially less than existing within-category price fluctuation.

- This April 2021 T&E technical briefing (partly based on Cerulogy analysis) sets detailed design recommendations for the EU's incoming ReFuelEU Aviation SAF mandate, building on T&E's earlier seven-country mandate comparison.
- Core recommendations: exclude all crop-based biofuels; set the overall target as a GHG-intensity reduction (2.9% by 2030) with a ring-fenced volumetric e-kerosene sub-target (1%, revisable to 1.3% mid-decade); require green (additional-renewable-electricity) hydrogen and a rising minimum DAC-CO2 share; apply the mandate to all fuel sales at EU airports (not just intra-EU) to avoid distorting competition between long-haul and short-haul carriers; set a dissuasive non-compliance penalty (€70/GJ); and allow fuel suppliers to count investment in zero-emission aircraft (e.g. green hydrogen supply, joint ventures) toward compliance, provided the e-kerosene sub-target is still met.
- T&E argues the mandate has a solid legal basis under customary international law and is compatible with the Chicago Convention and "Open Skies" bilateral agreements, since it targets fuel suppliers (not airlines) and doesn't constitute an explicit fuel tax.
- See [Making aviation fuel mandates sustainable](making-aviation-fuel-mandates-sustainable.md) for the underlying seven-country comparison this briefing builds on, and [How EU legislation can drive an uptake of sustainable advanced fuels in aviation](how-eu-legislation-can-drive-an-uptake-of-sustainable-advanced-fuels-in-aviation.md) for T&E's earlier, broader ReFuelEU design recommendations.
