# How to make Europe's green fuels law truly green

*Briefing · Brussels · 2021-05-27*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-to-make-europes-green-fuels-law-truly-green) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Final-TE-RED-III-position-27-05-2021.pdf)

> The Renewable Energy Directive (RED) is about to be reviewed by the European Commission, as part of the 'Fit for 55' package planned for release in July. Until now, the RED has been mostly a tool used to incentivise biofuels blending, which created a heavy reliance on crop biofuels with negative environmental, social and climate impacts.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- T&E recommends the RED's 2030 renewable-transport target be set at ~16%, not the 24% floated in the Commission's Climate Target Plan (same modelling as [Advanced renewable fuels in EU transport](advanced-renewable-fuels-in-eu-transport.md)).
- Under RED II, the binding advanced-fuels sub-target is 7% (of the overall 14% target); the EEA reports the average GHG intensity of transport fuels fell only 2.1% between 2010–2018 including ILUC impacts (3.7% excluding ILUC) — evidence, per T&E, that crop-biofuel reliance has barely moved the needle on real emissions.
- T&E estimates a renewable-electricity credit-trading mechanism could generate about €5.9 billion/year in EU27 revenue for fuel suppliers purchasing electricity credits, which could help fund EV charging infrastructure.
- Recommended GHG-savings threshold: at least 70% for all fuels (rising to 80% if crop biofuels aren't fully excluded from the 2030 target, to compensate for unaccounted ILUC emissions).
- Direct Air Capture minimum share requirement for publicly-supported e-fuel projects: at least 30% from the start, rising to 100% over time (same figure as in [E-kerosene mandate: key steps for ReFuelEU success](e-kerosene-mandate-key-steps-for-refueleu-success.md)).
- Illustrative energy-demand comparison: if all trucks over 16 tonnes were battery-electric by 2050, renewable-electricity demand would be 347 TWh; running half on hydrogen fuel cells instead would raise demand to 506 TWh (a 37% increase) — used to argue hydrogen should be reserved for genuinely hard-to-electrify segments.
- Renewable-fuel share of new bioethanol/biodiesel content: advanced biofuels (Annex IX part A) stood at just 0.9% of transport energy in 2019 against a 3.5% target; UCO/animal-fat-based "part B" biofuels stood at 2% (with over half of EU UCO imported).
- A T&E-commissioned Cerulogy study found EU soy biodiesel use could grow 2–4x by 2030 versus current levels, causing deforestation over an area larger than London — used to argue for reclassifying soy as a "high ILUC risk" feedstock alongside palm oil.

- This May 2021 T&E position paper sets out its full recommendations for the European Commission's Renewable Energy Directive (RED) revision, part of the "Fit for 55" package, arguing the RED must shift from a biofuels-blending tool to a framework fit for renewable electricity, hydrogen and e-fuels.
- Core recommendations: exclude crop-based biofuels from RED targets by 2030 (with high-ILUC-risk biofuels — palm and soy — phased out by end-2021), set the renewable-transport target around 16% (not 24%), establish an EU-wide tradeable renewable-electricity credit mechanism (already implemented in the Netherlands, Germany and starting in France) as an alternative to biofuel blending mandates, and require robust additionality/DAC safeguards for hydrogen and e-fuels.
- T&E argues RFNBOs (hydrogen, e-fuels) should be targeted specifically at aviation and shipping — where direct electrification isn't viable — rather than road transport, and opposes any role for "blue"/fossil hydrogen (steam methane reforming + CCS) in the RED framework, arguing green hydrogen is on track to be cheaper by 2030 regardless.
- T&E opposes crediting alternative fuels within vehicle CO2 standards (a proposal it attributes to oil/gas industry lobbying), arguing this would create loopholes and undermine the EV transition that CO2 standards have driven.
- See [Which renewable transport fuels deliver substantial carbon reductions?](which-renewable-transport-fuels-deliver-substantial-carbon-reductions.md) and [How to ensure the sustainability of electrofuels](how-to-ensure-the-sustainability-of-electrofuels.md) for T&E's detailed technical positions on RFNBO additionality and carbon sourcing referenced here, and [Advanced renewable fuels in EU transport](advanced-renewable-fuels-in-eu-transport.md) for the underlying 16%-vs-24% target modelling.
