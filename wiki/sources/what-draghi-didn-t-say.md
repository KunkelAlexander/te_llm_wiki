# What Draghi didn't say

> How to do industrial strategy when your own industry is sabotaging it?

<!-- ENRICHED SUMMARY: hand-written, preserved across regenerations by 1_build_wiki_subset.py -->
## Summary

*(No quantitative findings — this is an opinion piece by T&E's Executive Director William Todts responding to Mario Draghi's 9 September 2024 EU competitiveness report, not a data-driven analysis.)*

- Argues Draghi's report correctly diagnoses that the EU needs an industrial strategy to complement the Green Deal, but fails to address the core problem: incumbent industries (carmakers, oil and gas majors, shipping companies) publicly say they want to be part of the clean-tech "new" economy while lobbying to delay or kill the very rules (2035 car CO2 standards, China EV tariffs, EV subsidies) needed to get there.
- Cites shipping (35% of the world's fleet flies a European flag) and oil/gas majors' reluctance to invest in e-kerosene/e-ammonia as further examples of incumbents choosing short-term fixes (LNG, biofuels) over the clean fuels transition.
- Criticises Draghi's proposed "new governance" framework — moving major EU decisions to prime-minister-level European Council deals, cutting out Parliament and civil society — as risking corporate capture, arguing dieselgate and industry's historical resistance to electrification show "incumbents don't always know what is best for them."
- Recommends the European Commission hold the line on existing climate rules (car CO2 standards, fuel mandates) rather than cave to incumbent lobbying, while adopting Draghi's and Letta's other ideas: a genuine single market, more assertive EU trade policy, and a large-scale clean-tech investment programme comparable to the US Inflation Reduction Act or China's "Made in China 2025."
