# Building back better: Ukraine's transport infrastructure

*Report · Brussels · 2024-12-04*

> Lessons from EU funding in Central and Eastern European countries

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Since 2007, the EU disbursed €418 billion in cohesion funds to 10 Central/Eastern European (CEE) accession countries, of which €104 billion (25%) went to transport — a share that fell from 31% (2007-2013 period) to 20% (later period).
- Roads received twice as much transport funding as rail: 29,605 km of road were built/reconstructed with EU co-financing (€57.3 billion, ~€1.94 million/km) versus only 2,622 km of rail (€24.7 billion, ~€9.4 million/km) — despite rail construction costing roughly 3x more per km EU-wide (~€30 million/km benchmark).
- The road-building programme coincided with a 90% rise in motorisation, 68% rise in passenger car traffic, and 88% rise in road emissions since 2000; productivity losses from congestion averaged over €600/person in major CEE cities.
- Nearly two-thirds of decided rail projects were never built, versus about 40% of road projects.
- Urban/public transport got only 14% of cohesion transport funds despite 64% of the 10 countries' population living in urban areas; Hungary spent over 25% of its cohesion budget on a "controversial" Budapest metro line.
- Road freight volumes in CEE more than doubled (2005-2022); after €895 million in EU-co-financed intermodal rail-freight projects in Poland, intermodal's share of rail freight rose more than fivefold (2.5% to ~14%) by 2022.
- €1.1 billion went to airport infrastructure, with a "poor record of value for money" — some new/expanded terminals were underused and later needed state aid to operate (e.g. Rzeszów, Łódź in Poland).
- Ukraine's estimated transport-infrastructure war damage reached ~$34 billion by 2023, with $73.7 billion in recovery needs over the next decade; rebuilding this infrastructure could itself emit over 14 MtCO2e (roughly the Netherlands' annual car-fleet emissions) if done without best-practice materials/planning.
- Ukraine's transport emissions fell 70% since 1990 to 35 MtCO2e in 2021 (~13% of total emissions, over 70% of it from road); if Ukraine's car fleet grew as much as Poland's did (2005-2020, +110%), it could add over 15 million cars and push annual road emissions above 42 MtCO2 — comparable to Germany's 2020 truck emissions.

- T&E's Ukraine-office report examines EU cohesion-fund transport spending across 10 CEE accession states (2007-present) as a cautionary case study for how the EU's Ukraine Facility (€50bn, 2024-2027) should be spent, given Ukraine's own EU-accession trajectory.
- Recommends Ukraine prioritise projects via a sustainability-weighted framework, build robust cost-benefit/data analysis capacity before committing funds, and adopt the "polluter pays principle" (heavy-vehicle tolling, EU-aligned vehicle CO2 standards) rather than repeating the CEE road-heavy pattern.
