# How EU legislation can drive an uptake of sustainable advanced fuels in aviation

*Briefing · Brussels · 2020-07-16*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-eu-legislation-can-drive-uptake-sustainable-advanced-fuels-aviation) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/T_2024-04-29-112140_oker.pdf)

> The European Green Deal has identified sustainable alternative fuels as a key means of reducing aviation's climate impact, with the European Commission launching a "ReFuelEU" initiative as a means to bringing about an uptake of such fuels in this sector.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- European aviation emissions grew 26% between 2013 and 2018, and the sector represented 4.2% of European emissions at time of writing.
- The EU's 2011 Advanced Biofuels Flightpath targeted 2 million tonnes of sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) uptake by 2020; actual expected uptake was only around 0.05 million tonnes — roughly 2.5% of the target.
- EASA estimated SAF from used cooking oil could cost €900–1,015/tonne vs. ~€600/tonne for fossil kerosene.
- Only one of six certified SAF conversion routes (HEFA — hydroprocessing of oils/fats) is at commercial stage.
- Norway introduced a 0.5% SAF mandate from January 2020 (advanced fuels only, per RED II Annex IX), with an intention (not yet legislated) to raise this to 30% by 2030; the Netherlands was considering a 2030 target of 14%.
- T&E's recommended e-fuels (efuels/synthetic kerosene) mandate: 1–2% of aviation fuel demand by 2030, which the paper calculates would require building 9–18 new 100MW electrolysers, 13–26 TWh of additional renewable electricity generation (two to four times Germany's 2019 installed offshore wind capacity), and about €9.4bn of investment per percentage point.
- That 1–2% e-fuels mandate would raise airline fuel costs by an estimated 4.5–9%, and would cut fossil fuel demand by roughly 1,576–3,126 ktoe over the period modelled (assuming e-fuel production starts at ~3 ktoe in 2022 and rises to 574 ktoe by 2030).
- Advanced (waste/residue) biofuels are estimated to be able to meet at most ~11.4% of EU aviation fuel demand by 2050, and less by 2030 due to competing road-transport demand.
- Abolishing free ETS allowances for aviation was estimated to generate roughly €1bn/year in revenue, proposed to fund Contracts for Difference (CfDs) for e-fuels.

- This July 2020 T&E briefing sets out recommendations for the EU's incoming "ReFuelEU Aviation" legislative initiative (flagged in the European Green Deal), arguing it must avoid repeating the road-transport sector's mistakes with crop-based biofuels.
- Core recommendation: prioritise e-fuels (synthetic/electro-kerosene) over advanced biofuels, because advanced/waste-based biofuel feedstock availability is fundamentally limited and will increasingly compete with road transport demand, whereas e-fuels can in principle be scaled with additional renewable electricity.
- T&E recommends excluding crop-based biofuels entirely from ReFuelEU eligibility (citing indirect land use change harms already established in the road-fuels debate) and excluding fossil-derived "Recycled Carbon Fuels."
- Recommended safeguards: for advanced biofuels, tighten RED II's Annex IX feedstock list to exclude problematic materials (e.g. tall oil, palm oil derivatives); for e-fuels, require the renewable electricity used to be "additional" (not diverted from existing uses) and require the CO2 feedstock to come from Direct Air Capture rather than fossil point sources.
- Recommended instrument design: an EU-level regulation (not a directive) applied directly to the roughly ten fuel-supplier companies serving the sector, combining a GHG-intensity-based mandate (not a simple volume/energy mandate) with supply-side Contracts for Difference funded by revenue from abolishing free EU ETS aviation allowances.
- T&E also flags the possibility of e-fuel production and import from outside the EU (e.g. MENA region) as a way to scale supply, conditional on additionality safeguards and a robust certification system to verify imported e-fuels meet EU sustainability standards.
- T&E notes non-CO2 climate effects (contrails, particulates) as an additional potential benefit of switching to alternative fuels, an area it says is under-researched and should be factored into ReFuelEU's overall benefit accounting.
- See [Revision of the EU ETS for aviation](revision-of-the-eu-ets-for-aviation.md) for T&E's parallel recommendations on funding these fuels via ETS reform, and [EU strategy for Smart Sector Integration](eu-strategy-for-smart-sector-integration.md) for the broader electrofuels-for-hard-to-abate-sectors framing this briefing sits within.
