# Airline and green NGO urge MEPs to exclude palm oil by-products from European aviation

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- ICCT calculations cited: the life-cycle direct and indirect GHG emissions of Palm Fatty Acid Distillate (PFAD) are more than 2.5x worse than fossil jet fuel; ICCT projects enough PFAD-derived HEFA (hydroprocessed esters and fatty acids) could be produced by 2030 (2.38 Mt) to meet the entire 4% non-power-to-liquids share of the TRAN committee's proposed 6% 2030 SAF mandate (against an estimated 49.37 Mt of 2030 EU jet fuel demand, of which 4% is 1.97 Mt).
- Two-thirds of Brazil's 2020 maize production (77 million tonnes) was grown as an "intermediate crop" — illustrating how a loosely-defined "intermediate crop" SAF category could, in T&E's view, become a near-limitless backdoor for crop-based biofuels.

- This July 2022 joint letter from easyJet and T&E, sent to MEPs ahead of the 7 July 2022 ReFuelEU Aviation vote, urges Parliament to revert to the European Commission's original, stricter definition of "sustainable aviation fuels" (SAF) rather than the European Parliament's TRAN committee's widened version.
- Identifies three feedstock categories the widened definition would newly permit and argues each risks large displacement (indirect land-use-change) emissions because each already has established competing uses: Palm Fatty Acid Distillate (PFAD, used in soap-making/livestock feed, whose closest substitute is virgin palm oil); category 3 animal fats (used in pet food/animal feed, likewise substitutable mainly by palm oil); and "intermediate crops" (food/feed crops grown in an off-season slot, undefined and potentially unlimited as a SAF feedstock loophole).
- Notes the European Commission's original proposal, and the improvements added by the Parliament's ITRE and ENVI committees (which capped Annex IX Part B biofuels given their limited supply), were both undermined by the TRAN committee's later widening of the definition.
- Signatories: easyJet and Transport & Environment, explicitly framing this as an unusual airline-NGO alignment in defence of feedstock sustainability safeguards.
