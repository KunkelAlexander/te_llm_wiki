# Why direct air capture holds one of the keys to sustainable aviation

*Report · Brussels · 2021-07-06*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/why-direct-air-capture-holds-one-of-the-keys-to-sustainable-aviation) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Role-of-DAC-in-e-fuels-for-aviation-report-v7.0.docx-1.pdf)

> To produce e-kerosene, you need CO₂. Sourcing CO₂ from ambient air, a technique otherwise known as direct air capture (DAC), is better than capturing it from industrial sources; a technique which, while cheaper, has the unintended effect of encouraging industries to continue to rely on fossil fuels. This T&E-commissioned report from E4tech assesses whether, when and how DAC could be scaled up to meet the demands of an e-kerosene industry at the scale needed to decarbonise European aviation.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- T&E estimates e-kerosene demand for flights originating in Europe could grow to almost 40 Mt/year by 2050 (fully replacing fossil kerosene); supplying all of the CO2 needed for that e-kerosene (plus co-produced hydrocarbons) via DAC would require capturing 365 Mt/year of CO2.
- Two DAC technology types: high-temperature (liquid solvent, ~900°C regeneration heat) — largest plant today 365 tCO2/year (Carbon Engineering); low-temperature (solid sorbent, 80–100°C regeneration) — largest plant today 4,000 tCO2/year.
- Current DAC costs: €100–500/tonne CO2 captured, with developers projecting future costs as low as €25/tonne ultimately, typically in a €40–170/tonne range.
- High-temperature DAC plants are proposed at large scale (~1 Mt CO2/year each), built from components already used commercially in the chemicals industry.

- This June 2021 T&E-commissioned study (by consultancy E4tech) examines how, when, and at what scale Direct Air Capture (DAC) could be built out to supply the CO2 needed for e-kerosene to decarbonise European aviation, following T&E's 2018 "Roadmap to decarbonising European aviation."
- Central finding: DAC is essential in a net-zero energy system because point-source CO2 (from fossil/biomass power plants, cement, chemicals) will diminish as the wider economy decarbonises, and remaining point sources may not be co-located with the cheap renewable electricity e-fuel production needs.
- Siting considerations: DAC plants need reliable, abundant, low-cost renewable electricity above all; high-temperature DAC additionally needs access to natural gas/high-temperature waste heat and water, while low-temperature DAC is more flexible on siting.
- Policy recommendation: existing aviation fuels policy doesn't provide enough support to make DAC-sourced e-fuels cost-competitive with other SAFs, so ReFuelEU or member-state policy will need dedicated sub-targets and/or supply-side support (e.g. guaranteed demand) specifically for DAC-based e-kerosene, alongside clarifying EU rules on whether e-fuel CO2 must come solely from DAC (or DAC plus biogenic sources) to avoid double counting or fossil lock-in.
