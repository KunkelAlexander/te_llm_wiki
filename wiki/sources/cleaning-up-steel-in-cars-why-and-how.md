# Cleaning up steel in cars: why and how?

*Report · Brussels · 2024-07-10*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/cleaning-up-steel-in-cars-why-and-how) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/Green-steel-in-cars-briefing_July-2024.docx.pdf)

> An ambitious uptake of green steel in cars by 2030 is feasible and can deliver significant climate benefits with limited costs for both industry and consumers.

## Summary

*See the companion German-office press release, [Green steel can cut the climate impact of car production for just €57 per car](gr-ner-stahl-kann-die-klimaauswirkungen-der-autoproduktion-f-r-nur-57-euro-pro-a.md), for a shorter announcement version of this same T&E/Ricardo analysis.*

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Vehicle production ("embedded") emissions are projected to reach ~60% of an electric car's total lifecycle emissions by 2030, with steel accounting for 16–27% of that; steel and aluminium plus batteries make up ~70–75% of total EV embedded emissions.
- The automotive sector consumes 17% of EU steel (35.7 Mt in 2022) — the second-largest steel-consuming sector after construction (37% of demand) — making it well placed to become a lead market for green steel.
- Shifting to 40% green steel in new cars by 2030 would cut EU car-production CO2 emissions by 6.9 Mt/year (equivalent to 3.5 million fossil-fuel cars' annual emissions); 100% green steel by 2040 would cut emissions equivalent to taking 8.1 million ICE vehicles off the road.
- Cost impact: switching to green steel per T&E's proposed trajectory (40% in 2030, 75% in 2035, 100% in 2040) adds only €57 to a new BEV's price in 2030, €49 in 2035, and just €8 by 2040 (assuming lightweighting trends continue); without lightweighting, the 2030 cost rises to about €69 — still "less than the cost of fitting a new car floor mat."
- Projected green/lower-carbon steel production capacity in Europe reaches 172 Mt by 2030 (based on tracked announcements) — far exceeding projected automotive steel demand of ~21 Mt (with lightweighting) or 35.7 Mt (2022 consumption level, no lightweighting).
- The IEA's near-zero-emission steel threshold is 400 kg CO2e/tonne (0% scrap) sliding down to 50 kg CO2e/tonne (100% scrap), versus ~2,945 kg CO2e/t for conventional blast-furnace steel.
- The steel sector emits 7% of global CO2, 5% of EU emissions, and ~30% of Germany's industrial emissions; 75% of global steel (57% of EU steel) is still made via coal-fired blast furnaces.
- Automotive green-steel demand is currently outstripping other sectors — 22 of 48 (46%) global green-steel supply agreements in 2021 were automotive — but committed volumes (1.8 Mt by 2030) represent just 9% of projected 2030 automotive steel demand.
- Steel content of an average BEV is projected to fall from 35% of vehicle weight (2020) to 17% (2050), i.e. from over 700kg to under 240kg, due to lightweighting.
- Case study: Germany produced 35 Mt of steel in 2023 (Europe's largest producer), ~70% still via the conventional BF-BOF route; government/EU support for its green-steel transition totals almost €7 billion, but the first hydrogen-DRI plants won't be operational until 2026 and some (e.g. ArcelorMittal's Bremen plant) aren't required to use 100% green hydrogen until 2040.

- T&E (with a commissioned Ricardo study) argues the automotive sector should become Europe's lead market for green steel given ample projected supply and minimal cost impact, and recommends: an EU-wide IEA-aligned definition of green steel; mandatory minimum green-steel shares for carmakers (40% by 2030, 75% by 2035, 100% by 2040) via the revised End-of-Life Vehicles (ELV) Regulation; and quality standards to unlock more automotive-grade recycled steel scrap.
