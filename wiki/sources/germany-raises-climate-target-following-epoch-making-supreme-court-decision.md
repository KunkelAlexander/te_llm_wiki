# Germany raises climate target following "epoch-making" supreme court decision

*News · Brussels · 2021-05-28*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/germany-raises-climate-target-following-epoch-making-supreme-court-decision)

> "Epoch-making", "great" and "significant," tweeted Germany's economy minister Peter Altmaier, after the country's supreme court declared his own government's climate protection measures insufficient, forcing it to rewrite its climate targets. With an election on the horizon, Altmaier's CDU, alongside its coalition partner the SPD, subsequently raised the target above and beyond the court's suggestion.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Germany's 2019 climate law set a 2030 target of -55% GHG emissions vs. 1990, with net-zero (later net-neutral) by 2050; following the constitutional court ruling, the coalition raised the 2030 target to -65% and brought the net-carbon-neutral date forward to 2045.
- German transport must find an additional 10 million tonnes of emissions savings beyond the previous target; government-commissioned research found even the -55% target left transport on track for less than half the necessary 2030 reduction.
- A T&E-commissioned analysis found that making all new German company cars electric by 2030 would cut car emissions by a third, saving 27 million tonnes of CO2.
- Registration tax on high-emitting company cars: over €9,000 in France vs. just €1,500 in Germany; average CO2 emissions of German petrol/diesel company cars are 17% higher than in France as a result.
- Germany's emissions were 40% below 1990 levels at time of writing, meaning a further 25% cut was needed within nine years to hit the new 2030 target.

- This 2021 T&E news piece covers the German Federal Constitutional Court's ruling that the government's existing climate law left an "impossible task" for post-2030 generations, forcing Chancellor Merkel's coalition to raise Germany's 2030 emissions-reduction target and bring forward its net-neutrality date.
- T&E's Stef Cornelis calls the ruling highly significant, arguing Germany must now accelerate electric mobility for road transport and clean fuels (e-kerosene, hydrogen/ammonia) for aviation and shipping to meet the tightened targets, with transport having the most ground to make up.
- T&E's recommended "easy win": mandating all new German company cars be electric by 2030, which it argues would both hit a substantial share of the needed transport savings and accelerate the used-EV market via fleet turnover — paired with reforming Germany's comparatively low company-car taxation, which currently favours large, polluting vehicles relative to France.
- T&E also calls on Germany to support tighter EU car/van CO2 standards and ambitious ReFuelEU Aviation/FuelEU Maritime mandates as complementary measures.
