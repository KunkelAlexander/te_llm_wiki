# Aviation's climate pledges contradicted by huge growth forecasts

*News · Brussels · 2021-11-26*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/aviations-climate-pledges-contradicted-by-huge-growth-forecasts)

> Can the aviation sector honour its pledge of carbon neutrality by 2050 while continuing to expand?

<!-- ENRICHED SUMMARY: hand-written, preserved across regenerations by 1_build_wiki_subset.py -->

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Airbus projects a need for ~39,000 new passenger and cargo aircraft over the next two decades.
- IATA committed in 2010 to 10% SAF use by 2017; twelve years later, sector emissions are still expected to triple by 2050 and EU SAF use is estimated at just 0.05% of jet fuel consumption.
- IATA forecasts air traffic growing 3%/year between 2019–2050.
- Zero-emission propulsion technology is estimated to need at least 10 years to scale up.

- This T&E news piece (Andrew Murphy) argues the aviation industry's net-zero-by-2050 pledges are contradicted by its own growth forecasts (Airbus's ~39,000-aircraft demand projection, IATA's 3%/year traffic growth assumption), since research shows aviation growth at that scale is incompatible with a mid-century net-zero goal.
- T&E's core argument: with SAF and zero-emission aircraft both many years from large-scale deployment, and past SAF pledges (IATA's 2010 commitment) having badly missed their targets, "flying less" is currently the most effective near-term lever to cut aviation emissions — but this is conspicuously absent from the industry's own net-zero vision.
- T&E frames reliance on ICAO's CORSIA offsetting scheme for the long term as "just another distraction" from near-term measures to actually clean up flying.
- T&E's Andrew Murphy concludes IATA's net-zero goal is unrealistic "not because SAFs are unrealistic... but because nowhere in their forecasting are they accepting that aviation shouldn't grow as much as is predicted."
