# How to incentivise renewable aviation fuels through the Renewable Energy Directive

*Briefing · Brussels · 2017-09-05*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/how-incentivise-renewable-aviation-fuels-through-renewable-energy-directive) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/2017_09_Aviation_REDII_final.pdf)

> While fuel and ticket taxes, an effective emissions trading system, aircraft standards and other policies (discussed in our decarbonisation of aviation briefing) are essential to lower aviation emissions, sustainable, advanced low-carbon fuels will likely have to contribute too. This paper outlines how supply of sustainable fuels could be encouraged through the Renewable Energy Directive (REDII) in the period 2020-30.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Aviation was responsible for an estimated **4.9%** of man-made global emissions at the time of writing (2017).
- Intra-EU aviation CO2 grew by almost **8%** in 2016 alone.
- The European Commission's REDII proposal set a general 2030 target of **6.8%** advanced/renewable fuels for road and rail fuel suppliers, of which at least **3.6%** had to be advanced biofuels (waste/residue-based) and a maximum of **1.7%** could be certain capped biofuels (used cooking oil, animal fats, molasses).
- T&E's own analysis found the Commission's 3.6% advanced-biofuels sub-target unsustainably high and recommended it be lowered to **2.3%**.
- Aviation fuels sold under the Commission's proposal benefited from a **1.2x multiplier** toward suppliers' targets — T&E calculates this is too weak to offset aviation biofuels being **2–5x more expensive** than fossil kerosene.
- Aviation energy demand is roughly **20%** the size of the road-and-rail sector's — relevant to how much extra demand for scarce sustainable feedstock a mandate on aviation fuel suppliers would create.

- Because kerosene is exempt from fuel excise duty and VAT, sustainable aviation fuel alternatives are structurally more expensive than fossil kerosene, and market forces alone won't drive uptake.
- T&E backs an alternative proposed by MEP Bas Eickhout: require fuel suppliers to split their renewable-fuel supply obligation proportionally between road and aviation fuel sales, without adding aviation into the denominator of the overall REDII target — this avoids creating additional demand for a feedstock pool T&E already judges to be oversubscribed.
- T&E rejects multiplier-based incentives (like the 1.2x aviation multiplier in the EC proposal) as both ineffective (too weak to close the cost gap at low values) and unfair (any effective multiplier shifts the compliance cost from airlines onto road fuel users/drivers, even though airlines are the ones exempt from fuel tax and VAT).
- T&E's position requires strict sustainability criteria for any eligible fuel: only genuine wastes/residues count as "advanced" biofuels (feedstock supply is inelastic, and land-based biofuels should get no policy support after 2030 absent proper indirect-land-use-change accounting); power-to-liquids (PtL) must be produced entirely from new renewable electricity and CO2 sourced from direct air capture or biogenic sources, not from fossil industry flue gas (to avoid greenwashing polluting industries).
- T&E argues the cost of decarbonising aviation fuel should fall on airlines/flyers, not be socialized onto road users, given aviation's existing tax exemptions.
