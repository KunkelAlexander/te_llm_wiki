# LNG trucks: a dead-end bridge

*Report · Brussels · 2021-09-27*

[Read the original publication](https://www.transportenvironment.org/articles/lng-trucks-a-dead-end-bridge) · [PDF](https://www.transportenvironment.org/uploads/files/202109_FVT_emission_performance_of_N3_HDVs_final.pdf)

> LNG trucks are not a viable solution to reduce emissions, neither in terms of greenhouse gases or air pollutants, on-road tests commissioned by T&E show.

## Summary

**Key quantitative findings:**
- Real-world testing (Graz University of Technology, commissioned by T&E) of an IVECO LNG truck vs. an equivalent IVECO diesel truck found only a 7.5% GHG reduction over a 100-year global warming potential (GWP) — far below IVECO's claimed 15% tank-to-wheel CO2 reduction — and, over a 20-year GWP timeframe, the LNG truck actually emitted 13.4% *more* GHGs than diesel (due to methane's higher near-term warming potency).
- The LNG truck emitted 37x more (unregulated, very small) particles than officially measured/regulated particle levels, contradicting IVECO's claimed 95% particulate-matter reduction; urban-operation NOx emissions were close to the legal limit despite an overall NOx reduction versus diesel.
- Sustainably-sourced biomethane, even with subsidies up to 6x the retail fossil-gas price, would meet only 4–28% of expected 2050 road-freight energy demand across the six largest European countries.

- This September 2021 T&E report presents real-world emissions testing (by Graz University of Technology) of a diesel and an LNG-powered IVECO long-haul truck, testing industry claims that gas trucks are a viable "bridge technology" for decarbonising long-haul road freight.
- Central finding: LNG trucks deliver far smaller GHG savings than claimed on a 100-year basis and are actually worse than diesel on a 20-year basis (due to methane's short-term warming potency), while also failing to reliably cut air pollution — contradicting industry claims on both fronts.
- T&E argues renewable methane (biomethane or e-methane) will never be scalable or affordable enough to make gas trucks credibly low-carbon even by 2050, given feedstock constraints, so continued investment in LNG infrastructure risks stranded assets and fossil lock-in.
- Policy recommendations: turn the Alternative Fuels Infrastructure Directive into a "Zero Emission Infrastructure Regulation" excluding gas refuelling from EU support; remove tax exemptions for fossil gas as a transport fuel under the Energy Taxation Directive; tighten EURO VII pollutant limits (including particle-number limits low enough to require particle filters on gas trucks, and new limits for previously unregulated pollutants like sub-23nm particles and N2O); and set truck CO2 standards requiring at least 50% zero-emission truck sales by 2030.
- See [Decarbonising long-haul trucking in Germany](die-dekarbonisierung-des-lkw-fernverkehrs-in-deutschland.md) and [Unlocking electric trucking in the EU: recharging along highways](unlocking-electric-trucking-in-the-eu-recharging-along-highways.md) for T&E's parallel case that battery-electric (not gas) trucks are the credible zero-emission pathway for long-haul freight.
